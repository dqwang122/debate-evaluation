Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

For instance, a study by the Fordham Institute found school-delivered financial literacy courses largely ineffectual, with personal habits, life experiences, and access to financial knowledge as an adult being far more influential . This suggests that simply mandating a course does not guarantee improved financial habits.

Second, requiring financial literacy creates curriculum overload and significant opportunity costs. Integrating financial literacy into already packed school curricula raises concerns about overload, with many educators worried about adding to existing workloads . This additional burden can lead to student stress and burnout, negatively impacting academic performance . As curriculum overload results in stress and reduces student engagement, it can lower achievement levels . Furthermore, the opportunity cost of mandating financial literacy means sacrificing other potentially valuable subjects . Schools may have to cut back on arts, humanities, or other STEM courses to accommodate this new requirement, potentially hindering students' overall educational development.

Third, alternative approaches to financial literacy are more effective and flexible. Instead of a mandatory course, integrating financial literacy into existing subjects like math and social studies, or offering optional workshops and online resources, allows for tailored learning experiences that cater to diverse student needs and interests . These methods are more adaptable and less resource-intensive. For example, stand-alone personal finance courses would be a more comprehensive option . Moreover, community-based initiatives and online tools can provide accessible and relevant financial education, reaching a broader audience and accommodating different learning styles .

In conclusion, while financial literacy is undoubtedly important, mandating it as a required course is not the most effective solution. It fails to guarantee long-term behavioral change, creates curriculum overload, and overlooks more flexible and effective alternatives. We urge you to consider the opportunity costs and support approaches that provide tailored and accessible financial education without burdening students and educators.
