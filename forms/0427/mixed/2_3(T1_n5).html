Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

(Optional) For - Transcript
In this debate, we shall argue that financial literacy, defined as the understanding of how money works encompassing budgeting, saving, investing, and responsible credit use, should be a required course in schools. This ensures all students gain crucial financial skills, regardless of background.

Our judging criteria is maximizing long-term student well-being. We believe the judge should prioritize the side that best promotes students' lasting financial health and success. This aligns with education's goal of preparing students for a secure future.

First, a required financial literacy course demonstrably improves financial decision-making. Students gain essential skills in budgeting, saving, and debt management. For instance, a 2023 report by Champlain College found that financial literacy lessons have an "overwhelmingly" positive impact on students’ future financial habits . Moreover, research indicates that students who receive financial education in high school exhibit better financial outcomes as adults, including less debt and an improved quality of life . This directly contributes to long-term student well-being by providing the tools necessary for responsible financial management, reducing the risk of debt accumulation and promoting financial stability.

Second, financial literacy enhances economic opportunity, particularly for students of color who often face unique financial challenges . By providing all students with a foundational understanding of financial principles, they are empowered to make informed decisions about education, career paths, and investments. Studies show that financial literacy equips individuals to budget, save, and invest wisely, securing their futures . It allows them to break free from cycles of poverty and build lasting intergenerational wealth. Ultimately, this contributes to long-term student well-being by equipping students with the financial knowledge necessary to take advantage of opportunities and improve their socioeconomic standing.

Third, integrating financial literacy reduces financial stress and improves mental health. Managing finances poorly causes significant stress and anxiety, negatively impacting students' well-being. Equipping students with effective financial management skills lowers stress and enhances mental well-being . Informed financial decisions lead to peace of mind, as mentioned by USC's career blog . By fostering a mindset that views budgeting and managing money as empowering rather than restrictive, financial literacy contributes significantly to students' overall happiness and success.

In conclusion, requiring financial literacy in schools is crucial. It equips students with vital skills for improved financial decisions, promotes economic opportunity, and reduces financial stress, all contributing to their long-term well-being. We urge the judge to support our position and invest in the future financial success of all students.

Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript
Good morning, everyone. We're here today to discuss whether financial literacy should be a required course in schools. While we acknowledge the importance of financial literacy, we firmly believe that mandating it is not the most effective or equitable approach.

Let's begin by defining "financial literacy." Our opponents likely define it narrowly, focusing on budgeting, saving, and investing. While these are important components, a truly comprehensive understanding of financial literacy extends far beyond. It encompasses understanding the complexities of the economy, how government policies impact personal finances, and how global events can ripple through individual financial situations. This broader definition is crucial because it highlights the limitations of a standardized course. Mandating a course that attempts to cover such a vast scope would be unwieldy and ultimately less effective.

Now, regarding the judging criteria, our opponents may argue that long-term student well-being should be the primary focus. Of course, we agree that it's important. However, education is about more than just preparing for a secure financial future. It's about fostering critical thinking, creativity, and a lifelong love of learning. We must consider all aspects of a student’s development, not just their potential financial success.

So, why are we against making financial literacy a required course? Our first argument is that it neglects the diversity of student needs and interests. A "one-size-fits-all" approach simply doesn't work. Not every student aspires to be a financial expert. Many students are passionate about the arts, humanities, or skilled trades. Forcing them into a standardized financial literacy course could stifle their passions and limit their opportunities to excel in other areas. It's akin to forcing every athlete to train for the marathon, even if they are naturally gifted sprinters. Instead, we should focus on personalized learning approaches that cater to individual student needs and aspirations.

Second, effective financial literacy is best learned through real-world experience. It's about managing a personal budget, participating in investment simulations, or receiving guidance from a financial mentor. A classroom setting simply cannot replicate these experiences. As highlighted in *a 2022 study published in Frontiers*, experiential learning methods and life-event-focused content significantly enhance the effectiveness of financial education. It's like trying to learn how to swim by reading a book; you need to get in the water.

Finally, focusing solely on financial literacy deflects attention from the larger issue of systemic economic inequalities. Many individuals struggle financially not because they lack knowledge, but because they face systemic barriers such as low wages, limited access to affordable healthcare, and discriminatory lending practices. According to *a study in the UIC John Marshall Law Review*, ordinary citizens have little to no independent influence on policy decisions due to the deep entrenchment of money in American politics. Therefore, even financially literate individuals from marginalized communities may struggle to overcome these systemic barriers. It's like putting a band-aid on a broken leg; we need to address the root causes of financial insecurity.

Instead of a mandatory course, we propose alternative approaches such as integrating financial literacy concepts into existing subjects like math and social studies, offering optional workshops and extracurricular activities, and promoting online resources and mentorship programs.

Therefore, we urge you to vote against the motion and instead advocate for policies that empower students through tailored education and address the root causes of economic inequality.

Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's opening statement contains three key flaws, specifically their overly broad definition of financial literacy, their misrepresentation of our stance on long-term student well-being, and their arguments regarding the practicality and impact of financial literacy education.

First, the opposition attempts to define financial literacy so broadly that it becomes impractical for a high school course. While understanding the complexities of the global economy is beneficial, the core skills of budgeting, saving, investing, and responsible credit use are fundamental . Studies show that focusing on these practical skills equips students with essential tools for making informed financial decisions . Insisting that a financial literacy course must cover everything from government policy to global events is like arguing that a basic math class must delve into advanced calculus. It's an unreasonable expectation that obscures the real value of providing a solid foundation in personal finance .

Second, our opponents suggest that prioritizing financial literacy somehow diminishes the importance of critical thinking, creativity, and a love of learning. However, financial literacy directly supports these very qualities. Making informed financial decisions requires critical thinking. Finding innovative solutions to financial challenges fosters creativity. And understanding how money works can spark curiosity about economics and business . By empowering students to manage their finances effectively, we are not limiting their horizons, but rather providing them with the means to pursue their passions with greater confidence and security .

Third, the opposition argues that a "one-size-fits-all" financial literacy course neglects the diversity of student needs. However, core subjects like math and English are also required, because they provide essential skills for everyone . Moreover, financial literacy equips all students with universally applicable knowledge, regardless of their career paths . It's a false dilemma to suggest that students must choose between financial literacy and other subjects . Furthermore, basic financial literacy teaches necessary skills for college, such as how loans work and how to avoid predatory loans.

Fourth, they claim that financial literacy is best learned through real-world experience, not in the classroom. While real-world experience is valuable, it needs a foundation. Students need to learn the basics of budgeting, saving, and investing before they can effectively apply those skills in real-world scenarios . A classroom setting can provide this essential foundation through simulations, case studies, and guest speakers, preparing students to navigate the complexities of personal finance with greater confidence . They cannot claim that only real world experience matters, as they learned the information they now use from prior instruction .

Finally, our opponents suggest that focusing on financial literacy deflects attention from systemic economic inequalities. However, financial literacy empowers individuals to navigate these inequalities more effectively. While financial literacy may not solve systemic problems, it equips individuals with the knowledge and skills to make the best possible choices within the existing system . Knowing how finances and laws work is important and helpful in the face of financial inequality, and not knowing simply puts those in need at an even greater disadvantage .

In conclusion, the negative side’s arguments are unfounded. Therefore, we continue to advocate that requiring financial literacy in schools is crucial for equipping students with the tools they need to thrive in an increasingly complex financial world.

Studies conducted in South Korea and Hong Kong, as reported in *Frontiers*, emphasize the impact of parental financial behaviors and experiences on shaping financial literacy, especially for adolescents from low-income families. Therefore, before we declare financial literacy the king of decision-making, let's acknowledge the full landscape of influences.

Fourth, our opponents suggest that the classroom provides a sufficient foundation for real-world financial experience. But that's like saying reading a cookbook makes you a chef. Those who learn by doing are far more likely to develop genuine financial competence.

Fifth, they contend that financial literacy helps navigate systemic inequalities. This is simply not the case. Focusing solely on individual financial skills while ignoring broader systemic issues is like giving someone swimming lessons and dropping them in the middle of the ocean. It's a fallacy of composition to assume that individual skills training addresses the root causes of low wages and limited access to healthcare. As highlighted by the *Center for American Progress*, shrinking the wealth gap requires large, targeted policy interventions. We need policy changes that address these inequalities directly, not just better budgeting skills.

In conclusion, while financial literacy has its place, it is not a panacea. We must not allow it to distract us from the more fundamental reforms needed to create a truly equitable society. Instead of mandating a one-size-fits-all financial literacy curriculum, let's focus on personalized learning approaches and systemic reforms that address the root causes of economic inequality. I urge you to consider the limitations of the proponent's arguments and instead prioritize a more holistic and effective approach to education and economic empowerment.
