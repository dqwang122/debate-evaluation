Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Let's begin by establishing a shared understanding. When we refer to "labor unions," specifically unions like the AFL-CIO, we're talking about organizations that represent workers, advocating for improved wages, better working conditions, and enhanced overall well-being. "Economic growth," in this context, signifies a sustained increase in a nation's production of goods and services, leading to higher standards of living for its citizens.

To fairly evaluate the benefits of labor unions, we propose a primary judging criterion: overall societal benefit. This encompasses not only the direct economic effects of unions but also their broader impact on workers' lives, the well-being of communities, and the equitable distribution of economic gains.

First, unions foster a more competitive marketplace by acting as a countervailing force to corporate dominance. Without unions, large corporations often dictate terms, potentially leading to monopolies that stifle innovation and harm consumers. Unions level the playing field, ensuring that businesses compete on merit, with better products, services, and working conditions. As the *U.S. Department of the Treasury* pointed out in their report on *Labor Unions and the U.S. Economy*, nonunionized firms in competition with unionized workplaces may choose to raise wages, change hiring practices, or improve their workplace environment to attract workers . This competition ultimately benefits consumers and drives economic growth.

Second, union-negotiated benefits, particularly healthcare and retirement plans, reduce the strain on public social safety nets. When workers have access to robust employer-sponsored benefits, they are less likely to rely on government assistance programs. It's like having a reliable private insurance policy that reduces the demand on public healthcare. According to *2020 data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics*, 91 percent of union workers had access to a retirement plan compared to only 65 percent of nonunion workers . These employer contributions go into trust funds run by trustees selected by the union and the employers. By reducing the reliance on public funds for social safety nets, resources can be redirected towards education, infrastructure, and other essential services that benefit everyone, further stimulating economic growth.

Third, unions make critical investments in skills training through apprenticeship programs, equipping workers with valuable expertise for high-demand jobs. These programs ensure that the workforce possesses the skills necessary to thrive in a rapidly evolving economy. *Forbes* reported that companies achieve the best returns on investments in new technology when they merge implementation with complementary changes in work processes, employee training, and worker input . Moreover, unions actively promote diversity and inclusion in these programs. For example, *a study* found that registered apprenticeship programs conducted in partnership with a union register more Black, Latino and women workers than their non-union equivalents . By investing in human capital and promoting inclusivity, unions directly contribute to long-term economic growth and shared prosperity.

While some may argue that unions can sometimes resist innovation or protect underperforming workers, the evidence clearly demonstrates that their positive contributions to competition, reduced public burden, and skills training far outweigh these concerns. Unions are not relics of the past; they are vital engines for a more equitable and prosperous future. Therefore, we firmly believe that labor unions are indeed beneficial to economic growth.

Third, I'll discuss the claim of inflationary pressure, highlighting flaws in their reasoning. Finally, I will reinforce our key claims that unions are beneficial to economic growth. Let's dive in. Our opponents argue that labor unions are not beneficial to economic growth. We strongly disagree. They focus on cost while ignoring the bigger picture. Unions *do* promote economic growth by increasing worker productivity, reducing income inequality, and promoting social stability. Unions advocate for better training and safer working conditions, leading to a more skilled and motivated workforce. This translates to higher productivity and innovation, benefiting the entire economy.

Second, let's address concerns about increased labor costs. The claim is that higher union wages reduce investment and hiring. However, this assumes that higher labor costs always lead to decreased profitability, which isn't always true. Unionized firms often see increased productivity and innovation, offsetting those costs. *WorkRise Network* found that unions can improve business outcomes by helping firms hold on to tenured workers who tend to be more productive . This reduces turnover costs and supports investments in training. Furthermore, higher wages translate to increased consumer spending, stimulating demand and fueling economic growth. The evidence they presented may not fully account for these factors.

Third, let's talk about inflation. Our opponents suggest that union wage increases lead to inflationary pressures. But that's not the complete story. Wages often rise in response to existing inflation, not the other way around. Unions may negotiate higher wages to compensate workers for the rising cost of living. Plus, increased productivity from unionized workplaces can help absorb those wage increases without necessarily raising prices. Their cited study doesn't fully account for these factors.

Now, let's reinforce our key claims. Firstly, unions are a countervailing force to corporate dominance. They create a more competitive market. According to the *Economic Policy Institute*, union contracts establish wages for unionized workers, and nonunion employers raise wages to stay competitive . The *Economic Policy Institute* further reports that, on average, union workers earn 10.2% more than nonunion workers with similar education, experience, and occupation. Secondly, union-negotiated benefits reduce the strain on public social safety nets. For example, the *NIHCR* reports that unionized workers tend to have more comprehensive health benefits and lower patient cost sharing compared to nonunionized workers . Finally, unions make critical investments in skills training through apprenticeship programs. *SMART Union* reports that unions update their training regularly to anticipate technological changes, ensuring quality and safety .

Unions provide a vital safety net. They reduce the strain on public resources and ensure workers have the skills needed for a changing economy. These are investments in our society. That's the real definition of economic growth: shared prosperity and a better future for all. So, while our opponents focus on narrow cost concerns, we see the broader benefits. Unions are good for workers, good for communities, and good for economic growth.

Thank you, chairperson. Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's rebuttal contains several flaws. We will address their defense of unions regarding the judging criteria, worker productivity, consumer spending, the risk of inflation, their effects on social safety nets, and the adaptability of job training.

First, the affirmative side asks us to judge this debate based on "overall societal benefit." However, this standard is far too broad and subjective . While societal benefits are certainly desirable, they are difficult to quantify and compare. Our criteria of net positive contribution to GDP growth provides a clear, measurable, and universally recognized indicator of economic well-being. Historically, GDP growth has strongly correlated with improved societal well-being, poverty reduction, and increased access to healthcare and education. If our economy isn't growing, then any societal benefit is unsustainable .

Second, our opponents claim that unions increase worker productivity through better training and working conditions, but this argument commits the fallacy of correlation versus causation. Do unions actually *cause* productivity increases, or do already productive industries simply attract unionization ? Evidence suggests that unions may, in fact, decrease productivity by increasing labor costs and imposing restrictive work rules. These rules can hinder innovation and efficiency .

Third, our opponents argue that higher union wages drive economic growth through increased consumer spending. However, these wage increases come directly from company profits, which could otherwise be reinvested in innovation, expansion, and job creation . Moreover, higher prices due to union wage demands will offset the benefit of increased wages and lead to reduced consumption, especially for non-union workers who do not receive the same benefit. Union wage demands don't grow the economy, they simply redistribute wealth .

Fourth, the wage increases secured by unions can indeed lead to inflationary pressures. When unions negotiate wage increases that exceed productivity gains, businesses are often forced to pass these costs onto consumers in the form of higher prices. This creates a wage-price spiral, where union wage demands lead to higher prices, which then lead to further wage demands . This hurts non-union workers and those on fixed incomes the most.

Fifth, we disagree with the claim that unions reduce the strain on public social safety nets. While union jobs, with their higher wages and benefits, may discourage workers from seeking government assistance, they also create a disincentive for innovation and flexibility in the labor market . This can potentially lead to higher unemployment in the long run, which ultimately *increases* reliance on social safety nets.

Sixth, while unions invest in skills training, these programs are often focused on specific trades and may not be as adaptable to changing economic conditions as private sector training programs . Additionally, access to these programs is typically limited to union members only, excluding a large portion of the workforce.

For these reasons, we maintain that labor unions are not beneficial to economic growth.
