Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
In this debate, we shall argue that dating expenses should be shared equally between partners to foster equitable relationships.

**Definitions:**
- **Dating Expenses:** All costs incurred during planned activities undertaken by two individuals with the intention of fostering a romantic relationship. This includes, but is not limited to, the cost of meals, entertainment, transportation, and gifts.
- **Shared Equally:** Dividing the total dating expenses such that each partner contributes 50% of the total cost, either through direct payment or an agreed-upon equivalent contribution.

Our judging criteria is promoting equitable relationships. The side that best demonstrates how their model for managing dating expenses fosters equity, mutual respect, and healthy communication within a romantic partnership should win the debate.

First, equal sharing of expenses fosters gender equality and challenges traditional gender roles. Traditional dating etiquette often dictated that men should bear the financial burden, reinforcing outdated gender stereotypes . However, a majority of Americans now agree with statements measuring beliefs in gender equality . Sharing costs equally promotes gender equality, ensuring both partners contribute and have equal autonomy. According to Mashable, splitting the bill promotes gender equality by ensuring both people on the date, regardless of gender, have equal autonomy . This shift reflects a move towards more egalitarian relationships where financial contributions are not dictated by gender. Therefore, by promoting gender equality, equal sharing of expenses lays the foundation for a more balanced and respectful partnership.

Second, equal sharing of expenses promotes open communication and reduces financial stress. A survey indicates that couples, on average, discuss finances around five months into the relationship . Regularly discussing shared expenses helps foster open, ongoing communication and provides a platform to address any issues or concerns before they become larger problems . When both partners are transparent about their finances and expectations, it reduces the potential for misunderstandings and resentment. As The Money Engineers point out, if one partner earns significantly more than the other and covers most of the shared expenses, it could potentially create an imbalance of power . Therefore, by encouraging open communication and preventing financial imbalances, equal sharing of expenses contributes to a healthier and more sustainable relationship.

Third, equal sharing of expenses creates a foundation for a more balanced and respectful partnership. When both partners contribute financially, it reduces the potential for power imbalances and fosters a sense of mutual respect. As Black Girl Nerds mentions, splitting expenses can allow financial independence, so if dynamics ever shift, one person isn’t solely reliant on their partner . This balance contributes to a healthier and more sustainable relationship in the long run. A relationship where finances are discussed openly and shared equitably fosters mutual respect and a stronger partnership . Therefore, by reducing power imbalances and fostering mutual respect, equal sharing of expenses creates a more balanced and respectful partnership.

In conclusion, dating expenses should be shared equally between partners because it fosters gender equality, promotes open communication, and creates a foundation for a more balanced and respectful partnership.

(Optional) Against - Transcript A
My opponents advocate for the equal sharing of dating expenses to foster equitable relationships. However, we believe that a strictly equal approach is not only flawed but can actually undermine the very equity it seeks to achieve. Their definitions, while seemingly reasonable, oversimplify the complexities of modern relationships. Their notion of 'shared equally' as a rigid 50/50 split neglects the nuances of individual circumstances. A more flexible approach is needed.

The crux of their argument rests on the assumption that equal financial contribution automatically translates to gender equality, open communication, and balanced partnerships. However, this is a false equivalency. While financial transparency is important, mandating an equal split doesn't guarantee these outcomes. Couples can communicate effectively and maintain mutual respect regardless of how expenses are divided. As demonstrated by *a 2024 study conducted by the American Association of Marriage and Family Therapy*, 56% of couples argued about money more than any other topic . This shows that simply splitting expenses equally does not eliminate financial stress or guarantee better communication.

In fact, a strictly equal approach can be detrimental. Consider this: An insistence on strictly equal splitting disregards the inherent variations in individual financial capacities. A student with limited income should not be expected to contribute the same amount as a working professional. This creates an unfair burden and can lead to resentment, a clear contradiction to their goal of fostering equity. As *Tu, who worked at J.P. Morgan and Buzzfeed before pursuing her own business*, argued in 2023, splitting expenses evenly can lead to resentment in relationships with significant income disparities because the lower-earning partner is often forced to live beyond their means or sacrifice their financial independence . We are promoting true equity and they are forcing a potentially detrimental system.

Furthermore, enforcing an equal split undermines the intrinsic value of non-monetary contributions within a partnership. One partner might contribute more in terms of emotional support, household chores, or career sacrifices. These contributions are valuable and should be considered, not ignored by a rigid financial arrangement. Are we really saying that hours spent cooking a meal is worth nothing? Their framework focuses solely on the financial aspect, disregarding other crucial contributions. As highlighted in *an article about overcoming financial inequalities*, financial contributions are just one of many ways an individual can bring value to a relationship, and understanding and recognizing non-financial contributions can help both partners .

Finally, the emphasis on equal spending can create a false sense of equality, masking underlying power imbalances in the relationship. If one partner is pressured to spend equally despite not being able to afford it, this can create a subtle form of control and dependence. We acknowledge that in certain situations and in certain relationship structures, one party contributes far less financially than the other, but that should not serve as an excuse to force a blanket rule to split 50/50. According to *Robert Körner and Astrid Schütz in 2021*, socioeconomic resources such as income, occupational status, and educational attainment can influence one’s partner, which is usually termed positional power .

Therefore, we argue that a more nuanced and flexible approach, one that considers individual circumstances and values all forms of contribution, is essential for fostering truly equitable and healthy relationships. We are not against fairness; we simply believe that equality does not always equate to equity.

In this debate, we shall argue against the notion that dating expenses should be shared equally between partners. We define **dating expenses** as costs incurred during courtship activities, including but not limited to meals, entertainment, transportation, and gifts. We define **equally shared** as dividing dating expenses in a strict 50/50 split, regardless of individual income or financial circumstances.

Our judging criteria is **fairness and relationship health.** A dating expense arrangement is preferable if it fosters a sense of fairness, promotes healthy communication, and minimizes the potential for resentment and financial strain within the relationship.

First, **equal splitting ignores income disparity, leading to financial strain and inequity.** A strict 50/50 split disproportionately burdens the lower-income partner, potentially creating financial hardship and resentment. It fails to account for differing financial realities. For instance, if one partner earns significantly less, say $30,000 annually, while the other earns $70,000, an equal split of a $100 dinner places a far greater burden on the lower-income individual. A CNBC report highlights that 37% of individuals feel their relationship is financially unequal . This feeling of inequity can erode the relationship over time. Instead of promoting equality, it exacerbates existing financial inequalities outside the relationship, importing them directly into the dating dynamic.

Second, **equal splitting can be impractical and logistically complex.** Tracking every expense and ensuring a precise 50/50 split can be cumbersome, leading to unnecessary stress and conflict. It prioritizes accounting over the enjoyment of the date. As one person described to Buzzfeed, the downside of splitting everything was "the illusion of him paying me when he was really just paying me back for his half...This sometimes led to being unsure how much he owed me for bills I already paid" . This constant tracking can transform a romantic outing into a bookkeeping exercise, hindering spontaneity and genuine connection. The focus shifts from enjoying each other's company to meticulously calculating who owes what, creating an environment ripe for disagreements and resentment.

Third, **alternative approaches to sharing expenses foster stronger relationships through open communication and consideration.** Flexible approaches, such as income-based contributions, alternating payments, or shared expense accounts, promote open communication about finances and demonstrate consideration for each partner's circumstances, leading to a more equitable and fulfilling dynamic. For example, couples could calculate each partner's income percentage and apply that to shared expenses. If one partner earns 60% of the combined income, they cover 60% of the expenses . Therapist Elisabeth LaMotte advocates for candid communication, advising couples to "Have an honest conversation about money values early in the relationship," as transparency reduces anxiety . This proactive approach fosters mutual respect and understanding, strengthening the relationship's foundation.

In conclusion, we oppose the motion that dating expenses should be shared equally. Equal splitting ignores income disparities, introduces logistical complexities, and undermines the potential for open communication. We advocate for flexible, considerate approaches that prioritize fairness, healthy communication, and the overall well-being of the relationship.
