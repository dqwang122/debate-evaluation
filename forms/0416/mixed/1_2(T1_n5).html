Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Thank you, chairperson. Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's rebuttals fall short on several critical points. We will address their claims regarding social-emotional development, critical thinking, and ethical concerns.

First, the opposition claims that AI can enhance teacher-student relationships and that we misrepresent their position by suggesting a complete replacement of human interaction. However, even with integrated AI, the *quality* of human interaction is diminished . Teachers transition from mentors to supervisors of AI, reducing meaningful connections. What’s more, districts may be tempted to reduce teaching staff to save costs. The result is a net negative impact on social-emotional development, as students miss out on vital opportunities to learn empathy and navigate complex social situations . Studies show that activities like role-playing, group discussions, and even simple "I-messages" are essential for developing these crucial skills .

Second, while the affirmative argues AI can personalize learning and provide instant feedback, they miss a crucial point. The *convenience* of readily available answers discourages students from developing independent critical thinking skills . Students may become less adept at independently analyzing information, forming logical arguments, and making well-reasoned decisions . Rather than fostering deep understanding, AI tutors can encourage a focus on memorization over comprehension, emphasizing formulas instead of conceptual understanding . This ultimately undermines the development of true problem-solving abilities.

Third, the opposition dismisses our ethical concerns regarding data privacy and bias, touting anonymization and audits. However, these measures are insufficient. The affirmative ignores a crucial point: AI collects students' information and threatens their privacy . Anonymization is never foolproof, and the sheer volume of data collected still creates a risk of re-identification . In fact, AI tutors increase the risks to data and create larger data sets that can be hacked. Further, the affirmative's argument that "two wrongs make a right"—suggesting AI bias is acceptable because human bias exists—is a logical fallacy. Algorithmic bias perpetuates existing inequities at scale . While inherent biases in teachers are often known, the bias exhibited by AI is much harder for students to identify, giving it more power to negatively influence outcomes .

For these reasons, we maintain that the widespread adoption of AI tutors is premature and detrimental to holistic student development.
