Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
We stand firmly in support of the resolution: Labor unions are beneficial to economic growth. Let’s begin by defining our terms to ensure clarity. "Labor unions" are organized associations of workers formed to protect and promote their common interests through collective bargaining. "Economic growth" signifies a sustained increase in a country's production of goods and services, typically measured by GDP growth, but also encompassing improvements in living standards and overall societal well-being.

Therefore, our judging criterion for this debate is overall societal well-being, measured by economic prosperity, social equity, and innovation, all of which are integral to true and sustainable economic growth.

Labor unions are not relics of the past; they are vital engines driving a more prosperous and equitable future.

First, consider how labor unions reduce income inequality. By bringing workers’ collective power to the bargaining table, unions are able to win better wages and benefits for working people. *A 2021 Economic Policy Institute Fact Sheet* shows that, on average, a worker covered by a union contract earns 10.2% more in wages than a peer with similar education, occupation, and experience in a nonunionized workplace in the same industry . Unions shrink the gap between the highest and lowest earners, leading to a more equitable distribution of wealth. The *Economic Policy Institute* also notes that there was less income inequality in the decades following World War II, when union membership was at its highest rate in 1945 . As union strength steadily declined after 1979, income inequality worsened, reaching its worst point since the Great Depression . This boosts overall economic health through increased consumer spending and reduced social instability. Unions promote social stability and increase the spending power of lower-income individuals, further driving economic growth.

Second, union-negotiated benefits packages, including healthcare and retirement plans, attract and retain skilled workers, creating a more stable and productive workforce. Unions may also reduce turnover by increasing job satisfaction. *WorkRise Network* research indicates that unionized workers are less likely to express intent to leave their jobs . This stability supports investments in firm-specific human capital and training . Less turnover means companies save money on recruitment and training, and maintain a highly skilled and experienced workforce, driving long-term economic growth. While some argue that unions hamstring management’s flexibility, they also have pronounced advantages for productivity. Unions can make a company a more desirable place to work and thereby help attract more and better job applicants.

Finally, labor unions foster a culture of innovation by encouraging workers to share their ideas and suggestions for improving products, processes, and services. *A 2021 study* found strong support for the notion that local bargaining leads to product innovation, either alone or together with technological innovation . This leads to increased competitiveness and economic growth. Unions empower workers to voice their insights, creating a collaborative and innovative work environment. Continuous improvement drives productivity and gives companies a competitive edge in the global market.

In conclusion, labor unions are undeniably beneficial to economic growth. They reduce income inequality, cultivate a skilled workforce, and foster innovation. These benefits directly contribute to a more robust, equitable, and sustainable economy for all. Therefore, we urge you to support policies, such as the PRO Act, that help unions to promote economic growth, leading to a better future.

Output B - Against Side
(Optional) Against Transcript B
We acknowledge the opposition's definitions, but it's vital to understand that an exclusive focus on collective bargaining overlooks how unions can limit the flexibility and efficiency businesses need to compete effectively.

While improvements in living standards are a component of economic growth, prioritizing social equity and innovation at the expense of economic prosperity can result in unsustainable policies. The central question is: do labor unions truly foster economic growth? We firmly believe they do not.

Consider first how union-negotiated wage increases, disconnected from individual productivity, inflate labor costs and diminish overall economic competitiveness. These artificially elevated wages, driven by collective bargaining rather than individual output, can price businesses out of the market, leading to reduced hiring and slower economic expansion. While unions may claim to increase productivity, they often fail to address the core issue: higher wages and benefits inflate production costs. These costs are frequently passed on to consumers, decreasing demand and potentially leading to inflationary pressures that negate any productivity gains. This artificial inflation of costs can make businesses less competitive in the global market. According to a 2016 report by Per Krusell and Leena Rudanko from the National Bureau of Economic Research, a labor market controlled by a monopoly union can raise wages by 11%, consequently raising unemployment from 5% to 16%, and reducing output by 12%, relative to efficient outcomes . This demonstrates the significant distortion union wage policies can create in labor markets.

Second, union work rules and rigid job classifications stifle innovation and operational efficiency, hindering a business's ability to adapt to market changes. The claim of streamlined production ignores the negative impact of standardized work rules imposed by unions. Such rules prevent employers from adapting quickly to changing market conditions and adopting innovative technologies, ultimately reducing efficiency and flexibility. Furthermore, standardized wages limit the ability to reward high-performing employees, discouraging individual initiative and hindering overall productivity. A 2017 study by Daniel Bradley, Incheol Kim, and Xuan Tian in *Management Science* found that passing a union election results in an 8.7% decline in patent quantity . This illustrates how unionization can impede firm innovation.

Third, union-driven strikes and work stoppages disrupt supply chains and economic activity, leading to significant financial losses. These disruptions can cause ripple effects throughout the economy, impacting businesses, consumers, and overall economic stability. *Money Talks News* reported that the Volvo-owned Mack Trucks agreed to a contract with substantial raises for workers at facilities in Pennsylvania, Maryland and Florida after a strike that lasted more than a month . The Economic Policy Institute reported that major strike activity increased by 280% in 2023 . These disruptions have far-reaching consequences that extend well beyond the bargaining table.

In conclusion, while labor unions advocate for workers, they often hinder economic growth through inflated costs, stifled innovation, and disruptive actions. We urge you to consider the broader economic implications and reject the notion that labor unions are inherently beneficial to economic growth.
