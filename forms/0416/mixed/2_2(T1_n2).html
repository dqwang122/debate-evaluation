Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
We stand firmly in support of the resolution: Labor unions are beneficial to economic growth. Let’s begin by defining our terms to ensure clarity. "Labor unions" are organized associations of workers formed to protect and promote their common interests through collective bargaining. "Economic growth" signifies a sustained increase in a country's production of goods and services, typically measured by GDP growth, but also encompassing improvements in living standards and overall societal well-being.

Therefore, our judging criterion for this debate is overall societal well-being, measured by economic prosperity, social equity, and innovation, all of which are integral to true and sustainable economic growth.

Labor unions are not relics of the past; they are vital engines driving a more prosperous and equitable future.

First, consider how labor unions reduce income inequality. By bringing workers’ collective power to the bargaining table, unions are able to win better wages and benefits for working people. *A 2021 Economic Policy Institute Fact Sheet* shows that, on average, a worker covered by a union contract earns 10.2% more in wages than a peer with similar education, occupation, and experience in a nonunionized workplace in the same industry [1]. Unions shrink the gap between the highest and lowest earners, leading to a more equitable distribution of wealth. The *Economic Policy Institute* also notes that there was less income inequality in the decades following World War II, when union membership was at its highest rate in 1945 [1]. As union strength steadily declined after 1979, income inequality worsened, reaching its worst point since the Great Depression [1]. This boosts overall economic health through increased consumer spending and reduced social instability. Unions promote social stability and increase the spending power of lower-income individuals, further driving economic growth.

Second, union-negotiated benefits packages, including healthcare and retirement plans, attract and retain skilled workers, creating a more stable and productive workforce. Unions may also reduce turnover by increasing job satisfaction. *WorkRise Network* research indicates that unionized workers are less likely to express intent to leave their jobs [2]. This stability supports investments in firm-specific human capital and training [2]. Less turnover means companies save money on recruitment and training, and maintain a highly skilled and experienced workforce, driving long-term economic growth. While some argue that unions hamstring management’s flexibility, they also have pronounced advantages for productivity. Unions can make a company a more desirable place to work and thereby help attract more and better job applicants.

Finally, labor unions foster a culture of innovation by encouraging workers to share their ideas and suggestions for improving products, processes, and services. *A 2021 study* found strong support for the notion that local bargaining leads to product innovation, either alone or together with technological innovation [3]. This leads to increased competitiveness and economic growth. Unions empower workers to voice their insights, creating a collaborative and innovative work environment. Continuous improvement drives productivity and gives companies a competitive edge in the global market.

In conclusion, labor unions are undeniably beneficial to economic growth. They reduce income inequality, cultivate a skilled workforce, and foster innovation. These benefits directly contribute to a more robust, equitable, and sustainable economy for all. Therefore, we urge you to support policies, such as the PRO Act, that help unions to promote economic growth, leading to a better future.

(Optional) For - Transcript A
Thank you, chairperson. Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's opening statement contains four key flaws, which are their narrow definition of economic growth, their shifting of the judging criterion, their industry-specific examples, and their potential arguments against union-led innovation and productivity.

First, the opposition attempts to narrowly define economic growth through isolated industry impacts. We disagree. Economic growth must be viewed holistically, encompassing not just manufacturing productivity, construction costs, innovation automotive industry or airline industry competitiveness. Rather, it should be measured by overall societal well-being and economic prosperity, social equity, and innovation. As demonstrated by the U.S. Treasury Department [4], labor unions enhance worker benefits, reduce income inequality, and increase stability. This aligns with a broader, more accurate definition of economic growth found in mainstream economic texts. By attempting to narrow the scope, the opposition fails to address the true, sustainable economic growth that benefits all of society, not just select industries.

Second, the opposition implicitly tries to shift the judging criterion to focus on specific industries. This is unacceptable because it fails to capture sustainable economic growth and widespread economic prosperity. Focusing solely on specific industries ignores the interconnectedness of the economy and the need for broader societal benefits. We maintain that the judging criterion should be overall societal well-being. Our evidence clearly demonstrates that unions contribute to this broader goal by reducing income inequality, increasing worker wages, and fostering a more equitable society.

Third, the opposition will likely attempt to undermine our case with specific industry examples that paint labor unions in a negative light. Even if some industries see initial cost increases, the long-term benefits of reduced inequality and increased innovation outweigh these costs, leading to overall societal well-being. Moreover, the Economic Policy Institute [1] and the U.S. Treasury Department [4] finds that unions improve worker safety and lower health inequality. The positive impact of labor unions on economic productivity are typically largest where competitive pressure exists and these positive effects are largely restricted to the private.

Finally, the opposition might argue that unions stifle innovation. However, studies show labor unions benefit economic growth by reducing income inequality and raising wages, benefiting overall productivity and economic stability [1, 2, 4]. Unions also improve workplace conditions and worker safety. Continuous improvement drives productivity and gives companies a competitive edge in the global market, contributing significantly to economic growth [3].

(Optional) For Transcript B
Let’s begin by reinforcing our foundational definitions. Labor unions are organized associations of workers, formed to protect and promote their interests through collective bargaining. Economic growth signifies a sustained increase in a nation's production, improvements in living standards, and enhanced societal well-being. Remember, our judging criterion is overall societal well-being.

The opposition attacks our argument that labor unions foster a culture of innovation, claiming unions negatively impact manufacturing productivity and hinder innovation, particularly in the automotive industry. This contradicts our initial argument that unions foster a culture of innovation by ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions, which ultimately increases competitiveness. While unions certainly influence these sectors, attributing changes in productivity and innovation solely to their presence is a gross oversimplification. Innovation is a multifaceted process influenced by technological advancements, market demand, R&D investment, global competition, and the overall economic climate. As *Forbes* reported, companies achieve the best returns on investments in new technology when they merge implementation with complementary changes in work processes, employee training, and worker input.

Now, let's address the opposition's core arguments directly. First, they claim that labor unions negatively impact manufacturing productivity. This ignores numerous other critical factors. To blame unions alone is a gross oversimplification.

Second, they allege that labor unions increase construction costs. Construction costs are influenced by material prices, regulatory requirements, and land values. Again, blaming unions alone is an oversimplification.

Third, the opposition contends that labor unions hinder innovation in the automotive industry. Innovation in this sector depends on market demand, technological breakthroughs, and R&D investment, not just the presence or absence of unions.

Fourth, they argue that labor unions reduce competitiveness in the airline industry. Competitiveness in this industry is determined by things like fuel costs, route networks, and customer service, not just labor unions.

Finally, the opposition alludes to a study correlating union density and GDP growth. But correlation does not equal causation. Attributing GDP growth solely to union density is misleading. There are countless other significant economic drivers, including technological innovation, government policies, and global trade dynamics.

Furthermore, unions play a pivotal role in securing legislated labor protections and rights, such as safety and health, overtime, and family/medical leave, and in enforcing those rights on the job, according to *a 2003 report by the Economic Policy Institute*. The *Economic Policy Institute* also found that unions improve benefits for nonunionized workers because workers are more likely to be provided particular benefits and because the specific benefits received are better.

In essence, the opposition's arguments rely on oversimplifications and a failure to consider the multifaceted nature of economic growth. Their claims neglect the beneficial impacts of unions on worker morale and productivity, and they neglect the other crucial factors that drive economic prosperity. Remember, unions are not a silver bullet, but they are an essential component of a fair and thriving economy. They provide workers a voice and a path to a better life.
Therefore, we stand firm in our support of the resolution.

**Reference**
Walters, Matthew and Lawrence Mishel. "How unions help all workers." *Economic Policy Institute*, August 26, 2003.
