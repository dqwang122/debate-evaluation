Debate Output Comparison

You'll be comparing two different debate outputs side by side. Please evaluate both versions and provide ratings for each.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone.

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining point.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and deep audience connection.

Thank you, chairperson, and esteemed judges. In today's debate, our discussion primarily revolved around the definition of economic growth and the impact of labor unions on innovation and overall economic prosperity.

Let's begin with the very foundation of this debate: the definition of economic growth. While GDP is undeniably a component, it's insufficient in isolation. We must consider the broader picture – a picture that includes environmental responsibility, social equity, and, crucially, a climate that fosters innovation and adaptability. The Easterlin Paradox clearly indicates this shortsightedness. We must ask ourselves, what benefits union members cannot benefit the entire economy.

This brings us to the core of our disagreement: the impact of unions on innovation. Our opponents claim unions can boost innovation, but the evidence suggests otherwise. Research from the University of Texas Rio Grande Valley shows that passing a union election results in an 8.7% decline in patent quantity [1]. The Heritage Foundation report highlights the thousands of dollars in attorney fees spent negotiating changes in union workplaces. The increased prices for goods and services harm consumers, especially small business owners already struggling with inflation. Ultimately, businesses will move somewhere with cheaper labor, to innovate the same services somewhere else.

Our opponents champion union wage premiums as reflections of productivity, but these wages are often driven by collective bargaining power, not economic factors. This creates disparities and reduces a company's ability to invest in research and development or expand its operations. Strikes can deter businesses from investing in a region, leading to long-term economic stagnation.

Our opponents offer a vision of short-term gains for a select group of workers. We offer a vision of long-term economic prosperity for all.

**Reference**
(1) Bradley, Daniel, Incheol Kim, and Xuan Tian. "Do Unions Affect Innovation?" *Management Science*, 63, no.7 (July 2017): 2251-2271.
