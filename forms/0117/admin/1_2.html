Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

In this debate, we shall argue that pursuing a four-year college degree remains beneficial for young adults in today's society. A four-year degree is defined as completing an undergraduate program at an accredited institution, resulting in a Bachelor's degree. Beneficial is defined as contributing to overall well-being and future success, encompassing financial security, career prospects, personal growth, and societal contribution. Young adults are defined as individuals aged 18-25, the typical age range for undergraduate college enrollment.

Our judging criteria is maximizing future potential. The best approach maximizes the potential of young adults to thrive in the 21st century across various dimensions: economic, social, and personal.

First, a four-year college degree significantly enhances the economic prospects of young adults. Studies show that bachelor's degree holders earn substantially more over their lifetime than those with only a high school diploma. Research indicates a median weekly earnings difference of over $600, translating to a significant lifetime earnings advantage. This increased earning potential provides financial security and stability, crucial for maximizing future potential. Furthermore, the unemployment rate for degree holders is consistently lower , offering greater job security in a competitive market.

Second, college fosters crucial skills and personal development vital for success in a complex world. Research from the Association of American Colleges and Universities highlights the importance of critical thinking, communication, and problem-solving skills, all of which are cultivated through a four-year college education. Studies show that college graduates demonstrate higher levels of these crucial skills, equipping them to navigate complex challenges and adapt to the evolving demands of the 21st-century workforce, maximizing their professional potential.

Third, a college education expands social networks and provides opportunities for personal growth. The college environment offers diverse experiences and interactions , fostering valuable social connections and promoting personal development through exposure to different perspectives and ideas. Research indicates that college graduates are more likely to be involved in civic activities and report higher levels of life satisfaction. These experiences contribute to a broader understanding of the world and increased self-awareness, maximizing both personal and societal potential.

In conclusion, a four-year college degree, while not the only path to success, remains a highly beneficial investment for young adults, providing significant advantages in terms of economic prospects, skill development, and personal growth, ultimately maximizing their potential to thrive in today's society.

In this debate, we shall argue that pursuing a four-year college degree is not necessarily beneficial for all young adults in today's society. A four-year degree, while potentially valuable, is not the only path to a fulfilling and successful life, and its high cost and often misaligned curriculum can be detrimental to the overall well-being of young adults.

Our judging criteria is maximizing overall well-being and long-term success for young adults. This encompasses financial security, career satisfaction, personal growth, and contributions to society, acknowledging that true success extends beyond purely monetary measures.

First, the high cost of a four-year college degree and the resulting student loan debt create a significant financial burden that can hinder, rather than help, young adults' overall well-being. Studies show that student loan debt in the US has surpassed $1.6 trillion, with the average borrower owing tens of thousands of dollars. This debt can delay major life milestones, such as buying a home or starting a family, and create long-term financial stress. Furthermore, the escalating cost of tuition , averaging $43,505 at private colleges and $24,513 for out-of-state students at public universities in 2024-2025, often necessitates substantial borrowing, perpetuating this cycle of debt. This financial strain directly contradicts the idea that a four-year degree is universally beneficial, as it can severely impact a young adult's financial well-being.

Second, alternative pathways, such as trade schools, vocational training, apprenticeships, and online programs, offer more accessible and affordable routes to acquiring in-demand skills, leading to both financial stability and career satisfaction. Research indicates that trade schools provide focused, practical training for specific careers, often at a fraction of the cost and time of a four-year degree. These programs address the growing skills gap in industries like technology and healthcare, leading to high-demand, well-paying jobs. For example, the Alphabet Inc. and Udacity Nanodegree program offers specialized training in fields like data science, directly addressing industry needs. This targeted approach not only facilitates quicker entry into the workforce but also fosters career satisfaction by equipping individuals with practical, immediately applicable skills, thus contributing to their overall well-being.

Third, the existence of a significant skills gap in today's job market demonstrates that a four-year degree is not the sole path to career success. Studies show that employers increasingly prioritize practical skills and experience over traditional academic credentials. While a four-year degree can provide a broad theoretical foundation, it often lacks the specific, in-demand skills required for many jobs. For instance, the demand for skills like coding, data analysis, and digital marketing continues to grow, and these skills are often more effectively acquired through targeted training programs rather than traditional degree programs. This reinforces the idea that alternative pathways can be more effective in preparing young adults for successful careers, contributing to their long-term well-being and challenging the notion that a four-year degree is universally beneficial.


In conclusion, while a four-year college degree can be valuable for some, its high cost, potential for substantial debt, and frequent misalignment with current job market demands make it not universally beneficial for all young adults. Alternative pathways offer more accessible, affordable, and often more effective routes to fulfilling careers and overall well-being.

(Optional) Against - Transcript
Thank you, chairperson. Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's rebuttals, while attempting to address our concerns, fail to fully grasp the realities of the current economic landscape and the diverse pathways to success available to young adults today. Their arguments regarding long-term financial benefits, the adaptability offered by four-year degrees, and universities’ efforts to bridge the skills gap are insufficient to justify the significant costs and risks associated with pursuing a traditional four-year degree.

First, while the affirmative highlights the potential for higher lifetime earnings, they overlook the crucial element of *present value* and the crippling impact of immediate debt on young adults. The average student loan debt is now over $37,000 , a significant burden that impacts current financial well-being and delays major life decisions. Furthermore, the promised higher earnings are not guaranteed. A 2022 report by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York indicated that 41% of recent college graduates are underemployed , working in jobs that don't typically require a bachelor's degree. This, combined with research like the Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce’s report, "Learning for Earning: A Demand-Side Approach to Understanding College Outcomes" , which highlights the disconnect between college majors and career paths, further undermines the affirmative's ROI argument. While income-driven repayment plans may offer temporary relief, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau offers resources outlining potential downsides, including the complexity of the plans and the possibility of accruing additional interest if payments are too low .

Second, the affirmative's attempt to downplay the value of trade schools by citing expensive niche programs is misleading. Data from the National Center for Education Statistics, specifically Table 330.50, shows that the average tuition and fees for public two-year institutions were $3,800 in 2020-21, significantly less than the $10,740 for public four-year institutions , allowing for quicker entry into the workforce and faster earning potential. Moreover, the demand for skilled tradespeople is surging. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects robust job growth in several trades, including HVAC technicians , electricians , and plumbers between 2021 and 2031 , offering stable, well-paying careers that directly challenge the affirmative's assertion that a four-year degree offers greater adaptability and long-term career prospects.

Third, while some universities are making efforts to integrate practical skills, these programs remain the exception, not the rule. Most four-year degrees still prioritize theoretical knowledge over the in-demand skills employers seek. Adding programs like Northeastern's co-op or UC Berkeley Extension courses increases both the time and financial investment required. Furthermore, these specialized programs often have limited capacity, restricting access for many students. Comparatively, online platforms like Coursera and edX offer a vast array of affordable and readily accessible courses in high-demand fields like data science, coding, and digital marketing , making them a more practical and efficient solution for bridging the skills gap.
