Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 30 minutes and a $10 will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that student-athletes should receive profits from college sports revenue. This means that student-athletes, particularly in revenue-generating sports, should receive direct financial compensation beyond scholarships, stipends, and cost of attendance, derived from the revenue their athletic participation generates. This includes, but is not limited to, revenue sharing, appearance fees, and performance bonuses.

Our judging criteria is maximizing student-athlete welfare. This framework prioritizes the overall well-being of student-athletes, encompassing their financial security, educational opportunities, and physical and mental health. This is appropriate because the core purpose of collegiate athletics should be to benefit the students involved, not solely the institutions.

First, profit-sharing fairly compensates student-athletes for their labor and contribution to revenue generation. Student-athletes, particularly in high-revenue sports like football and basketball, generate substantial income for their institutions through ticket sales, merchandise, and lucrative media deals. For example, the NCAA's March Madness basketball tournament generates almost $1 billion in revenue annually . These athletes dedicate significant time and effort, often exceeding 40 hours per week , at the expense of other academic or personal pursuits, making their athletic participation akin to a full-time job. Profit-sharing acknowledges their substantial contribution to this revenue stream and provides fair compensation for their labor, much like revenue-sharing models in professional sports leagues like the NFL and NBA which distribute a percentage of league revenue to the players . Therefore, implementing profit-sharing in college sports recognizes the economic realities of these athletes' contributions and compensates them accordingly.

Second, profit-sharing promotes financial stability and reduces financial stress for student-athletes. Research indicates that many student-athletes, even those on scholarship, struggle financially . A survey found that 60% of college athletes reported concerns about daily expenses . Profit-sharing can alleviate these financial burdens, allowing them to focus on their academics and athletic performance without the added stress of financial insecurity. Stories of athletes benefiting from NIL deals further highlight this potential - for instance, one athlete used NIL earnings to support his family after his father lost his job . By improving financial well-being, institutions can facilitate a better learning environment by reducing financial-related stress, theoretically allowing students to focus more on academics. Thus, distributing a portion of sports revenue directly benefits athletes, allowing them to fully engage in their educational experience.

Third, profit-sharing enhances the educational experience and aligns with the values of collegiate athletics. By reducing financial burdens and providing fair compensation, profit-sharing enables student-athletes to fully engage in their educational experience. It can also promote a sense of professionalism and responsibility, teaching valuable financial management skills that will benefit them beyond their collegiate careers. Programs teaching financial literacy have been shown to improve financial outcomes for participants . This ultimately strengthens the educational mission of universities and aligns with the ideal of supporting student-athletes holistically. Just as professional athletes benefit from financial management training , so too would student athletes learn valuable financial skills. Therefore, sharing profits not only rewards their labor but prepares them for life after college.

In conclusion, implementing profit-sharing in college sports is a crucial step towards ensuring fair compensation, promoting financial stability, and enhancing the overall educational experience for student-athletes. It recognizes their significant contributions, addresses the economic realities of college sports, and ultimately aligns with the core values of higher education.

In this debate, we shall argue that student-athletes should *not* receive profits from college sports revenue. This means we oppose any system where student-athletes are directly compensated beyond their existing scholarships and benefits for their athletic participation.
Our judging criteria is the preservation of the collegiate model, which prioritizes the student-athlete's academic pursuits and the overall educational mission of the university. Any system of compensation should be evaluated based on its impact on this core principle.

First, paying student-athletes undermines the amateur ideal of college sports. The collegiate model is built on the foundation of amateurism, where student-athletes participate primarily for the love of the sport and the pursuit of education. Introducing financial incentives shifts this dynamic and creates a more professionalized environment, potentially detracting from the educational mission. Studies examining the history of amateurism in sport, such as , demonstrate how the shift towards professionalism can often lead to a decline in the intrinsic motivation and educational focus of participants.

Second, financial aid already provides substantial compensation for student-athletes. Many student-athletes receive scholarships covering tuition, fees, room, and board, representing a substantial financial investment by universities . Adding salaries to this existing compensation creates a system of double payment, which is inequitable compared to the vast majority of students who receive no financial aid. Furthermore, research like demonstrates that the current distribution of athletic scholarships already heavily favors revenue-generating sports, creating disparities within athletic departments. Direct compensation would likely exacerbate this issue, further disadvantaging athletes in non-revenue sports.

Third, paying student-athletes creates an uneven playing field and exacerbates existing inequalities. Wealthier universities with larger athletic budgets would inevitably offer higher salaries, creating an even greater competitive imbalance. This would essentially turn college sports into a bidding war, where success is determined by financial power rather than athletic talent or coaching ability . Such a system undermines the principles of fair competition and further concentrates resources among a select few elite programs. Studies like have shown the detrimental impact of financial disparities on competitive balance in professional sports, highlighting the potential pitfalls of a similar model in college athletics.

In conclusion, compensating student-athletes beyond their current scholarships and benefits undermines the amateur ideal, creates financial inequities both within and between universities, and ultimately jeopardizes the educational mission of the collegiate model.

This approach best maximizes student-athlete welfare, our judging criteria.

Thank you, chairperson, and esteemed judges. Today's debate centers on a fundamental question: what is the purpose of collegiate athletics? The affirmative argues for a pay-for-play system, claiming it modernizes amateurism. We argue it fundamentally *undermines* it, jeopardizing the very essence of the collegiate model and the long-term well-being of student-athletes.

Their argument that paying athletes reflects a modern understanding of amateurism is a fallacy. Introducing salaries inevitably diminishes the intrinsic motivation of student-athletes, shifting their focus from the love of the game to financial gain. While NIL deals allow for endorsements, direct payment from universities blurs the line between student and professional, turning college sports into a minor league farm system. This shift, as Guttmann explains, undermines the historical and philosophical underpinnings of amateurism, eroding the values that make college sports unique.

The affirmative claims scholarships are insufficient, pointing to the financial struggles faced by some athletes. However, they fail to acknowledge the substantial investment universities already make in student-athletes . Moreover, a pay-for-play system would create an unsustainable financial burden on athletic departments, potentially impacting non-revenue sports, coaching salaries, and even alumni donations. The long-term consequences for the financial stability of universities could be devastating. Instead of a radical restructuring, we advocate for targeted solutions, like increased need-based stipends and financial literacy programs , that address the genuine needs of student-athletes without jeopardizing the future of college sports.

Furthermore, their assertion that salary caps can address competitive imbalance is naive. Implementing and enforcing such a system in college sports would be a logistical and legal nightmare, especially considering Title IX implications. How do you fairly compensate athletes across men's and women's sports with vastly different revenue streams? A pay-for-play model risks creating legal challenges and exacerbating existing inequalities, potentially leading to lawsuits and sanctions. As Zimbalist highlights, such disparities threaten the very foundations of fair competition.

The collegiate model offers something far more valuable than a paycheck: an education. It prepares student-athletes not just for a few years of professional sports, but for a lifetime of success. It instills values of teamwork, discipline, and perseverance that transcend the playing field. By preserving the integrity of the collegiate model, we invest in the *future* of student-athletes, empowering them to achieve their full potential, both on and off the court, long after their playing days are over. Let us not sacrifice this invaluable opportunity for the fleeting allure of a paycheck.
