Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone.

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining point.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and deep audience connection.

Good morning, everyone. We stand firm today in our assertion that AI will lead to a decline in human creative arts. AI's threat to originality, copyright, and diversity forms a perfect storm that will diminish the richness and value of human creative expression. To clarify, we define "decline" not as complete obliteration, but as a demonstrable reduction in originality, diversity, and ethical integrity within artistic output. These three values – originality, diversity, and ethical integrity – are the cornerstones upon which we will base our arguments.

First, consider how AI's reliance on existing data stifles true artistic innovation. AI algorithms learn from vast datasets, inevitably reinforcing established trends and popular styles. This leads to a homogenization of artistic expression, discouraging artists from venturing into uncharted creative territories. It’s akin to a musician who exclusively listens to top-40 hits; their music will predictably mirror what's already on the radio, devoid of a unique and authentic voice. The heart of the matter is that genuine artistic innovation arises from experimentation, from fearlessly pushing boundaries, not merely replicating existing content. By prioritizing popular styles and established norms, AI curtails the exploration of truly unique and groundbreaking creative visions.

Second, AI art introduces complex copyright and ownership dilemmas. Determining the rightful owner of AI-generated art becomes exceedingly difficult, particularly when the AI is trained on pre-existing copyrighted material. This uncertainty discourages artists from fully embracing AI tools, fearing potential legal entanglements and intellectual property disputes. Imagine a sculptor hesitant to create, paralyzed by the fear of inadvertently infringing upon another artist's design. This legal ambiguity casts a shadow over the creative process, stifling artistic expression and innovation. More than half of artists worry that generative AI will have a detrimental effect on their revenue . The concern suggests that artists fear AI might devalue human-made art or increase competition in the art market.

Third, AI-driven creative tools have the potential to amplify existing societal biases. If the data used to train AI reflects inherent biases, the art it generates will likely perpetuate those biases, reinforcing harmful stereotypes and inequalities within society. This is not merely a matter of algorithms; it's about the potential for perpetuating societal inequities through artistic mediums. It’s like a hall of mirrors, reflecting and amplifying distorted images, thereby undermining the diversity of artistic voices and ultimately contributing to a decline in the richness and originality of human creative arts.

We anticipate the opposition will argue that AI enhances creativity by providing new tools and possibilities. However, this argument is flawed. While AI can generate outputs, it lacks the conscious intention, lived experience, and critical reflection that fuels truly meaningful art. True creativity demands originality, ethical considerations, and a diverse range of voices – all of which are jeopardized by the increasing dominance of AI in the arts. As we move forward, it is imperative that we critically evaluate AI's role in the arts, ensuring that it serves to enhance, not diminish, the unique power and potential of human creativity.
