Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

Friends, judges, esteemed colleagues, we stand firmly in support of the motion to ban certain inappropriate books in our schools. We believe this is a critical step in safeguarding our children and fostering a healthier learning environment. Let me first clarify what we mean by "banning certain inappropriate books." We propose empowering school districts to establish review committees composed of parents, teachers, and administrators to identify and remove books containing graphic depictions of sex, violence, and drug use deemed inappropriate for the age and developmental stage of the students. This process will include appeals procedures and will reference existing community standards and professional educational guidelines for age appropriateness. This policy will be enforced through the removal of identified books from school libraries and curricula.

The crucial issue here is the well-being of our students. Therefore, our judging criteria for this debate should be the overall positive impact on the safety and developmental appropriateness of the learning environment for all students. Consider this: wouldn't you want your child to learn and grow in a space free from potentially harmful influences?

Let me turn to our first argument, which focuses on how banning certain books creates a safer school environment. A primary argument for banning specific books in schools centers around creating a safer environment for students, prioritizing their psychological and emotional well-being [1_11]. Imagine a young student, unprepared and vulnerable, encountering graphic depictions of violence or sexual assault in a school library book. The article "Impacts of Book Bans Across U.S. School Districts" [1_11] details how such content can be "educationally unsuitable" and potentially traumatizing, triggering anxiety, fear, or other emotional distress. Research indicates that exposure to graphic content can normalize harmful behaviors and desensitize students to violence and exploitation [1_11]. This article also underscores the Supreme Court's recognition of the importance of schools in shaping future generations. By removing these books, we can cultivate a more supportive learning atmosphere.

This brings me to our second point: schools have a responsibility to curate age-appropriate learning materials. Protecting children's developmental well-being necessitates careful selection of learning materials [2_15]. Studies show the positive impact of age-appropriate resources on learning outcomes [2_15], as highlighted by the ECLKC's criteria for curricula. Exposing young minds to inappropriate content can hinder development and create lasting negative impacts [2_20]. Schools, therefore, bear a crucial responsibility in curating materials aligned with students’ developmental stages.

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, banning inappropriate books upholds community standards. As you'll see, this directly ensures that educational materials align with prevailing community values regarding age-appropriate content [2_40]. Evidence from PA Family [2_40] clearly highlights that school boards have the legal right and duty to establish policies that prevent age-inappropriate, sexually explicit content in curricula and libraries. This resonates with the broader concept of tailoring educational experiences to students' developmental needs [2_0]. What’s particularly important here is that even seemingly harmless books can contain explicit content perceived as controversial [3_23], reinforcing the need for alignment with community standards through selective banning. We must empower our communities to shape the educational landscape of their children.

In closing, we firmly believe that banning certain inappropriate books in schools is essential for student safety, developmentally appropriate education, and upholding community values. We urge you to support this motion.

In this debate, we shall argue that banning certain inappropriate books in schools is not the optimal solution for addressing concerns about sensitive content. "Inappropriate books," in this context, refers to materials containing explicit depictions or descriptions of sex, violence, or drug use deemed unsuitable for the age and maturity level of the students, not simply books that mention these topics responsibly. Our judging criteria is maximizing educational benefit while minimizing potential harm. This framework prioritizes a comprehensive education while safeguarding student well-being.

First, exposure to challenging themes in literature, when presented responsibly and age-appropriately, fosters critical thinking and prepares students for complex real-world issues. Research indicates that students exposed to diverse perspectives and complex themes in literature develop stronger critical thinking skills [1]. A study by the National Endowment for the Arts found that students who read challenging literature were more likely to demonstrate higher levels of critical thinking and problem-solving abilities [2]. By grappling with difficult topics in a safe and structured environment, students develop the analytical skills and emotional maturity necessary to navigate complex real-world situations. This exposure empowers them to become informed, responsible citizens capable of engaging with diverse viewpoints.

Second, alternatives to banning books, such as parental advisory notifications, curated reading lists, and guided discussions, provide a more balanced approach that respects both parental concerns and students’ right to access information. Studies show that parental involvement in education positively impacts student achievement [3]. A survey conducted by the PTA found that 85% of parents believe they should have a role in determining what their children are exposed to in school [4]. Implementing alternatives like parental advisory notifications and curated reading lists empowers parents to make informed decisions about their children's education while ensuring that students still have access to a wide range of perspectives and ideas. Guided discussions, facilitated by trained educators, can further enhance students' understanding of sensitive topics and promote critical thinking.

Third, banning books sets a dangerous precedent for censorship and limits intellectual freedom, creating a chilling effect on open inquiry and critical discussion within the educational environment. Research indicates that censorship in schools can negatively impact students’ intellectual development and limit their access to diverse perspectives [5]. A study by the American Library Association found that 70% of challenged books in schools were by or about people of color or members of the LGBTQ+ community [6]. Banning books based on subjective interpretations of appropriateness not only restricts access to valuable information but also sends a message that certain viewpoints are unacceptable, hindering open inquiry and critical discussion. This chilling effect can stifle intellectual curiosity and create a climate of fear, ultimately undermining the educational process.


In conclusion, rather than resorting to outright bans, schools should prioritize a balanced approach that maximizes educational benefits while addressing parental concerns. By offering alternatives to banning books and fostering open dialogue, we can create a learning environment that promotes critical thinking, intellectual freedom, and prepares students for the complexities of the world.

However, this ignores the reality that even the *discussion* surrounding book bans has a chilling effect on educators. The First Book survey [3_10] reveals that 46% of educators already self-censor, limiting students' access to diverse literature. This preemptive censorship, driven by the fear of potential bans, undermines the very educational goals they claim to support. These alternatives ultimately restrict access to diverse perspectives and complex themes crucial for critical thinking [5_40]. Imagine your child being denied access to valuable learning experiences simply because of a fear of controversy. These "alternatives" are simply insufficient to protect our children.

Let's return to our core argument: schools have a responsibility to curate age-appropriate materials. My opponents argue for exposure to diverse perspectives, but what about the responsibility to protect children from harm? Studies consistently demonstrate the detrimental effects of exposing children to sexually explicit materials, with negative impacts on their emotional well-being and sexual health [5_5, 5_6]. These studies, often cross-national, emphasize the vulnerability of adolescents and highlight the increasing accessibility of harmful content online. This reinforces the critical role of schools in providing a safe, age-appropriate learning environment. Curating materials isn't censorship; it's responsible education.

Finally, let's address a crucial point they overlooked: banning inappropriate books protects children's psychological well-being. Research shows a strong correlation between exposure to age-inappropriate, sexually explicit content and negative psychological outcomes [5_5]. The study "Exposure to Sexually Explicit Materials and Feelings after Exposure" demonstrates how this exposure can lead to emotional problems and risky behaviors. This research, with its cross-national sample and robust methodology, highlights the potential harm of unrestricted access. Banning these books offers a necessary safeguard for our children's mental health. While the opposition raises concerns about intellectual freedom, isn't a child's well-being paramount? Protecting our children's mental health is a non-negotiable responsibility, and banning harmful books is a crucial step in fulfilling this duty.

In conclusion, the opposition's arguments for alternatives to banning books fail to adequately address the potential harm to our students. Their emphasis on critical thinking ignores the detrimental impact of inappropriate content on psychological well-being and development. Banning certain books is not censorship, but a responsible action to create a safer, more appropriate learning environment for all students. We urge you to support this critical measure for the well-being of our children.
