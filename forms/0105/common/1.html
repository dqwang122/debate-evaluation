Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 30 minutes and a $10 will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that online public opinion trials bring us closer to justice. Online public opinion trials are defined as situations where legal cases or controversies are extensively discussed and debated in online public forums, potentially influencing legal proceedings or outcomes. This includes social media discussions, online petitions, and citizen journalism efforts related to legal matters. Justice is a state of affairs in which fairness, equity, and the rule of law prevail, ensuring that individuals receive due process, appropriate punishments or remedies, and protection from harm. "Closer to Justice" means progress towards a more just outcome, increasing fairness, equity, and adherence to the rule of law in legal processes and outcomes.

Our judging criteria is maximizing societal benefit. The side that best demonstrates a net positive impact on society's pursuit of justice, considering factors like fairness, accuracy, accountability, access to justice, and protection of individual rights, should win. This framework is appropriate because the motion is fundamentally about whether online public opinion trials improve or hinder the overall effectiveness and fairness of the justice system.

First, online public opinion trials enhance transparency and accountability within the legal system. Increased public scrutiny through online platforms holds legal actors accountable, exposes misconduct, and promotes fairness. For example, in 2021, public outcry on social media following the police killing of Elijah McClain contributed to a $15 million settlement in a civil rights lawsuit [1]. Studies show that public pressure resulting from online scrutiny can lead to police reforms and investigations into misconduct [2]. This transparency and accountability are crucial for a just legal system, as highlighted by research on the role of transparency in good governance [3].

Second, online platforms amplify marginalized voices and facilitate access to justice. Traditional legal processes can be inaccessible or biased against certain groups. Online platforms provide avenues for marginalized communities to share their experiences, organize legal support, and advocate for their rights. Research indicates that online campaigns have played a key role in legal reforms benefiting marginalized groups, such as the #MeToo movement raising awareness of sexual harassment and assault [4]. This increased access to justice and amplified voice for the marginalized contributes to a more equitable legal system.

Third, online public opinion trials facilitate information sharing and public education about legal issues, improving public understanding and engagement with the justice system. Open online discussions about legal cases can educate the public about legal principles, processes, and potential biases, fostering informed participation in legal and political discourse. For instance, online discussions surrounding high-profile court cases often involve explanations of complex legal concepts, making them more accessible to the general public. This increased awareness ultimately strengthens the pursuit of justice. A study by [5] demonstrated that online discussions of hypothetical legal problems led to deeper analysis and critical thinking among participants.

In conclusion, by fostering transparency and accountability, amplifying marginalized voices, and promoting public education, online public opinion trials ultimately contribute to a more just and equitable legal system, bringing us closer to, not further from, justice.
