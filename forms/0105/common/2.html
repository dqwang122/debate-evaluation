Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 30 minutes and a $10 will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that mandatory social media verification, defined as legally requiring all users to confirm their real-world identities with official documentation before using social media platforms, should not be implemented. Our judging criteria is the balancing of competing interests, meaning the optimal approach considers the trade-offs between safety and security, freedom of expression, privacy, and practical feasibility.

First, mandatory verification infringes on fundamental rights to privacy and anonymity. Requiring users to link their real-world identities to their online presence undermines their ability to express themselves freely without fear of reprisal, particularly for marginalized groups or activists. Studies show that anonymity is crucial for whistleblowers, dissidents, and vulnerable individuals who may face harassment or persecution if their identities are known [1]. For example, research indicates that real-name policies in South Korea and China have led to increased self-censorship and chilling effects on free speech [2]. This demonstrates how mandatory verification can disproportionately harm those who rely on anonymity for safety and self-expression, tilting the balance against freedom of expression and privacy.

Second, mandatory verification is practically infeasible and creates security risks. Verifying the identities of billions of social media users worldwide presents an immense logistical hurdle, requiring substantial resources and technical infrastructure. Research indicates that even sophisticated systems are vulnerable to breaches [3], and the sheer volume of sensitive personal data collected for verification would create a honeypot for hackers. For example, the 2011 data breach at Sony, where 77 million user accounts were compromised, demonstrates the vulnerability of centralized data storage [4]. This highlights the significant security risks inherent in mandatory verification, making it practically infeasible and potentially dangerous.

Third, less restrictive alternatives exist to address harmful content. Instead of blanket verification requirements, platforms can implement more targeted approaches, such as improved reporting mechanisms, community-based moderation, and AI-powered content filtering. Studies show that community-led moderation can be highly effective in addressing harmful content and fostering positive online environments [5]. For example, platforms like Reddit rely heavily on community moderators to enforce rules and maintain order, demonstrating the viability of alternatives to mandatory verification [6]. This proves that less restrictive and more nuanced approaches can effectively combat harmful content without sacrificing user privacy and anonymity.

In conclusion, while online safety is a valid concern, mandatory social media verification is an overreach that infringes on fundamental rights, poses significant security risks, and overlooks viable alternatives. A balanced approach that prioritizes privacy, free speech, and practical feasibility is essential for a healthy online ecosystem.
