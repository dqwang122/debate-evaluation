Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hour and a $30 Amazon gift card will be sent via email as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

Esteemed judges, worthy opponents, and attentive audience, we stand firmly for the motion that escaping from big cities, rather than returning, is the way out for today’s young people. Many young people today feel trapped in a cycle of exorbitant rent, intense job competition, and a pervasive sense of isolation in large urban centers. Escaping these metropolises offers a viable path to a more fulfilling and prosperous future.

"Escaping from big cities" refers to young people relocating from major metropolitan areas to smaller towns or rural areas to pursue opportunities for homeownership, less competitive job markets, and stronger community connections, in contrast to "returning," which implies a pre-existing tie to these alternative locations. Our judging criteria should be the overall well-being and future prospects of young people, encompassing financial security, career opportunities, and social connections. Now, let us delve into the reasons why escaping big cities offers a superior alternative.

Firstly, escaping big cities provides a far greater opportunity for young people to achieve the cornerstone of financial security: homeownership. The dream of owning a home feels increasingly unattainable in major metropolitan areas. Imagine trying to save for a down payment while paying sky-high rent, competing against seasoned professionals with established wealth. The 2017 American Community Survey revealed that homeownership rates in rural areas were significantly higher than in urban areas – 81.1% compared to 59.8%. This striking difference highlights the inherent affordability advantage of smaller communities. The USDA Rural Development's Single Family Housing Programs (1) further enhance this accessibility by offering loans, loan guarantees, and grants specifically designed to help families and individuals buy, build, or repair affordable homes in rural America. This support makes the dream of owning a home a tangible reality, not a distant fantasy.

Secondly, young people escaping big cities benefit from reduced competition in the job market. While some may argue for the abundance of jobs in big cities, they overlook the cutthroat competition that often accompanies it. Picture hundreds of applicants vying for the same entry-level position, many possessing years of experience and advanced degrees. In contrast, smaller towns and rural areas often experience a shortage of skilled workers, especially with the rise of remote work. According to Forbes (2), remote work is permanently altering residential real estate markets nationwide, with smaller communities welcoming a high number of new residents. This influx of remote workers creates new job opportunities and less competition for local talent, giving young people a greater chance to secure meaningful employment and advance their careers. Imagine finding a fulfilling job in a welcoming community without the constant pressure of a hyper-competitive urban environment.

Finally, escaping big cities offers a crucial element often lacking in urban life: a strong sense of community. In the anonymity of a big city, it’s easy to feel like just another face in the crowd. While cities may offer a diverse range of social activities, they often lack the genuine connection and support found in smaller communities. As highlighted in Upgraded Home (3), small towns are “more community-focused with a stronger sense of close relationship.” This close-knit environment fosters a sense of belonging, where neighbors know each other, support local businesses, and actively participate in community events. Imagine raising a family in a place where you feel supported, connected, and genuinely part of something larger than yourself. This sense of community, essential for well-being, is a compelling reason for young people to seek a life outside the urban sprawl.

Our judging criteria will be based on which side presents the most compelling and evidence-based argument for maximizing opportunities and overall well-being for young people.

This variety allows young people to choose housing that aligns with their budget and lifestyle, unlike smaller towns with limited and often homogenous housing stock. As Zillow points out in their "Picking the Type of Home That's Right for You" guide, condos, for example, “are usually more affordable than single-family detached homes.” This diverse housing market ensures affordability and choice, accommodating the financial realities and lifestyle preferences of diverse young populations. Research from SpringerLink's "Cities as Enablers of Innovation" emphasizes that cities create an “organizational climate” that “catalyses innovation." The concentration of creative industries and the cross-pollination of ideas in urban environments contribute significantly to innovation, offering unparalleled opportunities for young professionals in these fields. These environments offer young people a unique opportunity to learn, grow, and contribute to cutting-edge developments across various fields.

Finally, big cities provide unparalleled networking opportunities vital for career advancement, a crucial factor for young people starting their professional journeys. The concentration of businesses, industry leaders, and professionals in metropolitan areas creates a fertile ground for networking. In conclusion, big cities offer a dynamic and opportunity-rich environment uniquely suited to the needs and aspirations of today's young people. The diverse housing options, vibrant cultural landscapes that foster creativity and innovation, and the unparalleled networking opportunities collectively position big cities as the optimal choice for young people seeking personal and professional fulfillment. We urge you to oppose this motion and recognize the power of big cities in shaping the future of young people.

The opposition claims that big cities offer diverse housing options catering to various needs, citing Zillow’s guide on picking the right home. While they mention condos as a more affordable option, this ignores a critical reality: even condos in major metropolitan areas are often priced beyond the reach of young people just starting their careers. Imagine a young professional saddled with student debt, trying to save for a down payment on a condo that costs several hundred thousand dollars. This is the reality for many young urbanites. Further, their cited statistic from the American Housing Survey and U.S. Census Bureau that 31.4% of housing is multifamily primarily located in urban areas actually supports *our* argument. This concentration of multifamily housing indicates a greater reliance on renting, not owning, in urban areas, reinforcing the difficulty young people face in achieving homeownership in big cities. Escaping to smaller towns, where homeownership rates are significantly higher and prices are more attainable, offers a more realistic pathway to building equity and long-term financial security.

The opposition’s argument that big cities are incubators of creativity and innovation, citing BCG and SpringerLink, also misses a crucial point. While cities may offer a concentration of creative industries, this concentration also breeds intense competition and pressure, often stifling creativity rather than nurturing it. Imagine a young artist trying to make a living in a city with thousands of other aspiring artists, competing for limited gallery space, funding, and recognition. This hyper-competitive environment can be overwhelming and demoralizing. Furthermore, innovation isn't limited to big cities. With the rise of remote work and digital connectivity, smaller towns are also becoming hubs of innovation, attracting entrepreneurs and startups seeking a more balanced and affordable lifestyle. Escaping the urban pressure cooker can actually free young creatives to pursue their passions and develop innovative ideas in a more supportive and less saturated environment.

Finally, let's reinforce our argument about the stronger sense of community found in smaller towns. The opposition emphasizes networking opportunities in cities, citing Forbes and LinkedIn, but networking isn't synonymous with community. While cities may facilitate professional connections, they often lack the genuine, supportive relationships that define a strong community. The Washington Post (1) highlights research suggesting that small towns and rural areas are more conducive to forming strong social bonds, explaining the greater sense of belonging observed in these areas. Upgraded Home (2) further emphasizes this, stating that small towns are “more community-focused with a stronger sense of close relationship.” Imagine living in a place where your neighbors become your friends, where you can rely on your community for support, and where you actively contribute to the local fabric. This sense of belonging, so crucial for mental and emotional well-being, is a powerful argument for young people to escape the isolating anonymity of big cities. This is not to discount the importance of networking, but rather to highlight that true community offers a more profound and enriching social experience than mere professional connections.

Judges, esteemed colleagues, and audience members, the opposing side presents a vision of idyllic small-town life that unfortunately clashes with the realities faced by many young people today. While their arguments evoke a sense of nostalgia, they fail to address the concrete advantages that big cities offer for career advancement, personal growth, and access to diverse opportunities.

The opposition attempts to dismantle our point about housing diversity by claiming that even condos in big cities are unaffordable. While affordability is a valid concern, their argument overlooks the crucial aspect of choice. Yes, purchasing a condo in a major city can be expensive, but big cities offer a far wider spectrum of rental options compared to smaller towns. From shared apartments and micro-units to a range of apartment sizes and price points, young people in cities have greater flexibility to find housing that suits their immediate needs and budget. RentHop (1), for instance, highlights Minneapolis as the best city for young professionals due to “relatively affordable housing options (median rent at $1,525).” Furthermore, their interpretation of the multifamily housing statistic twists our point. While it’s true that multifamily housing often means renting, this very density of rental options is a *strength* of big cities, offering young people the flexibility to move, change neighborhoods, and adapt to their evolving career paths and lifestyle preferences – an agility not often found in small towns with limited rental markets.

The opposition’s assertion that the competitive environment of big cities stifles creativity misconstrues the nature of innovation. They paint a picture of struggling artists lost in a sea of competition, but this narrow focus ignores the crucial role of collaboration and cross-pollination of ideas that thrives in urban centers. The World Economic Forum (2), in their discussion of cities and innovation, highlights that agglomeration “generates multiple spillover effects, breeding learning processes that favor creation.” This collaborative ecosystem, born from the very density of talent in big cities, is a powerful driver of innovation. While smaller towns may offer a respite from competition, they often lack the critical mass of talent, resources, and infrastructure that fuels groundbreaking work. The claim that remote work equates to small towns becoming innovation hubs is equally misleading. While remote work opens up possibilities, it doesn't negate the advantages of proximity and in-person collaboration, which remain vital for many creative and innovative industries.

Finally, the opposing side’s emphasis on "community" in small towns, citing the Washington Post and Upgraded Home, presents a romanticized notion that contrasts sharply with the social isolation experienced by many young people in these environments. While smaller towns may boast close-knit relationships among long-term residents, they often present challenges for newcomers, particularly young professionals seeking to build a social and professional network. Nature (3), in their article “Urban youth most isolated in largest cities”, counterintuitively points out that young people can feel *more* isolated in smaller urban areas and suburbs. Big cities, with their diverse populations and multitude of social groups and activities, offer greater opportunities for young people to find their niche, connect with like-minded individuals, and build their own communities based on shared interests, not just proximity. Furthermore, networking, which the opposition attempts to downplay, is a crucial aspect of building social capital, not just for career advancement, but also for personal growth and social integration within a big city.

In conclusion, while small-town living might appeal to some, the dynamic environment of big cities offers unparalleled opportunities for young people to achieve their full potential. The diversity of housing options, the vibrant and collaborative ecosystem that fuels creativity and innovation, and the rich social tapestry that allows for diverse connections all contribute to a more fulfilling and prosperous future for young people. We urge you to oppose the motion and recognize the immense value that big cities hold for the next generation.
