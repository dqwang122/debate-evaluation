Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone.

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining point.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and deep audience connection.

In this debate, we shall argue that AI will lead to the decline of human creative arts. We define "decline" as a gradual and continuous loss of value, power, or quality in the perceived worth, economic viability, and cultural significance of human-created art forms. "Human Creative Arts" encompasses artistic endeavors primarily conceived and executed by human beings, excluding art generated solely by artificial intelligence. We refer to "AI" as computer systems capable of generating art.

Our judging criteria is artistic integrity and cultural value. We believe a world where human expression and creativity are valued over AI-generated content is preferable. The affirmative must prove that AI-driven art significantly undermines human artistic endeavors to meet this standard.

First, AI-generated art devalues human skill and effort. The ease and speed with which AI can produce art diminishes the perceived value of the years of training, practice, and dedication required to master traditional art forms. This leads to decreased demand for human artists and a loss of cultural appreciation for human artistic skill. Studies show that the proliferation of easily accessible AI-generated content can lead to a devaluation of human-created works. For instance, a study published in the *Journal of Cultural Economics* found that increased automation in creative industries often results in lower wages and fewer opportunities for human artists . This is because AI can flood the market with cheap, readily available content, undercutting the prices that human artists can reasonably charge. Second, AI art raises copyright and ownership issues, undermining artists' livelihoods. The use of existing artwork to train AI models raises significant copyright concerns. Artists may find their styles and creations replicated by AI without their consent or compensation, further devaluing their original works and potentially leading to legal battles and economic hardship. Legal scholars, writing in the *Stanford Law Review*, have noted the ambiguity in current copyright law regarding AI-generated art, particularly concerning the "fair use" doctrine . This ambiguity leaves artists vulnerable to having their work exploited without recourse. For example, visual artists are seeing their unique styles replicated by AI models, leading to their art being indistinguishable from AI generated content. This forces artists to compete with near-identical copies of their work, diminishing its value and creating marketplace confusion. **I need legal precedents on AI art copyright and examples of artists suing AI companies to add more weight to this argument.** Thus, unresolved copyright issues and potential for exploitation make it difficult for human artists to sustain their careers.

Third, AI art limits creative exploration and innovation by mimicking existing styles. Because AI art is trained on existing datasets, it tends to replicate and remix established styles rather than pushing the boundaries of artistic expression. This stifles true innovation and leads to a homogenization of art, ultimately diminishing the richness and diversity of human creative culture.

Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript
We stand firmly against the assertion that AI will lead to the decline of human creative arts. While we acknowledge the concerns, we believe this perspective overlooks AI's transformative potential.

Let's begin with a crucial clarification. Imagine a world where all art becomes homogenous and uninspired, reflecting only what algorithms deem popular – *that* would be decline. Our opponents define "decline" as a tangible degradation in quality, uniqueness, and societal value. We contend that this definition is overly restrictive. Instead, we envision a vibrant ecosystem where AI empowers artists to push boundaries, creating entirely new forms of expression we can’t even imagine today. Artistic evolution is not synonymous with decline, and to limit our understanding in this way is to ignore the dynamic nature of art itself. The definition should encompass evolution and transformation as art adapts to new technologies and societal values.

Furthermore, we propose that this debate be judged not solely on whether AI diminishes or enhances human creative endeavors, but on the overall impact of AI on human creativity. Focusing solely on potential drawbacks overlooks AI's significant contributions to artistic innovation and accessibility.

Now, let's address the specific arguments presented. First, the claim that AI's dependence on pre-existing data smothers genuine innovation is a mischaracterization. AI's dependence on existing data does not stifle innovation; rather, it provides a foundation for artists to build upon, experiment with, and create novel works.

Consider the historical parallel of photography. When it emerged, many feared it would spell the end of painting. Instead, photography became an art form of its own, and painting evolved alongside it. Similarly, AI can serve as a springboard for experimentation, allowing artists to explore new styles and techniques that were previously unimaginable.

Second, the assertion that AI imperils the livelihoods of human artists is not inevitable. The accessibility afforded by AI does not necessarily lead to apathy but can foster greater engagement and understanding of art, broadening the audience and creating new consumers. More people engaging with art, even AI-generated art, can stimulate interest in human-created art, leading to increased demand for authentic, unique pieces and art instruction. Thus, AI can augment, not destroy, the value and potential markets for human artistry.

Finally, the argument that over-reliance on AI diminishes essential skills is historically inaccurate. The claim that AI diminishes human skill development ignores the historical precedent of technological advancements augmenting, not replacing, artistic abilities. Instead, AI can augment human artists' abilities, allowing them to focus on higher-level creative tasks and explore new artistic frontiers. As Sungjin Park notes, generative AI introduces a dynamic interplay between technological liberation and dependency, democratizing artistic expression.

In conclusion, we believe that AI presents not a threat, but an opportunity. It enhances artistic accessibility, inspires creativity, and facilitates the preservation of our cultural heritage. We should start to embrace AI to create and experience the future of arts, because it not only enables the creation of more art, but also enables more interaction between human and arts.

**Reference**
[1] Park, Sungjin. "The work of art in the age of generative AI: aura, liberation, and democratization." *AI & SOCIETY* (May 3, 2024).

By equating any change with progress, they create a tautology where AI's impact can never be negative . The National Art Education Association and Culture.org have voiced significant concerns about AI's impact on artistic authenticity, emotional depth, and the ideation process. These concerns demonstrate that AI isn't merely changing art; it's degrading the very qualities that make human art valuable . We must adhere to the definition of decline as a degradation in quality and value to have a meaningful discussion about the risks posed by AI.

Second, our opponents suggest judging this debate on the "overall impact" of AI, including potential benefits like accessibility . While accessibility might be a marginal benefit, it does not outweigh the potential harm to the core aspects of human creativity . Focusing solely on overall impact ignores the demonstrable decline in quality, originality, and value that AI brings to the arts.
