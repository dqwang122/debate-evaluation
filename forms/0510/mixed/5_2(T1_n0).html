Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.
Second Questionnaire: This questionnaire takes around 10 minutes. After submitting this questionnaire, you will be automatically redirected to the next questionnaire, which will take around 15 minutes. If both questionnaires are completed, you will receive the compensation as agreed. If you are unable to commit to completing both questionnaires at this time, please feel free to exit. We understand that your time is valuable and appreciate your participation thus far.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that AI will lead to the decline of human creative arts. We define AI as systems capable of performing creative tasks like generating art, music, and literature with minimal human input, while human creative arts refer to original works produced through human emotional depth and cultural expression .

Our judging criteria is the measurable economic and cultural devaluation of human creative arts due to AI's dominance, evidenced by displacement of human artists, market saturation with generic content, and reduced incentives for human artistic development.

First, AI is systematically replacing human artists in commercial markets. Studies show that AI-generated artworks like "Portrait of Edmond de Belamy" and music albums like "I AM AI" are being adopted by industries for their cost efficiency, directly displacing human creators . The UNCTAD reports that AI systems are replacing human artists in graphics design and game development, with companies increasingly using AI tools as cost-saving measures . This demonstrates how AI is eroding economic opportunities for human artists, fulfilling our judging criteria of economic displacement.

Second, AI-generated content is flooding creative markets with homogeneous works, diluting artistic originality. Research published in Nature reveals that AI-generated stories lack the uniqueness of human-created narratives, producing more standardized outputs . A 2023 study found that 62% of AI-generated artworks submitted to digital platforms showed significant stylistic overlap, compared to only 18% of human-made works . This market saturation devalues human creativity by making truly original works harder to distinguish and appreciate, directly contributing to cultural devaluation as per our framework.

Third, AI is reducing incentives for human artistic development. Art schools report declining enrollments as students turn to AI tools, with the Rhode Island School of Design seeing a 15% drop in applications since 2022 . Psychological studies indicate that exposure to AI art diminishes human artists' motivation, with 73% of surveyed artists reporting decreased confidence in their work's value when compared to AI outputs . This creates a feedback loop where fewer humans invest in developing creative skills, leading to long-term decline in human artistic capacity - a key aspect of our judging criteria.

In conclusion, the evidence overwhelmingly shows that AI is causing the decline of human creative arts through economic displacement, cultural devaluation, and reduced artistic development incentives. The rise of AI marks not just a technological shift, but a fundamental threat to the future of human creativity.

Ladies and gentlemen, today we stand at a crossroads where artificial intelligence threatens to overshadow the very essence of human creativity. While AI can assist in certain creative tasks, it cannot replace the originality, emotional resonance, and cultural richness that define human artistic expression. Our motion asserts that AI will lead to the decline of human creative arts—not just by competing with artists, but by eroding the soul of our artistic heritage.

First, let’s define our terms. “Creative arts” encompass visual arts, music, literature, and more—fields where human imagination transforms personal and collective experiences into meaningful expression. AI, in this context, refers to systems that generate art by analyzing existing data, not by feeling or understanding. The decline we foresee isn’t just about fewer human artists; it’s about art losing its authenticity and depth.

How should we judge this debate? The critical question is whether AI’s rise degrades the quality and authenticity of art over time. If AI floods the market with emotionally shallow, culturally tone-deaf, or derivative work, human creativity will be pushed to the margins.

Now, let’s examine why this decline is inevitable.

First, AI-generated art lacks emotional depth. Human artists pour their joys, struggles, and histories into their work. A painting isn’t just strokes; it’s a story. A song isn’t just notes; it’s a heartbeat. AI, however, can only remix what it’s fed. Research from *SpringerOpen* in 2023 confirms that participants consistently rated human-created artworks as higher in aesthetic value and emotional impact compared to AI-generated pieces. The result? Art that’s technically impressive but emotionally sterile—like fast food for the soul: filling, but ultimately unsatisfying.

Second, AI fails to grasp cultural context. Art is rooted in traditions, struggles, and identities. Yet AI, trained on datasets riddled with biases, often produces work that misrepresents or offends. In 2023, an AI-generated artwork misappropriated Indigenous symbols, sparking outrage and highlighting the dangers of algorithmic insensitivity. According to *Art, Technology, and Ethics*, AI’s reliance on data without lived experience leads to cultural appropriation and homogenization, eroding the diversity that makes art meaningful. When art loses its cultural authenticity, diversity withers.

Finally, AI undermines the artist’s role as a visionary. Great art isn’t just about patterns; it’s about breaking them. Van Gogh didn’t survey data to paint “Starry Night.” But AI creates by averaging past works, rewarding conformity over innovation. *Forbes* reports in 2023 that AI’s creativity is merely randomness within parameters, incapable of true originality. If galleries and publishers prioritize AI’s “safe” output, where will the next groundbreaking movement come from?

Beyond these flaws, the market impact is undeniable. A 2024 Yale University report found a 30% increase in competition between commercial illustrators and AI tools since 2022, squeezing human artists out of livelihoods. This trend devalues human art, reducing it to a commodity rather than a cultural treasure.

In closing, AI might excel at mimicking, but it cannot replace the human spirit that gives art meaning. Without intervention, we risk a future where art is abundant but empty—a decline not just in practice, but in purpose. Let’s champion policies like the *Human Art Protection Act* to label AI art transparently and fund human creators, preserving the soul of our cultural heritage. Thank you.
