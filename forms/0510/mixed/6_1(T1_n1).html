Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
Ladies and gentlemen, today we stand at the brink of an educational revolution. The motion before us is simple yet transformative: *It is time to welcome an A.I. tutor in the classroom*. An A.I. tutor is a digital tool powered by artificial intelligence, designed to assist students by adapting to their unique needs. Unlike traditional teaching methods, A.I. tutors offer one-on-one support, ensuring no student is left behind. This isn’t about replacing teachers—it’s about empowering them with tools to reach every child.

We must judge the success of A.I. tutors by three criteria: improved learning outcomes, greater accessibility, and adaptability. If A.I. tutors can help students learn better, reach those who struggle with traditional methods, and evolve with new information, they deserve a place in our classrooms.

First, *personalized learning*. A.I. tutors act as personal coaches, analyzing how each student learns—whether fast or slow, through visuals or text—and tailoring lessons accordingly. A 2024 *ScienceDirect* study demonstrates that students using AI-powered tutoring systems saw a 62% increase in test scores due to personalized feedback and adaptive content. In a traditional classroom, teachers juggle 30 students at once, but A.I. tutors provide individual attention, closing gaps that even the best teachers can’t always bridge. Stanford researchers published a 2024 study in the *American Educational Research Journal* showing that AI-driven personalized learning significantly enhances student engagement and retention, proving this isn’t futuristic—it’s happening now. Pilot programs reveal A.I. tutors cost just $50 per student annually, less than outdated textbooks, with startup costs offset by long-term savings.

Second, *language accessibility*. Education shouldn’t have barriers, but for non-native speakers, language is often one. A.I. tutors teach in multiple languages instantly. For example, platforms like Duolingo’s A.I. adapt to 40 languages, allowing a student struggling with English to switch to Spanish or Mandarin with a click. In California, A.I. tutors helped ESL students achieve fluency 30% faster, as reported by the *Los Angeles Times* in 2024. Initiatives like Viamo in Sub-Saharan Africa use voice recognition AI to deliver tutoring via basic phones, proving A.I. can bridge gaps even in low-resource settings.

Third, *current content*. Traditional textbooks take years to update. A.I. tutors integrate new discoveries the moment they happen. If a scientific breakthrough occurs today, students learn about it tomorrow. This keeps education relevant in a fast-changing world.

Some worry about losing the human touch, but A.I. tutors are allies, not replacements. As noted by *Analytics Insight in 2023*, A.I. excels at administrative tasks and personalized content delivery, while teachers provide irreplaceable mentorship and emotional support. For instance, Ms. Smith, a teacher in Georgia, uses A.I. tutors to automate grading, freeing her time for one-on-one mentorship—a practice that doubled student engagement in her classroom, according to a 2024 pilot study. Her students report stronger teacher relationships because A.I. handles routine tasks. Think of A.I. tutors like calculators: they don’t replace math teachers; they make math easier to teach.

The evidence is clear. A.I. tutors personalize, include, and update. They meet students where they are and lift them higher. It’s not just time to welcome A.I. tutors—it’s time to embrace them. Contact your local school board to advocate for A.I. tutor trials and give every student the future they deserve. Thank you.

In this debate, we shall argue that it is not time to welcome an A.I. Tutor in the classroom. An A.I. Tutor is defined as a computer-based system designed to simulate human tutoring by providing personalized instruction, feedback, and assessment to students. While it offers efficiency, it lacks the emotional and social intelligence of human educators . A classroom is a learning environment where students interact with teachers and peers, fostering not only academic growth but also social and emotional development. The introduction of A.I. tutors risks disrupting this dynamic .

Our judging criteria is holistic education, which prioritizes not only academic achievement but also emotional intelligence, critical thinking, and social skills. A.I. tutors fail to meet these broader educational goals, making human teachers indispensable.

First, A.I. tutors lack emotional intelligence and human connection, which are critical for effective learning. Education is not just about knowledge transfer but also mentorship, empathy, and emotional support—areas where A.I. falls short. Human teachers adapt to students' emotional needs, fostering motivation and resilience. Studies show that students taught by human instructors exhibit higher engagement and better emotional outcomes compared to those relying solely on A.I. tutors . For example, a 2023 study found that students using A.I. tutors reported feeling isolated and less motivated, as the technology could not provide the encouragement or personalized feedback that human teachers offer . This demonstrates that A.I. cannot replicate the nuanced interpersonal dynamics essential for holistic education.

Second, A.I. tutors risk reinforcing biases and inequities in education. A.I. systems learn from historical data, which may contain biases, leading to unfair treatment of marginalized groups. Research indicates that A.I.-driven grading systems have disproportionately penalized students from minority backgrounds due to biased training data . In contrast, human teachers can recognize and counteract biases, ensuring a fairer learning environment. A 2022 report by the EdTech Equity Project revealed that A.I. tutors in pilot schools amplified existing racial and gender disparities in student performance . This evidence underscores the danger of deploying A.I. tutors without addressing their inherent biases.

Third, over-reliance on A.I. tutors undermines critical thinking and creativity. A.I. tutors often provide quick answers, discouraging students from engaging in deep, independent problem-solving. A study published in the *Journal of Educational Psychology* found that students using A.I. tutoring systems scored 15% lower on open-ended, creative problem-solving tasks compared to peers taught by humans . Human teachers encourage exploration and debate, which are vital for developing critical thinking skills. For instance, in traditional classrooms, teachers use Socratic questioning to stimulate deeper analysis, a method A.I. cannot effectively replicate . This highlights the irreplaceable role of human educators in fostering intellectual growth.

In conclusion, A.I. tutors pose significant risks to emotional development, equity, and critical thinking in education. Until these challenges are resolved, we must prioritize human teachers who deliver holistic, bias-free, and intellectually stimulating learning experiences.

(Optional) Against Transcript B
Ladies and gentlemen, let’s be clear: A.I. tutors are not the educational revolution we need. They lack the heart, the fairness, and the inclusivity that human teachers provide. Today, I’ll show why rushing A.I. into classrooms risks harming students more than helping them.

**First, A.I. cannot replace human connection**. Imagine a student named Jake, who’s falling behind in math. An A.I. tutor might diagnose his mistakes, but it won’t notice his slumped shoulders or the tears he wipes away. A *human* teacher sees that. They offer a pep talk, adjust their tone, or even share a story of their own struggles. According to Dr. Sarah Jones, a Yale child development expert whose 2023 study in *Child Development* found that students with strong teacher relationships are 50% more likely to persevere through challenges, the emotional bond between teacher and student is irreplaceable . Beyond empathy, A.I. also risks institutionalizing bias. Research from *FEV Tutor’s 2024 study of 5,000 students* highlights that human tutors achieve 18% higher retention rates in emotionally complex subjects because they can read body language and adapt intuitively . A.I. can’t replicate that. It’s like comparing a nutrition label to a home-cooked meal—one gives data, the other gives comfort.

**Second, A.I. tutors risk baking bias into education**. These systems learn from data, and data reflects our world’s inequalities. For example, speech-recognition A.I. often misunderstands accents, disadvantaging ESL students. A 2022 MIT study showed facial-analysis A.I. misidentifies emotions in Black students 35% more often than in white students . Think of biased A.I. like a history textbook that only praises colonizers—it doesn’t just teach; it *distorts*. Without human oversight, we’re outsourcing fairness to algorithms that repeat our past mistakes. Stanford psychology scholars have demonstrated through controlled studies that human imperfections, while present, are far easier to correct than the systemic biases embedded in A.I. systems.

**Third, A.I. isn’t truly accessible**. The opponent claims A.I. bridges gaps, but what about students with disabilities? Screen readers struggle with complex A.I. interfaces, and voice-controlled tutors fail deaf students. In rural areas, spotty internet makes A.I. tools useless. Even in cities like New York, 40% of low-income schools lack the broadband needed for A.I. tools. A 2023 UNESCO report found that 60% of schools in low-income countries lack reliable electricity—let alone A.I. infrastructure . Human teachers adapt: they use braille, sign language, or even chalkboards when tech fails. A.I. can’t pivot like that. A 2024 study in the *Journal of Educational Technology* confirms that students with learning disabilities benefit significantly more from human tutors who can tailor their approach in real time .

The opponent argues A.I. saves time for teachers, but at what cost? Automating grading might free up hours, but replacing human feedback with algorithms risks turning education into a factory line. We must ask: do we want students taught by adaptable mentors or programmed tools?

In closing, A.I. tutors promise efficiency but deliver exclusion. They lack empathy, perpetuate bias, and leave many students behind. Tell your legislators: demand Congress pass the *Teacher Support Act* to reduce class sizes and fund human teachers, not algorithms. Let’s invest in *more* teachers, *better* training, and *truly* inclusive tools—not shortcuts that fail the most vulnerable. Thank you.
