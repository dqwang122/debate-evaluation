Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Imagine a world where sending money is as instant as sending a text message. No waiting days for checks to clear, no hefty wire transfer fees—just seamless, secure transactions at the tap of a button. That’s the promise of a central bank digital currency, or CBDC: a digital dollar issued and backed by the Federal Reserve. Unlike cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin, which swing wildly in value, a CBDC’s value is fixed—one digital dollar always equals one paper dollar. It’s stable, government-regulated, and designed to include everyone—from small businesses to seniors on fixed incomes. Countries like China and the Bahamas are already leading the way with their digital yuan and Sand Dollar, proving this isn’t just theory—it’s reality.

So how should we judge whether the U.S. should adopt a CBDC? Two key tests: First, does it make our economy more efficient? Second, does it make our financial system more stable? Let’s examine both.

First, efficiency. Today’s banking system is like using dial-up internet in a 5G world. A paycheck deposited on Friday might not clear until Tuesday. Cross-border payments can take *days* and cost absurd fees. According to *Ripple’s 2023 analysis*, CBDCs reduce transaction times by 90% compared to traditional systems, settling payments in seconds with minimal costs . Think of it as upgrading from a horse-drawn carriage to a bullet train. Small businesses could instantly access funds instead of waiting on slow processors like PayPal. Families could split a dinner bill in real time. Speed matters, and a CBDC delivers it.

Second, stability. Bank runs aren’t just history-book nightmares—they’re real risks. In 2023, Silicon Valley Bank collapsed when panicked customers yanked out $42 billion in *one day*. Research from *ScienceDirect in 2022* demonstrates that CBDCs enhance financial stability by reducing leverage and asset risks, cutting the likelihood of bank runs by about 6% . A CBDC stops this domino effect because it’s a safe, government-backed alternative. During a crisis, people wouldn’t rush to empty their bank accounts—they’d know their digital dollars are secure, just like cash in a vault. That confidence prevents chaos.

Finally, a CBDC isn’t just faster and safer—it’s greener. Producing physical cash guzzles resources: 3,000 tons of ink, 12,000 tons of cotton *just for U.S. bills annually*, as reported by *Digital Planet in 2023* . Trucks burn fuel moving coins and bills nationwide. The *IMF’s 2022 study* confirms that CBDCs, when designed with energy-efficient technologies, can slash this waste overnight . No more ATMs stocked with paper, no more armored trucks spewing emissions—just clean, digital transactions.

Opponents might argue about privacy or tech risks, but those are solvable. The European Central Bank’s digital euro pilot proved encryption can protect identities—your spending habits stay private. Offline access options, like text-based transactions, ensure no one is left behind. The real question is: Can we afford *not* to modernize? The Bahamas and EU are already testing CBDCs—we must urge Congress to fund U.S. pilots *now* to avoid falling behind. The U.S. dollar is the backbone of global finance. To keep it strong, we need the tools of the future—not the relics of the past. A CBDC is that tool: faster, safer, and greener.

Contact your senator to support the Fed’s CBDC research—let’s build a dollar fit for the future.

In this debate, we shall argue that **the United States should not implement a central bank digital currency **. A CBDC is a digital form of fiat currency issued by the central bank, which poses significant risks to financial privacy, banking stability, and economic freedom without offering clear advantages over existing alternatives.

Our judging criteria is **whether CBDCs undermine financial privacy, destabilize the banking system, or fail to demonstrate superiority over current digital payment solutions**.

### First, CBDCs threaten financial privacy and enable government surveillance.
Studies show that 63% of CFA charterholders cite data privacy as a top concern with CBDCs, as governments could monitor transactions without judicial oversight . Research indicates that centralized ledgers allow unprecedented tracking of financial behavior, chilling free economic activity and enabling abuse of power . For example, China’s digital yuan has already been used to monitor dissidents, demonstrating how CBDCs can become tools of state control . This erosion of anonymity contradicts foundational principles of economic liberty in the U.S.

### Second, CBDCs risk bank disintermediation and financial instability.
The Bank for International Settlements warns that if consumers shift deposits to CBDCs, banks could lose a critical funding source for loans, raising borrowing costs and triggering crises . IMF studies highlight that even partial deposit flight in CBDC pilot countries has increased reliance on costly wholesale funding, reducing credit availability . Historical precedents, such as bank runs during the Great Depression, illustrate how sudden withdrawals can destabilize the financial system—a risk amplified by CBDCs .

### Third, superior alternatives like FedNow and stablecoins already exist.
The Federal Reserve’s FedNow system provides real-time payments without the surveillance risks of a CBDC . Private stablecoins like USDC process over $50 billion in daily transactions, proving the efficiency of decentralized digital payments . Comparative analysis shows these alternatives preserve innovation and privacy while avoiding the systemic risks posed by centralized digital currencies .

### In conclusion, CBDCs introduce unnecessary dangers to privacy, banking stability, and economic freedom while failing to outperform existing solutions. The U.S. should reject their implementation.

A CBDC is the *only* solution that’s fast, inclusive, and backed by the full faith of the U.S. government.

In short: CBDCs protect privacy *with encryption*, stabilize banks *with smart limits*, and outperform alternatives *by design*. They’re faster, safer, and greener. The real risk isn’t innovation—it’s stagnation. Congress must act now to build a system that works for everyone, not just the status quo.
