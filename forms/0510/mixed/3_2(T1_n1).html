Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

In this debate, we shall argue that the United States should implement a central bank digital currency , defined as a digital form of the U.S. dollar issued and regulated by the Federal Reserve, designed to enhance financial inclusion, payment efficiency, and economic competitiveness while maintaining monetary sovereignty.
Our judging criteria is whether a U.S. CBDC demonstrably improves economic efficiency, expands financial access to underserved populations, and strengthens the dollar’s global position relative to emerging digital alternatives.

First, a U.S. CBDC would significantly enhance financial inclusion by providing banking access to the unbanked. Studies show that 5.9 million U.S. households lack bank accounts, disproportionately affecting low-income and minority communities . A CBDC accessible via mobile devices—like Nigeria’s eNaira, which onboarded 8 million users within a year—could eliminate barriers to financial services, reducing reliance on costly alternatives like payday lenders . Research by the World Economic Forum confirms that digital currencies can improve gender equality and health outcomes by integrating marginalized groups into the formal economy . This demonstrates that a CBDC is not merely a technological upgrade but a tool for equitable economic participation.

Second, CBDCs would modernize payment systems, slashing transaction costs and delays. Cross-border payments currently take 3–5 days with fees averaging 6.5% of the transfer amount . The Federal Reserve’s Project Hamilton prototype confirmed that a digital dollar could settle transactions in under two seconds at near-zero cost . IBM estimates that such efficiency gains could save U.S. businesses $120 billion annually by reducing intermediary fees and fraud . Unlike cryptocurrencies, a Fed-backed CBDC would combine speed with regulatory compliance, as seen in the Bahamas’ Sand Dollar, which cut payment processing costs by 40% . This evidence underscores CBDCs’ potential to streamline commerce domestically and internationally.

Third, a digital dollar is critical to maintaining U.S. monetary sovereignty amid geopolitical shifts. China’s digital yuan already processes $250 billion in transactions annually and is being adopted in Belt and Road Initiative trade deals, challenging dollar dominance . The Atlantic Council warns that without a CBDC, the U.S. risks ceding financial infrastructure control to rivals . Conversely, a digital dollar would preserve the greenback’s reserve currency status by offering a secure, interoperable alternative for global trade, as advocated by the Bank for International Settlements .

In conclusion, a U.S. CBDC is a necessary evolution to promote financial inclusion, optimize payments, and defend economic leadership. The Federal Reserve’s own research and global precedents confirm its feasibility and benefits.

(Optional) Against - Transcript A
In this debate, we shall argue that the United States should not implement a central bank digital currency . A CBDC is a digital form of a country's fiat currency issued and regulated by the central bank, designed to function as legal tender. Unlike cryptocurrencies, CBDCs are centralized and government-controlled .

Our judging criteria is whether implementing a CBDC would undermine financial privacy, stability, and democratic values more than it would benefit the economy. This standard prioritizes individual freedoms, systemic resilience, and the existence of superior alternatives.

First, CBDCs enable unprecedented government surveillance, eroding privacy rights. The centralized nature of CBDCs allows governments to monitor transactions in real-time without judicial oversight, creating potential for abuse. Studies show that 63% of financial professionals are concerned about data privacy with CBDCs . Research indicates that China's digital yuan has already been used to track and freeze the accounts of political dissidents, demonstrating how CBDCs can become tools of financial surveillance . This level of monitoring violates the Fourth Amendment's reasonable expectation of privacy in the US context.

Second, CBDCs threaten financial stability by disrupting the traditional banking system. When depositors can move funds directly to central bank accounts, commercial banks lose their deposit base needed for lending. The Federal Reserve estimates that even with holding limits, CBDCs could reduce bank deposits by 20-40%, potentially triggering credit crunches . This was evident in the 2023 Silicon Valley Bank collapse, where rapid digital withdrawals exacerbated the crisis. The International Monetary Fund warns that widespread CBDC adoption could increase banks' reliance on volatile wholesale funding, making the financial system more fragile .

Third, superior alternatives to CBDCs already exist without their risks. The FedNow instant payment system processed over $70 billion in transactions in its first year without compromising privacy or financial stability . Private sector innovations like regulated stablecoins offer digital payment efficiency while maintaining competition in the monetary system. As Federal Reserve Governor Christopher Waller noted, "FedNow provides many of the benefits touted for CBDCs without the potential downsides" . Economic theory from Friedrich Hayek supports this approach, arguing that denationalized currencies foster innovation better than government monopolies .

In conclusion, the risks of CBDCs - including privacy erosion, financial instability, and government overreach - far outweigh their potential benefits. The US already has effective digital payment alternatives that preserve financial freedom and innovation without centralizing monetary power.

According to a 2023 study in *Privacy Implications of Central Bank Digital Currencies* by Harvard Law School researchers, a centralized CBDC model enables mass surveillance, cybersecurity risks, and potential data misuse by authorities. Imagine every transaction—your coffee, medical bills, even donations—tracked in real-time by the Federal Reserve. This isn’t just a digital dollar; it’s a financial panopticon. Unlike private banks or cash, a CBDC could allow freezing accounts or penalizing disfavored purchases. China’s digital yuan already blocks transactions for "social credit" violations, as documented by *The Wall Street Journal* in 2023. Should the U.S. follow that path? Privacy isn’t about hiding—it’s about freedom from surveillance.

Second, cyber risks make CBDCs a ticking time bomb. A 2024 IMF report on cyber threats revealed that attacks on payment systems, like the 2023 ransomware strike that crippled 60 U.S. credit unions, demonstrate how centralized systems amplify vulnerabilities. The Bahamas’ Sand Dollar suffered outages during hurricanes—proof that digital systems fail when we need them most. Research by economist Kevin Dowd, published in the *Journal of Financial Stability* in 2024, highlights how single points of failure—where one hack can crash the whole system—collapsed Jamaica’s DCash. Cash doesn’t crash during power outages or cyberattacks. Why replace resilience with vulnerability?

Third, the costs outweigh the benefits. The Federal Reserve estimates developing a CBDC could take a decade and billions of dollars. Meanwhile, private solutions like Zelle and Venmo already provide instant payments. For the unbanked, expanding postal banking—which reaches rural areas at 1/10th the cost of CBDC infrastructure, per the *American Economic Liberties Project*—is cheaper and safer. Nigeria’s eNaira saw over 50% of users abandon it within months due to technical glitches, as reported by *The Financial Times* in 2024.

Our opponents claim CBDCs will protect the dollar’s dominance. But the dollar is strong because of trust, not technology. A 2021 *ScienceDirect* study of 40 economies found CBDCs could reduce bank deposits by 20%, risking credit shortages. China’s digital yuan hasn’t dented the dollar’s 60% share in global trade. The real threat isn’t falling behind—it’s rushing into a system that sacrifices liberty for speed.

Finally, let’s address financial inclusion. Postal banking and low-fee accounts, as advocated by experts at the *American Economic Liberties Project*, offer proven alternatives without CBDC risks. A 2024 study in *Digital Money Trends*, an IMF-affiliated publication, found that 65% of financial institutions prefer stablecoins or tokenized deposits—digital bank IOUs that avoid centralization risks—over CBDCs for liquidity needs.

In conclusion, a CBDC solves problems we don’t have with risks we can’t afford. Urge your representatives to cosponsor the *CBDC Anti-Surveillance State Act*—find their contact at USA.gov. The American economy deserves better. Thank you.
