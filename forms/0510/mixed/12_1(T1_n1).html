Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Question 2: Opening Stage
For Side
(Optional) For - Transcript
Ladies and gentlemen, today we stand in firm support of imposing a fat tax in developed countries. A *fat tax* is an additional charge on unhealthy foods and drinks—those high in saturated fats, sugars, or empty calories . Its goal is clear: to reduce consumption of these products and combat obesity. Importantly, this policy includes safeguards like revenue-funded subsidies for fresh produce to protect low-income groups, ensuring fairness.

How should we judge this policy? Success means three measurable outcomes: lower obesity rates, improved public health, and governments fulfilling their duty to protect citizens. These are not abstract ideals—they are achievable targets backed by evidence.

First, let’s address effectiveness. Unhealthy food is cheap and ubiquitous, driving obesity epidemics. But when prices rise, consumption falls. Research from *Imperial College Business School in 2024* confirms that taxes on high-fat, high-sugar foods reduce sales by 8% to 18%, with the greatest impact among low-income groups. This mirrors the success of Mexico’s soda tax, which cut consumption by 12% in just two years—equivalent to 1 million fewer sugary drinks daily, enough to fill an Olympic pool. Small price changes lead to significant shifts in behavior.

This brings us to government responsibility. Critics argue, "Let people choose!" But when unhealthy choices strain public healthcare—costing $147 billion annually in the U.S. alone—governments have a duty to intervene, just as they do with smoking or pollution. Obesity is not just a personal issue; it’s a systemic crisis. Just as Denmark’s saturated fat tax reduced intake by 4% and saved an estimated 123 lives annually, this policy leverages economic tools to promote health without banning options.

Moreover, the tax protects vulnerable populations. Low-income communities and children are disproportionately targeted by marketing for cheap, unhealthy foods. According to *Dr. Shu Wen Ng, a Distinguished Scholar in Public Health Nutrition at UNC*, combining taxes with subsidies for healthier options—like fruits and vegetables—ensures affordability while discouraging harmful consumption. This dual approach, as seen in Hungary’s "chips tax," reduces health inequalities by addressing both price and access.

Finally, let’s dispel the myth that fat taxes are untested. The *American Heart Association* endorses such measures, citing their proven ability to curb diabetes and heart disease. When Denmark implemented its tax, butter prices rose by 20%, and saturated fat intake dropped. Similarly, Hungary’s tax reduced unhealthy food sales by up to 12%. These are not theoretical models—they are real-world results.

In closing, a fat tax is a practical, evidence-based solution. It respects personal freedom while steering society toward healthier choices. With obesity rates doubling in 30 years, the time to act is now. Let’s tax harm, not health. Thank you.

Output A - Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript A
In this debate, we shall argue that developed countries should not impose a fat tax. A fat tax is defined as a levy on foods and beverages high in unhealthy fats, such as sugary drinks, fast food, and processed snacks, with the purported aim of reducing consumption and combating obesity .

Our judging criteria is whether the fat tax is effective in reducing obesity without creating disproportionate economic and social harms, and whether better alternatives exist. The negative side will demonstrate that fat taxes fail to meet these criteria due to their regressive nature, ineffectiveness in changing eating habits, and the availability of superior alternatives.

First, fat taxes are economically regressive and disproportionately harm low-income families. Studies show that low-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on food, and unhealthy foods are often more affordable. A fat tax exacerbates financial strain on these families without guaranteeing health improvements. For example, research indicates that 80% of Danes did not change their shopping habits despite the tax, and the policy was repealed after just one year due to its economic impact on vulnerable groups . This demonstrates that the tax fails to achieve its health goals while unfairly burdening those least able to afford it.

Second, fat taxes are ineffective in changing long-term eating habits or reducing obesity. Demand for food is relatively inelastic, meaning price increases have minimal impact on consumption. In Denmark, consumers circumvented the tax by shopping across borders or switching to cheaper, equally unhealthy alternatives . Observational studies found no significant reduction in total fat consumption or obesity rates, undermining the tax's rationale . This evidence shows that fat taxes do not address the root causes of obesity and are easily bypassed by consumers.

Third, better alternatives to fat taxes exist, such as subsidies for healthy foods and public education campaigns. Combining taxes with subsidies for fruits and vegetables has been shown to yield better health outcomes by making nutritious foods more affordable . Education campaigns, unlike fat taxes, address behavioral and socioeconomic factors driving obesity without regressive economic effects. For instance, Brazil’s food policies, which emphasize education and access, have demonstrated sustainable improvements in public health . These alternatives are more equitable and effective than narrow fat taxes.

In conclusion, fat taxes are a flawed policy that disproportionately harms low-income families, fails to reduce obesity, and is inferior to alternatives like subsidies and education. Developed countries should reject fat taxes in favor of more comprehensive and equitable solutions.

Output B - Against Side
(Optional) Against Transcript B
Ladies and gentlemen, let’s be clear: a fat tax isn’t just ineffective—it’s a regressive policy that harms the very people it claims to protect. Our opponents define it as a levy on unhealthy foods to combat obesity, but this narrow definition ignores the disproportionate burden it places on low-income families.

Their success metrics—reduced obesity rates and improved health—fail to account for the harsh reality: this tax punishes those who can least afford it. Imagine taxing staple foods like bread or pasta simply because they’re affordable and filling. That’s the essence of a fat tax. Low-income households rely on calorie-dense foods to stretch their budgets. When prices rise, they don’t suddenly switch to organic salads; they either pay more or skip meals altogether. This isn’t speculation—it’s fact. According to a 2023 study by the Danish Health Authority, Denmark’s fat tax led to a 10% increase in food prices for low-income families, yet obesity rates dropped by a negligible 0.5%. To put that into perspective, that 0.5% drop meant just 200 fewer obese Danes—while 500,000 low-income families faced higher bills. The policy was repealed within a year because it failed both economically and health-wise. If the goal is genuine progress, why repeat a policy that has already proven disastrous?

Now, let’s examine the evidence. Our opponents cite studies showing minor declines in junk food sales, but sales data isn’t the same as health outcomes. Where’s the proof that obesity rates actually fall? In Hungary, a "chips tax" reduced snack purchases by 15%, but obesity rates remained unchanged, as reported by the Hungarian National Health Institute in 2022. Mexico’s soda tax cut consumption by 12%, yet obesity rates continued to climb, according to the Mexican Ministry of Health. Taxes don’t educate people about nutrition or make healthier options more accessible. They’re a superficial fix for systemic problems.

Beyond harming families economically, the fat tax also fails its core health mission. Research from the *Journal of Social Policy* in 2023 reveals that such taxes often lead to substitution effects—people simply switch to other unhealthy, untaxed foods rather than adopting healthier diets. This undermines the entire premise of the policy. Even with subsidies, Denmark’s tax still hurt budgets—because fresh produce can’t replace calorie-dense staples for stretched families.

And let’s not overlook the broader principle at stake: personal freedom. Smoking bans protect others from secondhand smoke, but your dietary choices harm no one but yourself. If personal health costs warrant taxes, should we tax all risky choices—from skiing to sugary coffee? If governments can tax fries today, what’s next—a couch tax for sedentary lifestyles? Personal autonomy matters.

There are better solutions. Subsidizing fresh produce, as seen in a 2022 USDA pilot program, increased consumption by 15% in low-income areas. Funding nutrition education and expanding access to grocery stores in food deserts are proven strategies. A fat tax, however, is unfair, unproven, and unnecessary.

The fat tax is a recipe for failure—one that punishes the poor, ignores evidence, and invades your kitchen. Let’s demand policies that help, not harm. Thank you.
