Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

When healthcare is scarce, the government must step in to ration care based on priority, not privilege. This isn’t about playing God—it’s about saving lives fairly. Let’s break this down.

First, what do we mean by “priority-based allocation”? It’s a system where the government distributes healthcare resources using transparent, ethical rules—like medical urgency or survivability—not wealth or connections. Take organ transplants: patients are ranked by need and compatibility, not their bank accounts. This ensures everyone gets a fair shot, and no one cuts the line unfairly. Global studies show that rationing frameworks, like those used in organ transplants, ensure resources are distributed equitably and rationally. Without them, the wealthy monopolize care, leaving others to suffer.

Now, how do we judge this proposal? The standard is clear: *Does it save the most lives while ensuring fairness and preventing systemic collapse?* Here’s why it does.

**First, priority-based allocation ensures fairness**. Without government oversight, healthcare becomes a luxury. During COVID, wealthy nations hoarded vaccines while low-income countries waited. But when governments distribute based on need—like prioritizing frontline workers or the elderly—more lives are saved equitably. Research from *ScienceDirect in 2020* confirms that transparent allocation protocols minimize bias, ensuring decisions are based on objective criteria, not money or influence. Scarcity demands rules, not chaos.

**Second, it prevents system overload**. In emergencies, hospitals use triage to prioritize critical cases. Why? Because if everyone gets equal attention, the system collapses, and *more* people die. Priority-based allocation isn’t cruel—it’s pragmatic. During flu surges, hospitals redirect resources to ICU beds. Without this, chaos reigns. A *2022 analysis by PMC* of pandemic rationing shows that without structured allocation, healthcare systems face catastrophic overload, worsening outcomes for all.

**Third, it reduces bias**. Doctors are human; they might favor patients they like or those who can pay. Centralized rules remove that temptation. For example, kidney transplants follow strict criteria, not a doctor’s gut feeling. Objectivity saves lives. Norway’s 2016 healthcare reforms, as reported in *Public Participation Studies*, demonstrate how transparent criteria—like health benefit and disease severity—create a fairer system, with 47% of citizens acknowledging improved preparedness.

Some argue that rationing is “playing God.” But isn’t *not* rationing playing favorites? When resources are scarce, *someone* decides. Should it be the government—accountable to the public—or private interests? The choice is clear. Today, wealth plays favorites; our plan replaces dollars with need.

And this isn’t theoretical. After the UK’s NICE guidelines were implemented, 90% of patients received critical care faster because the system prioritized those who needed it most. That’s the power of fair rules.

To those worried, “What if *I’m* deprioritized?”—this plan protects you. Without rules, your spot goes to the highest bidder. With ERs overflowing, we can’t afford to wait.

In closing, priority-based allocation is the fairest way to navigate scarcity. It’s the lifeboat protocol for healthcare: tough, but necessary. Let’s choose the system that saves the most lives, fairly. Demand transparency—your family’s life could depend on it.
