Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

In this debate, we shall argue that developed countries should impose a fat tax, defined as a government levy on foods high in unhealthy fats, sugars, or salt to reduce consumption and improve public health. This policy targets obesity and diet-related chronic diseases prevalent in nations with advanced economies.

Our judging criteria is the effectiveness of a fat tax in achieving public health improvements and economic benefits .

First, fat taxes significantly reduce obesity rates and improve public health. Research indicates that high taxes on unhealthy foods lead to measurable declines in obesity prevalence, particularly when combined with subsidies for healthier options . For instance, Denmark’s fat tax reduced saturated fat consumption by 41.8 grams per person weekly . This demonstrates how fiscal policy can directly alter dietary habits, preventing chronic diseases like diabetes and heart conditions. The evidence clearly supports fat taxes as a tool for achieving better population health outcomes.

Second, fat taxes generate substantial economic benefits by lowering healthcare costs and creating new revenue streams. Studies show that obesity-related health problems cost New York state $6.1 billion annually . A proposed 18% soda tax was projected to raise $404 million in healthcare funding in its first year alone . This revenue can subsidize healthy foods or finance public health campaigns, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of improved health and fiscal efficiency. By reducing the economic burden of preventable diseases, fat taxes align with responsible governance.

Third, fat taxes incentivize the food industry to reformulate products toward healthier options. Behavioral economics research confirms that even small price differences influence consumer choices . When Denmark implemented its fat tax, manufacturers initially responded by reducing saturated fat content in products . This systemic shift complements individual behavior change, demonstrating how fiscal policy can reshape entire markets toward public health goals. The food industry’s adaptability proves that fat taxes can drive innovation without compromising economic viability.

In conclusion, imposing a fat tax in developed countries is a scientifically supported and economically sound strategy to combat obesity, reduce healthcare costs, and promote healthier food environments. The evidence from Denmark, Hungary, and economic models overwhelmingly justifies this intervention.
