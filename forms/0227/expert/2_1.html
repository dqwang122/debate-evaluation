Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

The proposition argues for legally protecting the right to delete traumatic memories, defining them as intrusive and distressing recollections. However, this definition overlooks a crucial point: traumatic memories are not merely emotional responses; they are the factual record of the event itself. Deleting the emotional distress associated with a memory does not erase the factual knowledge embedded within it. This distinction is critical to our argument, as our judging criteria today should be maximizing overall societal well-being and minimizing potential harm. This framework considers both individual autonomy and the broader societal implications of memory deletion, a balanced approach essential given the complex ethical considerations at play. We contend that legal protection for memory deletion is detrimental to society on three grounds.

First, deleting memories, even partially, hinders an individual's ability to recognize and avoid future dangers. As Suzanne L. Pineles et al. argue in their 2011 article in the *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, avoiding trauma memories or reminders may impede the natural recovery process. Consider a specific example: a woman who chooses to delete the memory of being assaulted in a dimly lit alleyway might unknowingly put herself at risk again by walking down a similar alleyway in the future. The erased memory, containing crucial contextual information, deprives her of the learned caution necessary for self-preservation. This vulnerability extends beyond personal safety. Societal learning from historical traumas, like genocides or systemic discrimination, relies on the preservation of collective memory. Deleting these memories, even if painful, could hinder efforts to prevent future atrocities.

Second, traumatic memories are often vital in legal proceedings. Adam J. Kolber, an expert in law, neuroethics, and memory dampening, notes in his 2006 *Vanderbilt Law Review* article that "traumatic events frequently lead to legal proceedings that rely on memories of those events." Deleting these memories could obstruct investigations and prevent perpetrators from being held accountable. Imagine a scenario where an employee is pressured by their employer to delete memories of workplace harassment to protect the company's reputation. Without the memory, the employee is deprived of the ability to seek justice, and the perpetrator remains free to harm others. Legal protection of memory deletion could severely undermine the justice system by obstructing the pursuit of truth and accountability.

Finally, legal protection for memory deletion opens the door to misuse and coercion. Joseph A. Boscarino and Charles R. Figley, experts in health research and disaster mental health, respectively, argue in their 2009 article in the *Journal of Nervous and Mental Diseases* that repressing traumatic memories can lead to adverse health outcomes. This repressed emotional state, coupled with the potential for coercion, creates a dangerous combination. Consider the potential for exploitation: individuals could be pressured to delete memories of corporate negligence, medical malpractice, or political corruption, shielding powerful entities from accountability. The potential for misuse in such scenarios is immense, highlighting the dangers of enshrining a right to memory deletion into law. Instead of focusing on memory deletion, we should prioritize ethical and evidence-based trauma treatments, such as EMDR and prolonged exposure therapy, that facilitate healing without compromising the integrity of memory. We urge you to oppose legal protection for memory deletion and support policies that promote ethical and evidence-based trauma treatment.

Our opponents accuse us of advocating for the deletion of factual memories, calling it a mischaracterization of their position. This is a misrepresentation of our argument. We are not advocating for forgetting the event itself, but rather against the erasure of the emotional impact of trauma. The crucial distinction lies in the inextricable link between the emotional weight and the factual memory. Severing this connection prevents individuals from learning from their experiences and adapting their future behavior accordingly. Imagine a soldier patrolling a hostile environment. They recall a previous near-fatal explosion caused by stepping on an improvised explosive device. If the fear associated with that memory is erased, they might recklessly patrol the same area, endangering themselves and their comrades. This specific scenario illustrates how the emotional response is not extraneous but integral to the memory, informing future actions and promoting survival.

The comparison of memory modification to other medical procedures, suggesting that ethical guidelines and oversight can mitigate potential misuse, is a flawed analogy. While other medical interventions carry risks, memory modification presents a unique threat to personal identity, the legal system, and societal well-being. Memory forms the very basis of who we are, shaping our self-understanding and our ability to learn from experience. Altering memories has the potential to fundamentally change an individual's identity and their interaction with the world, a level of vulnerability not present in other medical procedures. Furthermore, as legal scholar Adam J. Kolber argues in his 2006 Vanderbilt Law Review article, "Therapeutic Forgetting," the justice system relies heavily on the integrity of memory. Eyewitness testimony, accurate recollections of events, and the ability to recall details crucial for investigations are all dependent on reliable memories. Manipulating these memories, even with benevolent intentions, risks compromising due process and obstructing justice. Existing regulations for procedures like organ donation simply cannot address the complex ethical challenges posed by memory manipulation. Independent review boards, while offering a degree of oversight, cannot fully prevent coercion or address the inherent risk of altering one's sense of self.

While we acknowledge the importance of bodily autonomy, it is not an absolute right. The right to make medical decisions does not extend to actions that could harm others or obstruct justice. Just as we restrict the right to drive under the influence of alcohol to protect public safety, we must consider the societal implications of memory deletion. Protecting trauma survivors is paramount, but not at the expense of public safety or the integrity of the legal system.

Finally, the argument that reducing emotional intensity facilitates learning is fundamentally flawed. The intense emotions associated with trauma, while undeniably painful, are essential for processing the experience and integrating it into one's life narrative. As highlighted in the 2018 article "Memory Matters" published in the British Journal of General Practice, access to positive emotions, even under stress, mediates psychological growth and resilience. While therapies like Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing can be valuable tools for processing trauma by reducing PTSD symptoms, they do not involve deleting the memory itself. This distinction is crucial. The emotional content of a memory, while distressing, is critical for learning, growth, and preventing future harm, both individually and societally. We urge you to oppose any legislation that would legalize memory deletion technologies and instead support policies that promote ethical and evidence-based trauma treatment.
