Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 30 minutes and a $10 will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Today, we're debating whether processed foods should play a larger role in sustainable food systems. Let's be clear: we're not talking about sugary drinks or nutrient-empty snacks. The term "processed foods" encompasses a vast range, and our focus is on specific types of processing that offer demonstrable benefits. Think of everyday examples like canned tomatoes, packaged milk, or fortified cereals. We define "processed foods" as foods altered from their natural state using methods that *enhance* nutritional value, extend shelf life, and minimize environmental impact. This includes fortified foods, foods produced using precision fermentation, and those packaged for extended shelf stability.

Our criterion for judging this debate is straightforward: Which side promotes a food system that minimizes environmental impact, ensures food security, and improves public health? We contend that a greater reliance on strategically developed and responsibly processed foods demonstrably achieves this goal.

Consider how fortified processed foods offer a practical solution to combat malnutrition in underserved populations where access to fresh produce is limited. Food deserts often lack access to fresh, nutrient-rich options, making fortified processed foods a lifeline. By adding essential vitamins and minerals through reformulation of food, we can address critical nutritional deficiencies in these vulnerable communities, improving health outcomes where they are needed most. Think of fortified foods as a form of public health infrastructure, delivering vital nutrients directly to those who need them, much like accessible healthcare.

Beyond nutrition, processed foods designed for longer shelf life minimize spoilage throughout the supply chain, contributing to overall waste reduction. One third of food produced is wasted . By extending shelf life through innovations in packaging, we minimize spoilage, creating a more efficient and environmentally friendly food system. Shelf-stable foods can help reduce food waste as they last longer . We will see examples across cultures and time that relied on preservation and storage of food.

Finally, precision fermentation offers a revolutionary path toward sustainable protein production. Precision fermentation involves using microorganisms to produce protein ingredients in a controlled environment, requiring significantly less land and water compared to raising livestock. In North America, the precision fermentation market is thriving, driven by increasing consumer awareness and demand for sustainable products . Around 40% of U.S. adults, over 90 million individuals, are projected to embrace products made using precision fermentation, with an estimated reach of 132 million consumers .

We anticipate arguments claiming that processed foods are inherently unhealthy. However, we emphasize responsible processing and fortification, not the exclusion of all altered foods. Dismissing all processed foods ignores the potential of sustainably produced, fortified options to address critical nutritional needs and reduce environmental impact. It also ignores the reality of limited food access in many communities. By supporting this motion, you're voting for a future where children have access to the nutrients they need to thrive, where food waste is minimized, and where our planet is protected for future generations. Let's embrace smart processing for a healthier planet and a well-fed world.

Question 3: Rebuttal Stage
For Side
(Optional) For - Transcript
The opposition contends that increasing the role of processed foods harms our environmental and social well-being in the long run. This argument is far too broad and overlooks the potential for innovation within the food processing sector. The central issue is their failure to differentiate between types of "processed foods." They lump everything together, ignoring our clear definition that emphasizes responsibly produced and nutritionally enhanced options.

Let's address their environmental concerns. They claim processed foods undermine sustainability due to increased emissions and resource depletion. However, this argument ignores the significant advancements in processing efficiency that actively reduce environmental impact. Consider precision fermentation, a process that slashes land use by up to 90% compared to traditional animal agriculture . This isn't about ignoring environmental concerns; it's about acknowledging that innovation is creating solutions.

Furthermore, their argument fails to recognize the growing trend of processed food companies incorporating sustainable ingredients and promoting responsible agricultural practices. Many companies are actively sourcing sustainably grown ingredients, driving demand for more environmentally friendly farming methods. It's a market-driven shift towards sustainability that benefits everyone. In fact, agriculture is responsible for approximately 18,000 deaths annually caused by pollution, the vast majority of these deaths are a consequence of ammonia emissions .

Now, let's move to the public health claims. They argue that increased reliance on processed foods worsens public health crises. This argument conveniently ignores the immense potential for fortification and responsible formulation to *improve* nutritional outcomes, especially for vulnerable populations. Processed foods can be powerful vehicles for delivering essential vitamins and minerals to people who lack consistent access to fresh produce. Fortification programs have demonstrated the ability to reduce iron deficiency anemia by as much as 50% in certain populations .

Finally, they argue that prioritizing processed foods diminishes the resilience of local and regional food systems. This argument presents a false choice. Community gardens and local agriculture are undoubtedly valuable, but they face inherent limitations in scalability and geographic reach that processed foods can overcome. It's not an either/or proposition. Integration and collaboration can enhance both local production and efficient distribution. Processed food companies can actively utilize locally sourced ingredients, providing crucial support to regional farmers and economies.

Before concluding, let's consider their judging criteria: maximizing long-term resilience. While a worthy goal, it shouldn't overshadow the immediate benefits that strategically processed foods offer in addressing pressing issues of food security and public health. The nutrient value of food is almost always altered by the kind of processing it undergoes . Are we willing to sacrifice immediate gains in nutrition and accessibility for a hypothetical long-term resilience that may never materialize? Don't let perfect be the enemy of good. We can address the food insecurity and malnutrition today, while working for a better tomorrow.

We urge you to recognize the immense potential of responsibly produced, strategically processed foods to contribute to a more sustainable, equitable, and healthy food system for all.
