Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

We're here today to discuss whether dating expenses *should* be shared equally, and we firmly believe that mandating such a rigid approach is not only impractical but also potentially harmful. We will demonstrate why a strict 50/50 split is an oversimplification that ignores the complexities of real-life relationships. We will address the definition of "shared equally," the potential barriers to participation, the misconception that equal financial contributions guarantee relationship equality, and the clash with cultural norms.

First, let's address the definition of "shared equally." Our opponents likely define it as a strict 50/50 split. However, this definition is far too narrow. Relationships aren't always perfectly symmetrical, and insisting on a rigid 50/50 split simply doesn't reflect the realities of differing financial situations and diverse contributions within a partnership. Instead of a rigid 50/50 split, couples could agree on proportional contributions based on income, alternate who pays for dates, or prioritize non-financial contributions like household chores and emotional support.

Building on this, our second main point is that mandating equal payment ignores the potential for one partner to be a student or unemployed, creating a significant barrier to participation. Imagine a college student working part-time, trying to date someone with a full-time job. Insisting on a 50/50 split immediately puts the student at a disadvantage, potentially making dating inaccessible. It creates a barrier to participation based purely on financial status. As highlighted by *WorkRise Network in 2023*, unemployed individuals are *twice as likely* to experience psychological distress due to financial worries . Imposing such a financial burden on a student or unemployed partner is not only unfair but also potentially detrimental to their mental well-being.

Beyond financial considerations, our third point is that equal financial contributions don't guarantee relationship equality or mutual respect. You can split every bill down the middle and *still* have an unequal relationship. True equality encompasses emotional support, shared decision-making, and simply treating each other with kindness. Our opponents might equate equal financial contribution with 'fairness,' implying it solves all relationship imbalances. However, fairness is subjective and multifaceted. One partner might contribute more financially, while the other offers more emotional support, helps more with household tasks, or takes on more family responsibilities. As financial expert Tu argued in *a 2023 article*, insisting on a 50/50 split when income disparities exist can force the lower-earning partner to live beyond their means, breeding financial resentment . Forcing strict financial equality neglects these intangible contributions and can actually create resentment, undermining the very equality it seeks to achieve.

Furthermore, the concept of strict equal dating expense is not universally accepted and can clash with cultural norms. Different cultures have different expectations around dating and finances. As noted in *a scholarworks.gvsu.edu article*, cultural perspectives significantly influence expectations in relationships . Imposing a Westernized 50/50 expectation can clash with traditional practices and values, potentially causing friction and misunderstandings.

For all of these reasons, we believe that dating expenses should *not* be shared equally. Let's move beyond the simplistic 50/50 model and embrace a more nuanced, compassionate, and sustainable approach to dating expenses – one that prioritizes communication, fairness, and connection.
