Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Good morning, everyone. We're here today to discuss a critical issue: the books our children encounter in schools. To ensure a productive discussion, let's define "inappropriate books." We're talking about books that contain graphic depictions or promote sexual activity, violence, or illegal drug use. This definition applies to students under 18 in publicly funded K-12 schools within the United States. It's important to acknowledge that what constitutes "inappropriate" is determined by community standards and established legal precedents concerning obscenity and age appropriateness.

Our guiding principle is simple: the safety and well-being of our students. The most critical factor is whether these books contribute to a secure and supportive learning environment.

First, schools have a fundamental responsibility to foster a safe learning environment by removing inappropriate books. A safe learning environment is essential for students' well-being and academic success. Removing books containing inappropriate content, such as explicit depictions of violence, drug use, or sexual activity, directly contributes to a safer environment by minimizing exposure to potentially harmful influences. This proactive measure aligns with the school's duty to protect students from elements that could disrupt their learning or negatively impact their development. It's akin to building a fence around a playground – we do it to protect children from potential dangers. Studies by *PMC* show that children exposed to violence may exhibit neurological changes, impacting memory and self-regulation . This underscores the importance of creating a safe environment, as these changes can directly hinder a child's ability to learn and succeed academically.

Second, schools need guidelines to ensure the books are age-appropriate for students. Age appropriateness is critical to education. Schools need a framework to assess if a book's content is suitable for a student's maturity level and developmental stage. Without such guidelines, students may be exposed to material that is confusing, disturbing, or emotionally damaging. Establishing age-appropriate guidelines ensures that the books available in schools align with the cognitive and emotional capabilities of the students, fostering a more effective and supportive learning environment. It's like giving a child a complex calculus problem before they've learned basic arithmetic. It's not only confusing but potentially discouraging. *American University* reported in *2024* a sharp rise in the number of contested books in schools that were considered to be too mature for students .

Finally, schools should shield students from content glorifying self-harm or suicide. Such content can normalize and promote dangerous behaviors. Protecting children helps prevent tragic outcomes. Schools have a moral imperative to shield students from such material, as it can normalize and promote dangerous behaviors. Protecting children from this type of content helps prevent tragic outcomes and reinforces the message that self-harm and suicide are never acceptable solutions. *PMC* reported in *2023* that exposure to content that depicts illegal or psychologically maladaptive behavior, including self-harm, should be minimized, reported, and removed . Shielding students from this content is not censorship, but a necessary step in protecting their mental health and well-being.

In conclusion, by proactively addressing these issues and supporting policies that prioritize student safety through the removal of inappropriate books, we create a better, safer learning environment for our students, which is our paramount goal. I urge you to join us in this crucial endeavor and engage in further discussion and action within your communities. Thank you.

The opposition's arguments miss the central point: safeguarding children within our schools. We will demonstrate why our stance is not censorship, why student safety is paramount, and why their proposed solutions are inadequate.

First, let's dismantle the false dichotomy that banning inappropriate books equates to censorship. This isn't about suppressing ideas; it's about responsible curation of content suitable for young minds. Think of it as protecting a playground from hazards – we aren't banning play, we're ensuring a safe environment. *ACE*, the Association of College and Research Libraries, clarifies in *a 2024 publication, titled "The definition of book banning"*, that book banning involves removing or censoring books due to objections to their content. Our approach is more nuanced; we aim to provide age-appropriate materials, not stifle intellectual exploration.

Second, the opposition argues that banning books disproportionately affects marginalized voices. While we deeply value diverse perspectives, the well-being of students remains our foremost concern. Removing graphically explicit content isn't about silencing voices; it's about acknowledging and respecting age-appropriateness. Diverse content can still be accessible, but with careful consideration of its suitability for young audiences. The key is that the proposed definition of "inappropriate books" focuses on graphic content rather than the identity of the author or the subject matter.

Third, the opposition claims we limit intellectual freedom. However, unrestricted access doesn't automatically equate to understanding or benefit. Shielding children from harmful content isn't limiting their future intellectual growth; it's ensuring their psychological well-being today. According to *a 2013 report by the American Psychological Association*, media's influence on children's psychosocial development is profound, so guidance on age-appropriate use is important . There is a necessity to protect children's psychological development by only teaching them appropriate content.

Fourth, they suggest parental guidance and open discussions are better than bans. While these are valuable tools, they aren't always sufficient. Not all students have supportive parents, and the impact of some content extends beyond a simple discussion. According to *a 2021 study published in JAMA Pediatrics*, exposure to certain content, such as that glorifying self-harm, can normalize these behaviors in teenagers . These approaches should work in conjunction with curating inappropriate content.

Fifth, let's address their judging criteria: upholding intellectual freedom and educational value. We concur, but argue that curating books creates a safer learning environment that ultimately enhances intellectual freedom and educational value. As *the National Education Association* stated in *a 2023 report on school safety*, a safe and secure environment is of the utmost importance for schools, ensuring the well-being of students, teachers, and staff, and playing a crucial role in academic performance and teacher satisfaction . Students cannot learn effectively when they don't feel safe and supported.

Finally, let's reiterate that "inappropriate content" is explicitly defined. For example, *the American Academy of Pediatrics* provides guidelines on media use for children, explaining that materials should align with children's cognitive and motor skill development at different ages . Similarly, schools can establish clear guidelines for age-appropriate content, ensuring that students are exposed to material that is both educational and suitable for their developmental stage.

In summary, our position ensures a safer, more supportive learning environment. Protecting our children's well-being is the foundation of education.
