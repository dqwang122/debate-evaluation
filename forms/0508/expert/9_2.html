Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

Moderate (3)

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

Strong (4)

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In the context of art, this refers to artists incorporating elements—styles, motifs, techniques, etc.—from other cultures into their work. (Optional) Against - Transcript
The motion before us today is whether artists should be free to borrow from cultures other than their own. We stand firmly against this proposition. While cultural exchange can seem positive, the concept of "free borrowing" often masks the potential for harm, exploitation, and the perpetuation of existing inequalities.

First, let's address the critical distinction between cultural appreciation and cultural appropriation. While appreciation involves genuine understanding and respect, appropriation occurs when elements of a non-dominant culture are used in a way that reinforces stereotypes, contributes to oppression, or disrespects their original meaning. As *Verywell Mind reported in 2024*, cultural appropriation often lacks credit to the source and can be deeply offensive . Even well-intentioned cultural borrowing can unintentionally cause harm because artists often lack sufficient understanding of the cultural context and significance of what they're borrowing. For example, a musician might use a sacred melody from an Indigenous culture in a pop song without grasping its spiritual meaning. This isn't about intent; it's about impact. To prevent unintended disrespect and harm, we must be extremely cautious about cultural borrowing.

Second, we must spotlight how "free borrowing" allows dominant cultures to profit from the creativity of marginalized cultures. The freedom to borrow often translates into dominant cultures exploiting the artistic expressions of marginalized communities for economic gain, without fair compensation or recognition. *ScienceDirect* highlights this exploitation, noting that dominant groups often view subordinated cultures as resources to be mined, leading to economic disadvantages for those communities . Consider fashion brands that appropriate Indigenous designs without acknowledging or fairly compensating the communities who originated them. The result is profit for the dominant culture, while the marginalized culture sees its heritage commodified and exploited. This economic exploitation deepens existing power imbalances.

Third, it's crucial to realize that focusing on borrowing distracts from addressing systemic inequalities faced by marginalized artists. The debate over cultural borrowing can divert attention from the more pressing need to address systemic inequalities and lack of opportunities faced by artists from marginalized communities. As discussed in "*Open to Debate*", the focus on cultural appropriation can distract from the real injustices, such as the lack of opportunities and funding for marginalized artists . If a Native American artist struggles to get funding for their traditional art while a non-Native artist profits from Native American-inspired art, is that a fair system? We believe the focus should be on leveling the playing field for marginalized artists, not simply celebrating cultural exchange.

Finally, let's address the argument that responsible cultural borrowing is always beneficial. Proponents of free borrowing might argue that it fosters cultural exchange and artistic innovation. However, research from *PMC in 2023* reveals that cultural borrowing in the fashion industry often raises concerns about cultural misappropriation, lack of recognition and compensation for original cultures, and the perpetuation of power imbalances . Therefore, even seemingly responsible borrowing can perpetuate harm. This requires not only caution in borrowing from other cultures but also proactive measures such as increased funding for Native American arts programs, scholarships for marginalized artists, and policies that ensure fair compensation for cultural intellectual property.
