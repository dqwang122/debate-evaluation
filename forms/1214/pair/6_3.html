Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hour and a $30 Amazon gift card will be sent via email as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

Friends, judges, esteemed colleagues, let's talk about something really important today: the future of young people. We believe that escaping big cities, not returning to them, is the way forward for today’s youth. Now, what do we mean by "escaping"? We're talking about young people building their lives and careers in smaller towns, rural areas, or more affordable suburbs, instead of chasing opportunities *only* in big, expensive cities. We believe this offers a more sustainable and fulfilling path to personal and professional growth.

Our argument rests on three key pillars: affordability, civic engagement, and community connection. Let's start with affordability. Think about trying to buy your first home. In a big city, it can feel like a pipe dream, right? High urban housing costs force many millennials to postpone or even abandon their dreams of owning a home. The numbers tell a stark story: 81% of millennials in rural areas own homes, compared to just 58% in urban areas. That's a huge difference! (Source: [1, 2]) Lower property prices and less competition in smaller towns make homeownership much more attainable for young people looking for an affordable way to get into the housing market. And it's not just lower prices. Financial incentives like USDA loans, available specifically in rural communities, can help millennials achieve homeownership even sooner. (Source: [4])

This brings me to our second point: civic engagement. Imagine your kids' future in this community. Do you want them to have a voice? To make a real difference? Relocating to smaller communities offers young people unique opportunities to get involved and make their voices heard, opportunities they often don't have in large cities. Research shows how important civic engagement is for both personal development and the well-being of the entire community. (Source: [5, 6]) While young people in both urban and rural areas have similar access to information, studies show that rural youth often lack the support they need to turn that information into action. (Source: [7]) Smaller communities offer a much more accessible entry point for young leadership. With fewer people competing for leadership positions, young people can more easily step into roles where they can directly influence local politics and development initiatives. (Source: [8]) This direct involvement gives them a stronger sense of ownership and empowers them to shape their communities.

Finally, let's talk about connection. Think about your morning commute today. Were you surrounded by people, yet feeling completely alone? Studies show a strong link between urban living and decreased mental well-being, including increased social isolation. (Source: [9, 10]) Research highlights the challenges young people face in forming meaningful connections in cities, often due to social isolation and a fast-paced lifestyle. (Source: [9]) Smaller communities offer a completely different social landscape. Existing research emphasizes the increased community involvement and public engagement opportunities available in small towns. (Source: [11, 12]) This increased interaction creates a sense of belonging and helps young people build stronger interpersonal relationships. The close-knit nature of small towns allows for more frequent and meaningful interactions, leading to stronger bonds.

So, what's the takeaway here? Escaping big cities offers young people a real path towards a more affordable, engaged, and connected life. While cities may seem to offer certain advantages, the crushing financial burdens, limited opportunities for civic engagement, and the isolating social environment create significant obstacles to a fulfilling life for many young people. Smaller communities offer not just an escape, but a real opportunity to build a better future. Thank you.

Friends, colleagues, fellow debaters, let’s talk about the future, *our* future, and where we want to build it. My opponents paint a picture of big cities as the land of opportunity, the place to be for young people seeking a better life. They talk about jobs, education, and social mobility. But I ask you, have they considered the *real* cost of this so-called "better" future? Have they factored in the stress, the debt, the sheer exhaustion of city life?

Let’s start with jobs. Yes, big cities have traditionally been job hubs. But things are changing. Think about how many people now work from home, how many businesses are moving outside of major urban centers. The landscape of opportunity is shifting. In fact, the 2023 Census Bureau data shows a clear trend: young people, those aged 25 to 44, are increasingly moving *away* from big cities and towards smaller towns and rural areas. (Source: [1]) This isn’t just a blip; it’s a significant shift that tells us something important: opportunity is no longer confined to concrete jungles.

Next, let’s talk education. My opponents highlight the prestigious universities located in big cities. But let’s be honest, how many of us can afford those sky-high tuition fees? And even if you get in, you’re facing cutthroat competition. Meanwhile, online education and remote learning programs are making quality education accessible no matter where you live. This levels the playing field, making location less of a barrier to a good education. What’s more, smaller communities often offer specialized training programs geared towards local industries, giving you the *exact* skills you need for jobs in your area.

Now, let’s address social mobility. My opponents claim it’s higher in big cities. But consider this: how can you climb the social ladder when you’re drowning in debt? The cost of living in big cities, especially housing, is crippling for young people. It’s like trying to run a marathon with a lead weight strapped to your ankle. You’re stuck in a cycle of debt, struggling just to stay afloat. In smaller towns, lower housing costs and a lower overall cost of living free up resources that you can invest in your future – whether it’s education, starting your own business, or simply building a secure financial foundation.

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, let’s talk about well-being. My opponents seem to forget that a “better” future isn’t just about a bigger paycheck. It’s about living a fulfilling, balanced life. Studies show that young adults in cities experience higher stress levels and more mental health challenges compared to their rural counterparts. (Source: [6, 7, 8]) Think about it: limited access to nature, increased social isolation, the constant pressure cooker of city life. (Source: [2, 3, 5]) Is that really the future we want? In smaller communities, you have more access to nature and outdoor recreation. (Source: [2, 9, 10, 11]) This isn’t just about leisure; it’s about your mental and emotional health. Studies show that spending time in nature reduces stress and improves overall well-being. Smaller towns also offer a healthier work-life balance. Lower living costs (Source: [4]), shorter commutes (Source: [12]), and a slower pace of life (Source: [4]) mean more time for yourself, your family, your community. (Source: [13]) It means stronger social connections, a sense of belonging, a real community.

So, while big cities may have their allure, let’s not be blinded by the bright lights. The costs, the competition, the impact on our well-being – these are real challenges that we can’t ignore. Escaping to smaller communities isn’t about giving up; it’s about choosing a different path, a path that leads to a more affordable, fulfilling, and balanced life. It’s about choosing a brighter future, not just a bigger one.
