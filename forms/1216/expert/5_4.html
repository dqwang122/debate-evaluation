Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hours and a $30 Amazon Gift Card will be sent as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

I know this can be a sensitive topic, but I believe it's a necessary step towards a healthier future for all of us.

Let's be clear about what a fat tax is: it's a small tax added to the price of unhealthy foods and drinks. Think about those sugary sodas, processed snacks, and fast food meals that are so readily available and often so tempting. The goal isn't to punish anyone for enjoying these things occasionally, but to encourage healthier choices and help offset the enormous healthcare costs associated with diet-related diseases.

My argument for a fat tax rests on three key pillars. First, *it actually works*. It demonstrably reduces the consumption of unhealthy products. A comprehensive 2024 review published in *Food Policy* analyzed 20 different studies on these types of taxes. The results were consistent: sales and consumption of taxed foods went down, especially when healthy options were also made more affordable. Think about it – if something becomes a little more expensive, we naturally start to consider alternatives. And the higher the tax, the bigger the change. In Mexico, an 8% tax led to an 18% drop in supermarket sales of these unhealthy items, and in some cases, the reduction was as high as 40%. What's particularly important is that this review found lower-income groups were the *most* responsive to the tax, experiencing the largest drop in consumption. This shows that fat taxes can be a powerful tool in promoting healthier diets, especially for those most vulnerable.

Second, fat taxes generate significant revenue that can be directly reinvested into our communities. Consider Denmark, which projected an annual revenue of €160 million – that's equivalent to roughly $175 million – from its saturated fat tax. By earmarking these funds for public health, we create a sustainable cycle of positive change, tackling the root causes of obesity and improving overall well-being. This isn't about adding another tax; it's about investing in our collective health.

Finally, and perhaps most crucially, fat taxes disproportionately benefit low-income communities, the very people who often suffer the most from diet-related diseases. As that 2024 *Food Policy* study showed, lower-income families are more sensitive to price changes. A fat tax, combined with subsidies for healthier foods, can empower them to make healthier choices without breaking the bank. This isn't about burdening anyone; it's about leveling the playing field and ensuring everyone has access to affordable, nutritious food. It's about breaking the cycle of diet-related illnesses that disproportionately affect these communities.

Let me be clear: this isn't about taking away anyone's choices. It's about creating an environment where healthy choices are the easier choices. It's about investing in the health and well-being of our communities, especially those most in need. A fat tax is a necessary and effective policy intervention. It reduces unhealthy food consumption, generates revenue for vital public health programs, and disproportionately benefits low-income communities. By implementing a fat tax, we invest in a healthier future for all. I urge you to support this motion.

In this debate, we shall argue that a fat tax, defined as a tax or surcharge on foods and beverages considered unhealthy due to high fat, sugar, or salt content, should not be implemented.

Our judging criteria is maximizing societal well-being. This encompasses public health outcomes, economic impact across all income levels, and the preservation of individual liberty. We contend that a fat tax ultimately fails to meet this standard.

First, fat taxes are regressive and disproportionately burden low-income individuals. The USDA's 2020 Consumer Expenditure Survey reveals that households in the lowest income quintile spend a significantly higher proportion of their income on food (34.7%) compared to the highest quintile (10.1%).(1) A fat tax, therefore, extracts a larger percentage of income from those least able to afford it, exacerbating existing economic inequalities and potentially leading to food insecurity. This directly undermines societal well-being by harming a vulnerable population segment. For example, Mexico’s 2014 tax on sugar-sweetened beverages, while showing some reduction in consumption, disproportionately affected low-income households, highlighting the regressive nature of such taxes.(2)

Second, fat taxes are demonstrably ineffective at achieving their intended goal of reducing obesity and improving public health. Denmark's 2011 "fat tax," repealed just 15 months later, serves as a prime example of this failure.(3) A study published in the journal Health Economics found no significant long-term impact on body weight following the tax's implementation, indicating its ineffectiveness in altering dietary habits and health outcomes.(4) Moreover, focusing solely on taxation neglects the multifaceted nature of obesity, which is influenced by factors like access to healthy foods, education, and socio-economic circumstances. A comprehensive approach addressing these root causes would be more beneficial for public health.

Third, fat taxes infringe on individual liberty and personal autonomy. While promoting public health is a legitimate government interest, it should not come at the cost of individual freedom of choice. Individuals should be empowered to make their own dietary decisions, even if those choices are deemed unhealthy by some. The government's role should be to provide education and access to healthy options, not to restrict choices through coercive taxation. Philosopher John Stuart Mill's "harm principle" argues that individual liberty should only be restricted to prevent harm to others, not to prevent self-harm.(5) A fat tax, primarily aimed at influencing individual behavior for purported self-benefit, violates this principle and undermines the fundamental right to autonomy, thus diminishing overall societal well-being.

In conclusion, while the intention behind a fat tax may be noble, its regressive nature, proven ineffectiveness, and infringement on individual liberty demonstrate that it fails to maximize societal well-being and should not be implemented.
