Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hour and a $30 Amazon gift card will be sent via email as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Poor (1)

Claims lack evidence/reasoning or contradictory evidence. Tone is flat/disengaging with no audience awareness.

Weak (2)

Limited evidence with poor organization and inconsistent emotional delivery.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Substantial evidence and clear logic. Engaging language that resonates with audience value. Substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and masterful emotional appeal that creates deep connection. Strongly convince you to support the given position.

In this debate, we shall argue that escaping big cities, defined as relocating from a major metropolitan area to a smaller town, suburb, or rural area, is the optimal "way out," or path towards a better future encompassing improved well-being, financial stability, and personal fulfillment, for young people (individuals aged 18-35).

Our judging criteria is maximizing overall well-being for young people, encompassing mental and physical health, financial security, and community engagement. This framework is appropriate because the motion concerns the best "way out" for young people, implying a focus on their overall quality of life.

First, escaping big cities allows young people to achieve greater financial stability. The high cost of living, particularly housing, in major metropolitan areas presents a significant barrier to financial security for young people. For example, a 2022 report by the National Low Income Housing Coalition found that in no state, including rural areas, could a full-time minimum wage worker afford a modest two-bedroom rental unit. (1) Escaping to smaller towns and rural areas, where the cost of living is generally lower, enables young people to allocate their income more effectively, save for the future, and pursue opportunities for homeownership, all contributing to enhanced financial well-being. The median home price in Q4 2023 in Manhattan, NY was \$1 million, while the median home price in Bend, OR (a popular smaller city destination) was $535,000. (2) This substantial difference in housing costs demonstrates the financial advantages of escaping big cities, freeing up resources for other important life goals.

Second, escaping big cities improves mental and physical well-being. The fast-paced, competitive, and often isolating environment of large urban centers can negatively impact mental health, leading to increased stress, anxiety, and depression. A 2023 study published in the journal "Cities & Health" found a positive correlation between urban density and mental health issues among young adults. (3) Conversely, smaller communities often offer stronger social connections, increased access to nature, and a slower pace of life, all factors that contribute to improved mental and physical well-being. Access to green spaces and outdoor recreational activities, often more readily available in smaller communities, promotes physical activity and reduces stress, as highlighted in a 2021 report by the World Health Organization. (4)

Third, escaping big cities opens up diverse career and entrepreneurial opportunities. While certain industries may be concentrated in major metropolitan areas, the rise of remote work, the growth of the gig economy, and the increasing availability of high-speed internet in smaller towns and rural areas are creating a more geographically diverse job market. A 2024 survey by Upwork found that 26.7% of the American workforce worked remotely. (5) This allows young people to pursue careers in fields of their choice, regardless of location, while enjoying the benefits of a smaller community. Furthermore, escaping big cities can foster entrepreneurship, as lower startup costs and a supportive local environment can encourage young people to launch their own businesses, contributing to both personal fulfillment and local economic development. A 2023 report by the Small Business Administration found that small businesses created 1.9 million net new jobs in 2022. (6) This entrepreneurial spirit can revitalize smaller communities while offering young people a path towards self-sufficiency and career satisfaction.

In conclusion, escaping big cities provides a path towards a better future for young people, offering enhanced financial stability, improved mental and physical well-being, and diverse career and entrepreneurial opportunities. By embracing a life outside the confines of major metropolitan areas, young people can maximize their overall well-being and build a more fulfilling future.

Thank you, chairperson. Esteemed judges, ladies and gentlemen, the opponent's opening statement contains several flaws, specifically their arguments that "escaping big cities allows young people to achieve greater financial stability," that such a move "improves mental and physical well-being," and that it "opens up diverse career and entrepreneurial opportunities." These claims, as I will demonstrate, are fundamentally flawed.

First, the affirmative's claim that "escaping big cities allows young people to achieve greater financial stability" overlooks the crucial aspect of purchasing power. While housing may indeed be less expensive in smaller towns, salaries in major metropolitan areas are significantly higher, often offsetting the higher cost of living. Furthermore, their assertion that "in no state, including rural areas, could a full-time minimum wage worker afford a modest two-bedroom rental unit" is misleading. Minimum wages vary drastically across states, and a proper affordability analysis must consider these regional variations. Moreover, many young people share housing in urban areas, a common and viable option their analysis ignores. Their comparison of Manhattan's million-dollar median home price to Bend, Oregon's $535,000 median price is a disingenuous use of outliers. While Bend is more affordable, the job market and salary potential are drastically different. A software engineer in Bend, OR, averages $98,000, while their New York City counterparts make $133,000 (1). Therefore, simply moving to a smaller town doesn't guarantee financial stability; it presents a different set of financial challenges.

Second, regarding their claim that "escaping big cities improves mental and physical well-being," we acknowledge the benefits of access to nature. However, major cities offer extensive green spaces and recreational facilities. Central Park in New York City and Golden Gate Park in San Francisco, to name just two examples, provide ample opportunities to connect with nature within an urban environment. Additionally, many urban areas promote active lifestyles with walkable neighborhoods, dedicated bike lanes, and robust public transportation systems. The affirmative's citation of a 2023 study in "Cities & Health" linking urban density and mental health issues lacks crucial context. This correlation often fails to control for other significant contributing factors, such as socioeconomic disparities and access to mental healthcare (2). For example, Peen, Schoevers, Beekman, and Dekker (2017) found that socioeconomic status significantly mediated the relationship between urbanicity and mental health outcomes. Furthermore, research suggests that the diversity and cultural richness of urban environments can be stimulating and contribute positively to personal growth among young people (3). Specifically, Florida's (2002) work on the creative class highlights how urban centers attract and retain young talent due to their diverse and vibrant social environments.

Third, concerning the assertion that "escaping big cities opens up diverse career and entrepreneurial opportunities," we agree that remote work is on the rise. However, the affirmative oversimplifies this trend. Many high-paying, specialized roles, particularly in fields like finance and technology, still require a physical presence in major metropolitan hubs. Their portrayal of the gig economy as a path to financial stability is equally misleading. Gig work often lacks benefits, job security, and consistent income, potentially hindering long-term financial planning. While acknowledging that starting a business might be cheaper in a small town, we contend that the affirmative underestimates the critical importance of resources readily available in large cities. Access to venture capital, mentorship networks, and a skilled workforce is significantly greater in urban areas, all of which are essential for scaling a business. The vibrant startup ecosystems, incubators, and accelerators concentrated in major cities offer unparalleled support and resources that drastically improve the chances of entrepreneurial success.

In conclusion, the affirmative's idyllic portrayal of escaping big cities fails to account for the complex realities faced by young people. While smaller towns offer certain advantages, the dynamic environment, robust job markets, and concentrated resources of big cities are crucial for career advancement, financial stability, and access to diverse opportunities. The negative's arguments, supported by evidence, demonstrate that remaining in or returning to major metropolitan areas offers young people the best path towards achieving their full potential and building a successful future.
