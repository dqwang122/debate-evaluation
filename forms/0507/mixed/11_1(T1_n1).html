Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
Good morning, everyone. We're here today to discuss a critical issue: the books our children encounter in schools. To ensure a productive discussion, let's define "inappropriate books." We're talking about books that contain graphic depictions or promote sexual activity, violence, or illegal drug use. This definition applies to students under 18 in publicly funded K-12 schools within the United States. It's important to acknowledge that what constitutes "inappropriate" is determined by community standards and established legal precedents concerning obscenity and age appropriateness.

Our guiding principle is simple: the safety and well-being of our students. The most critical factor is whether these books contribute to a secure and supportive learning environment.

First, schools have a fundamental responsibility to foster a safe learning environment by removing inappropriate books. A safe learning environment is essential for students' well-being and academic success. Removing books containing inappropriate content, such as explicit depictions of violence, drug use, or sexual activity, directly contributes to a safer environment by minimizing exposure to potentially harmful influences. This proactive measure aligns with the school's duty to protect students from elements that could disrupt their learning or negatively impact their development. It's akin to building a fence around a playground – we do it to protect children from potential dangers. Studies by *PMC* show that children exposed to violence may exhibit neurological changes, impacting memory and self-regulation . This underscores the importance of creating a safe environment, as these changes can directly hinder a child's ability to learn and succeed academically.

Second, schools need guidelines to ensure the books are age-appropriate for students. Age appropriateness is critical to education. Schools need a framework to assess if a book's content is suitable for a student's maturity level and developmental stage. Without such guidelines, students may be exposed to material that is confusing, disturbing, or emotionally damaging. Establishing age-appropriate guidelines ensures that the books available in schools align with the cognitive and emotional capabilities of the students, fostering a more effective and supportive learning environment. It's like giving a child a complex calculus problem before they've learned basic arithmetic. It's not only confusing but potentially discouraging. *American University* reported in *2024* a sharp rise in the number of contested books in schools that were considered to be too mature for students .

Finally, schools should shield students from content glorifying self-harm or suicide. Such content can normalize and promote dangerous behaviors. Protecting children helps prevent tragic outcomes. Schools have a moral imperative to shield students from such material, as it can normalize and promote dangerous behaviors. Protecting children from this type of content helps prevent tragic outcomes and reinforces the message that self-harm and suicide are never acceptable solutions. *PMC* reported in *2023* that exposure to content that depicts illegal or psychologically maladaptive behavior, including self-harm, should be minimized, reported, and removed . Shielding students from this content is not censorship, but a necessary step in protecting their mental health and well-being.

In conclusion, by proactively addressing these issues and supporting policies that prioritize student safety through the removal of inappropriate books, we create a better, safer learning environment for our students, which is our paramount goal. I urge you to join us in this crucial endeavor and engage in further discussion and action within your communities. Thank you.

Good morning, everyone. We're here today to discuss banning certain books in schools. My opponent defines "inappropriate books" as those with graphic depictions of sex, violence, or drug use, judged by community standards. But this definition is far too broad. What one person considers "graphic," another might see as a realistic portrayal of the world. Community standards also vary widely, leading to inconsistent and potentially biased censorship. We need a clearer, more consistent approach than simply labeling books as "inappropriate" based on subjective criteria.

Our goal in this debate is to maximize education for all students. We believe that an open and inclusive learning environment best achieves this. The most critical factor is whether a policy supports intellectual freedom and prepares students for the complexities of the real world. Banning books, as my opponent suggests, directly undermines these objectives.

First, banning books severely limits students’ freedom to explore different ideas and perspectives. It restricts intellectual curiosity, hindering their ability to think critically and form their own opinions. As Sonya Douglass, a Professor of Education Leadership at Teachers College, Columbia University, argued in 2023, book bans ultimately diminish the quality of education and limit exposure to diverse perspectives . My opponent suggests that banning books creates a safer environment, but true safety comes from education, not censorship. Banning books might seem like a quick fix, but it ultimately leaves students unprepared to navigate the complexities of the world and engage with difficult issues responsibly. Shielding students from difficult topics prevents them from developing the critical thinking skills necessary to navigate the complexities of the real world. Removing books that deal with difficult topics limits students' ability to engage with diverse perspectives and develop critical thinking skills.

Second, teachers are trained professionals who can select appropriate materials for their students. Trusting educators' expertise in curriculum development and age-appropriate content allows for flexible and responsive learning experiences tailored to specific student needs. The National Coalition against Censorship stated in 2021 that even books or materials that many find ‘objectionable’ may have educational value, and the decision about what to use in the classroom should be based on professional judgments and standards, not individual preferences .

Finally, banning books does not address the root causes of societal problems like violence and drug use. Instead of banning books, which is a superficial solution, schools should invest in comprehensive mental health programs and resources. Removing books from libraries shuts down vital conversations and reinforces stigma around difficult topics, making it harder for students to seek help and support. As The White House reported in 2012, effective community-based drug prevention should be comprehensive in scope and include a combination of evidence-based interventions in various settings, community-wide public awareness activities, and sound public health and public safety policies .

In conclusion, I urge you to join me in opposing book bans in our schools. Speak out at school board meetings, contact your elected officials, and support organizations that champion intellectual freedom. Together, we can ensure that all students have access to the diverse perspectives they need to succeed in the real world. Thank you.

Censorship undermines the educational process by preventing students from engaging with challenging ideas and developing critical thinking skills. Research indicates that intellectual freedom, encompassing the freedom to read, express, and know, is vital for a well-rounded education . Opponents of book bans argue that restricting information and discouraging freedom of thought undermine one of the primary functions of education: teaching students how to think for themselves . By shielding students from controversial topics, we hinder their ability to navigate the complexities of the real world and prepare them for informed participation in a democratic society. Therefore, banning books undermines the core educational mission.

Second, book bans disproportionately affect marginalized voices and perspectives. Challenges to books often target works by or about people of color, LGBTQ+ individuals, and other underrepresented groups, further marginalizing these communities . This creates a biased learning environment where certain voices are silenced and students are denied the opportunity to learn about diverse experiences and perspectives. For example, challenges to books often cite violence or religious viewpoints , demonstrating a tendency to suppress content that challenges dominant norms. This censorship not only harms marginalized students by erasing their experiences but also deprives all students of the opportunity to develop empathy and understanding for different cultures and identities. Thus, banning these books perpetuates harmful biases and undermines efforts to create inclusive and equitable schools.

Third, alternative approaches, such as parental guidance and age-appropriate discussions, are more effective than outright bans. Open dialogue and critical evaluation of materials, guided by parents and educators, foster media literacy and critical thinking skills. Instead of shielding students from potentially sensitive content, we should empower them to engage with it critically and responsibly. These approaches teach students how to analyze information, identify bias, and form their own informed opinions . According to the American Library Association, challenges to books often cite concerns about violence or religious viewpoints . Instead of banning books, schools can create diverse, inclusive curricula and promote open discussions about challenging topics. By fostering critical thinking and media literacy, we equip students with the tools they need to navigate complex issues and make informed decisions.

In conclusion, we stand against the banning of "inappropriate" books in schools. Such bans limit intellectual freedom, disproportionately affect marginalized voices, and are less effective than alternative approaches. We urge you to uphold intellectual freedom and educational value by rejecting this motion.
