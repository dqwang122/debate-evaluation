Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

For Side
(Optional) For - Transcript
In this debate, we shall argue that Congress should abolish the debt ceiling, a legislative limit on the amount of national debt the U.S. Treasury can incur. We define "abolish" as formally putting an end to the system, practice, or institution of the debt ceiling.

Our judging criteria is the promotion of economic stability and efficient governance. We contend that the debt ceiling, while intended to promote fiscal responsibility, has repeatedly created economic instability and political gridlock. Abolishing it will lead to a more stable economic environment where the government can reliably meet its obligations and focus on long-term economic growth.

First, the debt ceiling creates unnecessary economic instability and uncertainty. The debt ceiling has repeatedly led to political standoffs and threats of default, which negatively impact financial markets and the overall economy. For instance, the 2011 debt ceiling crisis led to a downgrade of the U.S. credit rating by Standard & Poor's, resulting in increased borrowing costs for the government and businesses . A report by the Government Accountability Office found that the 2011 debt ceiling impasse resulted in billions of dollars in increased borrowing costs . Eliminating the debt ceiling would remove this recurring source of instability and prevent future economic damage. According to a CNN article in June 2023, reforms that make future standoffs less risky would be a "positive" from a credit ratings perspective . By removing the debt ceiling, we are actively securing the U.S. credit rating and avoiding needless economic damage.

Second, the debt ceiling does not effectively control government spending. The debt ceiling is a poor tool for fiscal discipline, as it only addresses the *result* of spending decisions, not the decisions themselves. Congress still has the power to set the budget, and the debt ceiling simply creates a potential crisis when the government needs to pay its bills. The debt ceiling has not achieved its stated goal of imposing fiscal discipline on Congress. The statutory debt ceiling is a completely arbitrary value, there has never been any economic justification for any of its historical values . A New York Times article argues that the debt ceiling has not served a useful purpose in living memory . Congress can implement other fiscal restraints that provide more direct oversight and impact on fiscal policy.

Third, abolishing the debt ceiling would streamline government operations and reduce political gridlock. The debt ceiling process consumes significant time and resources, diverting attention from more pressing economic issues. It also provides opportunities for political posturing and gridlock, hindering the government's ability to address important challenges. Louise Sheiner, in her testimony to the House Budget Committee, argued that the debt ceiling distracts from real, tangible economic challenges . Freeing Congress from these manufactured crises would allow them to focus on actual economic policies that bolster the economy.

In conclusion, the debt ceiling causes unnecessary economic instability, fails to control government spending, and fosters political gridlock. Abolishing the debt ceiling will promote economic stability, improve government efficiency, and allow policymakers to focus on addressing real economic challenges. For these reasons, we strongly affirm the resolution: Congress should abolish the debt ceiling.

Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript
We stand firmly against the resolution that Congress should abolish the debt ceiling. While our opponents may argue that this promotes economic stability, we contend it will foster fiscal irresponsibility and long-term economic harm. Let's delve into why maintaining the debt ceiling is crucial for fiscal responsibility.

First, abolishing the debt ceiling eliminates a crucial check on government spending, potentially leading to uncontrolled debt accumulation. Our opponents might suggest that budget resolutions and spending caps are effective substitutes, but we disagree. The debt ceiling compels Congress to periodically review and authorize further borrowing, providing a vital opportunity to scrutinize spending levels. Removing it eradicates this built-in accountability measure. According to *the Baker Institute*, these measures have proven ineffective, stating that "neither the BCA nor the debt ceiling limit Congress’s ability or will to spend money that is financed with debt." Without the debt ceiling, Congress has a green light to spend without constraint, burdening future generations with unsustainable debt.

Second, eliminating the debt ceiling *will* erode investor confidence in U.S. debt, potentially increasing borrowing costs. Our opponents might say that the debt ceiling is an outdated mechanism, but we believe it *is* a vital signal of fiscal discipline. As *the World Economic Forum* points out, only a handful of countries employ a debt ceiling to "signal fiscal discipline to their lenders and investors." Removing this signal could be interpreted as a lack of commitment to managing debt responsibly, prompting investors to demand higher returns for lending to the U.S. government. This isn't just about abstract economic theory; higher borrowing costs translate directly into higher interest rates for average Americans – on mortgages, car loans, and credit cards.

Third, the debt ceiling, despite its flaws, forces Congress to prioritize essential government services over discretionary spending. While our opponents may claim that the debt ceiling leads to political gridlock, we maintain that it is a necessary tool for fiscal prioritization. The need to raise the debt ceiling compels lawmakers to consider which government programs are most critical and ensure they are adequately funded before allocating funds to less essential areas. In 2011, *Republicans in Congress used the debt ceiling as leverage for deficit reduction* because of the lack of Congressional normal order for fiscal year budget votes. While this can lead to political brinkmanship, it also provides a crucial opportunity to reassess spending priorities and ensure that taxpayer dollars are being used effectively.

Now, let's address the opposition's claims directly. They argue that the debt ceiling creates unnecessary economic instability. While it's true that debt ceiling debates can lead to political standoffs, as evidenced by *the 2011 debt ceiling crisis, where the S&P 500 index fell by approximately 17%*, removing it doesn't guarantee smoother fiscal management. It eliminates a critical check on spending, potentially leading to increased debt and economic instability. Political brinkmanship is not solely a result of the debt ceiling; it's inherent in the budgetary process, and removing the debt ceiling won't eliminate it.

They also argue that the debt ceiling does not effectively control government spending. But, while the debt ceiling may not always be effective, its existence introduces uncertainty that compels negotiation and compromise on spending. The budget process alone is insufficient to control debt because it lacks the immediate and direct pressure of the debt ceiling.

For these reasons, we strongly oppose the resolution. Thank you.
