Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

(Optional) For - Transcript
Good morning, everyone. We are here today to discuss why mandatory wage transparency laws are essential to address the persistent gender wage gap. By “mandatory wage transparency laws,” we mean that employers across all sectors and company sizes are required to disclose salary ranges or pay data to their employees and/or the public. This focuses on ensuring equal pay for equal work, regardless of gender, and enabling fairer negotiations and the identification of systemic biases in the labor market.

Our judging criterion today is simple: which side presents the most compelling case for reducing the gender wage gap and promoting fairness in the workplace?

First, wage transparency is a powerful tool for closing the gender wage gap by revealing unfair pay differences. When salaries are open, women can readily see if they are being paid less than their male counterparts for the same work. This visibility empowers them to negotiate for fair pay or file complaints, directly reducing pay inequality. *Forbes* magazine, a leading American business publication, reports that wage gaps and pay inequity have been found to be reduced in organizations that utilize salary transparency . In fact, the gender pay gap could be reduced by up to 40% with broad pay transparency. Consider the story of a woman who discovered she was being paid significantly less than her male colleague, despite having the same qualifications and experience. Armed with this knowledge, she successfully negotiated a salary increase, highlighting the real-world impact of transparency. This isn't just about individual cases; it's about systemic change. Moreover, closing the gender wage gap through transparency not only ensures fairness but also boosts economic growth by unleashing the full potential of our workforce.

Second, wage transparency reduces the need for individual salary negotiations, which are often influenced by bias. With transparent pay structures, salaries are based on objective criteria rather than negotiation skills, which can reduce the impact of gender bias in pay decisions, leading to fairer outcomes for women. When the pay is already made visible, it reduces the impact of any biases during negotiation. Research indicates that pay negotiations are notoriously unfavorable to women. According to a *October 2023* fact sheet from the Society for Human Resource Management, when an employer doesn’t provide job applicants or employees information about the pay for job positions, women lose out . The outdated practice of keeping pay secret allows discrimination and unjustified pay gaps to flourish and keeps employees from trusting they are paid fairly.

Third, wage transparency benefits younger generations entering the workforce. New graduates and younger workers gain valuable information about fair pay, empowering them to negotiate better starting salaries and avoid early-career pay gaps. This ensures they start their careers on a more equitable footing and are less likely to perpetuate existing pay disparities. It helps them better understand the market value of their skills. The salary transparency movement isn’t just a trend; it’s a revolution in how we approach compensation. From entry-level employees to seasoned executives, understanding and leveraging pay transparency empowers you to make informed career decisions.

While some may worry about the administrative burden of wage transparency, many modern payroll systems can easily generate the required reports, and the long-term benefits of a more equitable workforce far outweigh the initial costs.

Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript
In this debate, we shall argue that mandatory wage transparency laws are not the optimal solution for addressing the gender wage gap. We define **Mandatory Wage Transparency Laws** as laws requiring employers to disclose salary information to employees, potential hires, or the public, and the **Gender Wage Gap** as the difference between median earnings of men and women relative to median earnings of men.

Our judging criteria is **maximizing overall economic well-being and individual privacy**. The most desirable policy is the one that leads to the greatest overall improvement in societal welfare, while also respecting individual privacy rights. Mandatory wage transparency, while intending to address inequality, may have negative economic consequences and infringe upon privacy, thus hindering overall well-being.

First, **mandatory wage transparency can decrease overall productivity and morale**. While some argue that transparency boosts morale by fostering trust and fairness, this is not always the case. Transparency can lead to employee resentment if perceived as unfair or if discrepancies are large . Employees may feel distrust if they believe management is hiding pay disparities. Even justifiable reasons for pay differences, such as experience or performance, may not be readily apparent or accepted by all employees, leading to dissatisfaction. As John List notes, when employees know how much other workers at the same level were being paid, they became resentful and demotivated if any of their peers were earning more than them . This can reduce morale and, consequently, productivity, harming the overall economic output of the company and the economy.

Second, **mandatory wage transparency infringes upon individual privacy rights**. Salary information is personal financial data, and mandatory disclosure can expose employees to unwanted scrutiny and potential discrimination . Employees have a right to keep their compensation private. Balancing wage transparency with individual privacy is essential for fair practices . Forcing employees to disclose their salaries can lead to discomfort and stigma among employees.

Third, **alternatives to mandatory wage transparency are more effective and less intrusive**. Solutions such as pay equity audits, blind hiring practices, and promoting flexible work arrangements can address the gender wage gap without the negative consequences of mandatory transparency. Pay equity audits are effective in identifying wage gaps and promoting fair compensation, especially when followed by thorough action . Blind hiring reduces bias and improves diversity in hiring outcomes . Flexible work arrangements can help reduce the gender pay gap by enabling more women to return to work and advance their careers . Furthermore, employer-provided onsite childcare increases the retention of female managers during parental leave by 18.9% . These approaches are less likely to cause resentment, productivity declines, or privacy violations.

In conclusion, mandatory wage transparency laws, while well-intentioned, are not the optimal solution for addressing the gender wage gap. They can decrease productivity and morale, infringe upon individual privacy rights, and are less effective than alternative solutions like pay equity audits, blind hiring, and flexible work arrangements. We advocate for policies that maximize economic well-being and respect individual privacy.
