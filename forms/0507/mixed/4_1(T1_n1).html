Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
Good morning, everyone. We're here today to discuss the crucial role that processed foods should play in sustainable food systems. To ensure a productive conversation, let's define our terms. "Processed foods" encompass any transformation beyond basic cleaning or cutting, including preservation and packaging. A "sustainable food system," as we define it, minimizes environmental harm, is economically sound, and provides nutritional security for all, without jeopardizing future generations.

When evaluating our arguments today, we should prioritize those that demonstrably improve food security and environmental sustainability, especially for our most vulnerable populations.

With that in mind, I will present three key arguments demonstrating why processed foods are not just relevant, but essential, to building sustainable food systems.

First, processed foods significantly enhance food security by extending shelf life and drastically reducing waste. Unlike fresh produce, which spoils rapidly, processed foods offer a longer shelf life. This extended usability reduces spoilage, making food available for longer periods and in more locations. *Food Safety*, a trusted resource on food-related topics, reports that studies show about 50 percent of consumer food waste can be prevented by extending the shelf life of the food product. This is particularly critical in regions with limited access to fresh food or unreliable supply chains. Moreover, *a 2019 study* highlights that wastage of processed fruits and vegetables is approximately 14% lower than that of fresh produce. This demonstrates the power of processing to minimize waste across the supply chain.

Second, processed foods are often more accessible and affordable than fresh produce, especially for individuals with limited time for meal preparation, disabilities that make cooking difficult, or those living in areas with limited transportation options. While we acknowledge concerns about the nutritional profile of some processed foods, it's important to recognize that they provide essential calories and nutrients to individuals who would otherwise struggle to obtain sufficient nourishment. According to *The Annie E. Casey Foundation in 2024*, meals rich in vegetables, fruits, fish, and nuts are, on average, $1.50 more expensive per day than diets rich in processed foods, meats, and refined grains. Furthermore, *WebMD* notes that U.S. food producers have been fortifying foods since the 1920s to prevent nutrition-related illnesses.

Third, processing enables the utilization of agricultural byproducts and waste streams, transforming what would otherwise be discarded into valuable resources. As highlighted in *a 2021 study in PMC*, extrusion processing can be an excellent avenue for the utilization of these by-products in foods.

Therefore, processed foods are not the antithesis of a sustainable food system; they are, in fact, a critical tool for achieving it, and should be considered by individuals, and supported by policymakers.

Output A - Against Side
(Optional) Against - Transcript A
Good morning, everyone. My team and I stand firmly against the proposition that processed foods should play a larger role in sustainable food systems.

To begin, let's define our terms. Our opponents will likely define "processed foods" very broadly, encompassing almost anything beyond basic washing or cutting. While processed foods *can* include things like pasteurized milk, the *core issue* lies in the prevalence of *unhealthy* processed foods – those loaded with added sugars, unhealthy fats, and artificial ingredients – which have a disproportionately negative impact on public health and the environment. While some processed foods are fortified, the overall nutritional profile of a diet heavily reliant on these products is often inferior to a diet rich in fresh, whole foods. Moreover, fortification doesn't address the other negative impacts, such as increased environmental pollution and market distortions. Therefore, for the sake of this debate, we must focus on these *unhealthy* processed foods.

Next, consider the judging criteria. While our opponents will undoubtedly prioritize arguments around boosting food security and environmental sustainability, this completely overlooks the crucial element of *public health*. A truly sustainable food system must support not only the planet and access to food, but also the health of its citizens.

With these points clarified, let's examine why expanding the role of processed foods is a detrimental idea.

First, increased processed food consumption worsens health inequities, especially in low-income communities. These foods are often cheaper and more readily available in these areas, leading to higher rates of obesity, diabetes, and other diet-related diseases. As Dr. Martin Weber, Team Lead for Quality of Care at WHO/Europe, stated in his 2024 report, "The inequality epidemic: low-income teens face higher rates of obesity," highlighting that "the affordability and accessibility of healthy food options are often limited for families with lower incomes, leading to a higher reliance on processed and sugary foods, which can have detrimental effects on adolescent health" . It’s a vicious cycle that reinforces existing health disparities. While processed foods might seem like a solution to food insecurity, they often fail to address underlying issues such as poverty, lack of education, and unequal access to land and resources.

Second, expanding processed food production intensifies environmental pollution. Think about the packaging waste, transportation emissions, and resource-intensive manufacturing. According to *ScienceDirect's 2024 report on Carbon footprints evaluation for sustainable food*, transportation emissions account for almost 43% of total emissions in the food supply chain . The refining, chemical modification, and packaging of processed foods consume vast amounts of energy and water, and generate substantial waste. According to the EPA, in 2019, about 60% of wasted food was sent to landfills .

Third, government subsidies distort the market, favoring commodity crops like corn and soy, which are heavily used in processed foods. This creates an uneven playing field for farmers who grow fruits, vegetables, and other nutritious foods. *An American Enterprise Institute study in 2023* by Chris Edwards argued that the crop insurance program provides farmers with incentives to waste resources through moral hazard .

In conclusion, relying on processed foods is not a sustainable solution. A sustainable food system requires a holistic approach that prioritizes public health and addresses the root causes of food insecurity, reduces the environmental impact, and ensures a level playing field for all producers.

A **sustainable food system** is one that provides food security and nutrition for current generations without compromising the ability of future generations to do the same, being environmentally sound, economically viable, and socially equitable .

Our judging criteria prioritize **holistic sustainability**, meaning the judge should favor the side that best promotes environmental health, human well-being, and the long-term viability of food systems. Arguments favoring processed foods should be rejected if they significantly harm any of these core pillars, even if offering short-term gains in areas like affordability.

First, increasing reliance on processed foods exacerbates environmental degradation. Processed food production relies on resource-intensive agriculture, contributing to deforestation and high greenhouse gas emissions . For example, the production of ultra-processed foods contributes heavily to waste and emissions, undermining environmental sustainability . Studies show that processed food production significantly harms the environment through land degradation and increased food waste . This reliance on intensive farming methods and non-biodegradable packaging further strains our planet's resources . Thus, the environmental costs associated with processed foods outweigh any perceived benefits in a sustainable system.

Second, a greater role for processed foods undermines human health. These foods are often high in sugar, salt, and unhealthy fats, leading to increased rates of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes . Research indicates that ultra-processed foods are particularly concerning due to their association with numerous health issues . Increased consumption of processed foods is linked to higher risks of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes. Specifically, ultra-processed foods are associated with numerous health issues . Therefore, promoting processed foods undermines the goal of creating healthy, resilient populations.

Third, prioritizing processed foods hinders the development of more sustainable alternatives. Focusing on processed foods distracts from investing in regenerative agriculture, local food systems, and plant-based diets, which offer long-term benefits for both the environment and human health . Sustainable food systems promote healthy alternatives to processed foods, emphasizing local, fresh produce . Specifically, these systems reduce environmental impact and support community health by growing and consuming locally sourced foods . Prioritizing processed foods diverts resources and attention from more sustainable and beneficial practices, hindering the transition to a truly sustainable food system.

In conclusion, increasing the role of processed foods in sustainable food systems is counterproductive. It worsens environmental problems, harms human health, and impedes the development of genuinely sustainable alternatives. We urge the judge to oppose this motion and support a future where food systems prioritize health, environment, and long-term viability.
