Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

(Optional) For - Transcript
Good morning, everyone. We stand firmly in support of the motion: It is time to welcome an AI Tutor in the classroom.

To ensure clarity, let's define what we mean by "welcoming an AI Tutor in the classroom." We propose that for K-12 students, ages 5 to 18, regardless of gender or socioeconomic status, schools should begin integrating AI-powered tutoring systems as a regular component of instruction. This integration should supplement and support the role of human teachers, starting in the immediate future – that is, within the next academic year. The primary goal is to enhance personalized learning and improve educational outcomes. It's crucial to emphasize: we are not suggesting replacing teachers. Instead, we advocate providing them with a powerful new tool to help students succeed.

Our guiding principle today is this: Does integrating AI tutors enhance the learning experience and outcomes for students? We firmly believe it does.

First, AI tutors offer personalized learning experiences that adapt to each student's unique pace and style. Unlike traditional classrooms, where instruction is often one-size-fits-all, AI tutors continuously assess a student's understanding and adjust the curriculum accordingly. *Research published in the Journal of Digital Learning and Education in 2024 by Sasikala & Ravichandran* demonstrates that AI-powered platforms effectively customize math and language instruction, leading to improvements in student performance and engagement . Think of it this way: a human tutor can realistically only work with one student effectively at a time. While *Harvard research* suggests that, in specific contexts, students using AI can achieve learning outcomes at a faster pace and with a high degree of satisfaction, when compared to traditional methods, it is important to note that more study is needed to better understand the conditions where AI tutors can have the most positive impact on students' academic careers . This level of personalized attention was previously unattainable at scale.

Second, AI tutors can help bridge the achievement gap by providing personalized support to struggling students. These tools can identify and address learning gaps, providing targeted interventions to help students catch up with their peers. *Sasikala & Ravichandran's 2024 study* highlights AI's ability to identify areas where students struggle, enabling teachers to provide targeted interventions and ensure everyone reaches their full potential . In fact, *a study published in the Journal of Educational Data Mining* revealed that students who used an AI-powered math tutoring system showed a 22% increase in engagement and a 15% increase in motivation compared to students who received traditional instruction .

Third, AI tutors can prepare students for the future workforce by developing their digital literacy and critical thinking skills. *A recent study* found that 91% of educators observe enhanced learning when their students utilize creative AI, attributing this improvement to students engaging more deeply with content through creative thinking activities and real-world projects that are integrated into the curriculum .

While the integration of AI tutors presents numerous opportunities, it's important to acknowledge potential challenges such as cost and data privacy. We propose addressing these concerns through careful planning and policy development.

In conclusion, welcoming AI tutors into the classroom is not about replacing teachers, but about empowering them and providing students with personalized, effective learning experiences. It's time to embrace the future of education.

(Optional) Against - Transcript A
In this debate, we shall argue against the resolution that it is time to welcome an AI Tutor in the classroom. We define an AI Tutor as an artificial intelligence-powered system designed to provide personalized instruction, feedback, and support to students, typically through digital interfaces . We believe the most desirable outcome in education is to foster student well-being and holistic development, encompassing not only academic achievement but also social-emotional skills, critical thinking abilities, and ethical awareness. Therefore, our judging criteria is based on whether AI Tutors truly contribute to student well-being and holistic development.

First, AI Tutors undermine social-emotional development. Relying heavily on AI tutors reduces crucial teacher-student interactions, hindering the development of social skills and emotional intelligence. Walden University noted that relying more and more on AI may reduce the teacher-to-student interactions and relationships and take away from the social-emotional aspects of learning. If those interactions diminish, students’ social skills and interpersonal development will suffer. The human connection in education fosters empathy, communication, and collaboration skills essential for success in life. AI tutors lack the human touch, which is important for student success and well-being . AI tutors cannot replicate these nuanced interactions, which are vital for a student's comprehensive growth.

Second, AI Tutors present significant ethical concerns. The use of AI tutors raises ethical issues related to data privacy, algorithmic bias, and equitable access. AI systems collect and store significant amounts of data about students, including their performance, behavior, and preferences, as highlighted by Paradiso Solutions . Ensuring the privacy and security of this data is paramount. Furthermore, a study by the University of Illinois points out that AI can unintentionally perpetuate biases, which can negatively influence students’ learning experiences and improperly shape their perceptions of the world. These biases can exacerbate inequalities and negatively impact student learning experiences, creating an unfair and potentially harmful educational environment.

Third, human educators and peer learning offer superior alternatives. Human teachers provide personalized guidance, emotional support, and real-time feedback that AI tutors cannot replicate. Physics Sensei emphasizes that AI tutors lack human interaction, emotional support, and real-time feedback. Harvard Independent mentions that AI can't handle real-time feedback. Peer learning fosters collaboration, communication, and critical thinking skills. These alternatives offer a more holistic and effective approach to education, promoting both academic achievement and overall student well-being. Instead of isolating students with AI, we should invest in proven methods that foster a well-rounded education.

In conclusion, we firmly believe that welcoming AI Tutors into the classroom is not the right step forward. The reduction in social-emotional development, significant ethical concerns, and the existence of superior alternatives demonstrate that AI Tutors do not contribute to student well-being and holistic development. Therefore, we urge you to oppose the resolution.

(Optional) Against Transcript B
We appreciate the other side laying out their vision for AI tutors in the classroom. To be clear, we aren't against innovation. However, we believe that a rush to embrace AI tutors overlooks some serious drawbacks that could actually hurt students.

Instead of focusing solely on "enhancing the learning experience and outcomes," as our opponents suggest, we propose a different guiding principle: Do AI tutors promote equitable educational outcomes for all students, regardless of background? Because we believe the question of equity is the most important.

First, AI tutors may perpetuate existing biases present in their training data, leading to unfair educational outcomes. If AI is trained on biased datasets, it can unintentionally discriminate against certain student groups. While the opponent emphasizes guidance capabilities, this guidance is inherently shaped by the data used to train the AI. It's like giving a student a textbook filled with errors and expecting them to learn the truth. Imagine a student from a low-income background whose AI tutor, trained on data primarily from affluent schools, consistently suggests lower-level academic tracks, subtly limiting their future opportunities. As *PMC* pointed out in a 2021 article, existing power structures and biases are embedded into machine-learning models . The opponent praises AI's ability to adapt teaching strategies based on data. However, if the data used to train the AI contains biases, the AI will perpetuate and amplify those biases. The 'improved outcomes' the opponent cites may be skewed by the data used to train these AI tutors, potentially reinforcing existing inequalities. Erin Mote, the co-founder and executive director of InnovateEDU, a national nonprofit, stated in 2024 that AI tools are "as biased as we are because they have been trained on us" . Therefore, we must ask ourselves: are we willing to amplify existing societal biases through the widespread adoption of AI tutors?

Second, the lack of transparency in AI algorithms used by tutors raises concerns about accountability and fairness. The opaque nature of these algorithms could prevent educators and parents from understanding how decisions are made. It’s like a black box: we put student data in, but we don't really know what happens inside or how the AI arrives at its conclusions. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to identify and correct errors, and it raises serious questions about who is responsible when things go wrong. *ScienceDirect* published a study in 2023 highlighting the significant ethical challenges of AI in auditing, including algorithmic biases, transparency, accountability, and fairness .

Third, the high initial investment and maintenance costs of AI tutoring systems strain already limited education budgets. Funding should be prioritized for proven resources like teacher training and classroom materials before unproven AI technology. While AI tutors might lead to long-term cost savings, these savings are uncertain. A *Forbes Advisor* survey in 2024 found that teachers are worried about issues like job security, equity and safety, specifically citing concerns that AI could devalue the teaching profession and lead to job losses .

Finally, we caution against the appeal to novelty fallacy. The opponent suggests a recent study found enhanced learning, but just because something is new and shiny doesn't automatically make it better. Before we rush to embrace AI tutors, let's prioritize investments in proven methods that address the root causes of educational disparities and ensure that all students have access to high-quality, equitable learning experiences.
