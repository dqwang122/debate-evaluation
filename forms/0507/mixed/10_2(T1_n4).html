Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that when healthcare resources are demonstrably scarce, the government should intervene to ration care, thereby deciding whose life is worth saving, based on principles of maximizing overall societal well-being and ensuring equitable distribution. We define "health care" as services provided by healthcare professionals to maintain or improve a person's health; "scarce resource" as a resource for which demand exceeds supply; "rationing" as a process of allocating limited resources in a controlled manner; "government" as the governing body responsible for policies; and "worth saving" as a valuation of life based on potential for future contribution and quality of life. Our judging criteria is utilitarianism: the side that demonstrates the greatest overall good for the greatest number of people should prevail.

First, government intervention ensures a more equitable allocation of scarce healthcare resources. The Santa Clara University's Markkula Center for Applied Ethics highlights the economic rationality of healthcare rationing, noting the scarcity of resources amidst increased demand . Without government oversight, healthcare access is largely determined by socioeconomic status, exacerbating existing inequalities. As the University of Pennsylvania Carey Law School notes, resources spent on one patient mean fewer resources for others, necessitating governmental decisions for the common good . Government intervention, through transparent and standardized protocols, aims to distribute these limited resources more justly, addressing the inherent inequities of a market-based healthcare system.

Second, government rationing maximizes societal benefit by prioritizing those with the highest likelihood of recovery and future contribution. A utilitarian approach dictates that resources should be allocated to achieve the greatest overall good. For example, diverting resources from an elderly, retired population to younger members of society who are more likely to be working can increase economic productivity . Triage protocols, as utilized during crises, prioritize individuals with the highest probability of survival . While ethically challenging, this approach ensures that scarce resources are used to maximize the number of productive life years saved, thus benefiting society as a whole.

Third, government oversight is crucial in preventing discrimination and bias in healthcare rationing decisions. An article in The Regulatory Review notes that, to maintain public trust, governments should implement processes that protect against bias and other forms of institutional discrimination . Absent government intervention, implicit biases and discriminatory practices can influence resource allocation, leading to unjust outcomes. By establishing clear, objective criteria and monitoring their implementation, the government can mitigate these biases and ensure that rationing decisions are based on medical need and potential for recovery, rather than on factors such as race, socioeconomic status, or other irrelevant considerations.

In conclusion, we firmly believe that government intervention in healthcare rationing, when resources are scarce, is not only necessary but ethically imperative. By ensuring equitable allocation, maximizing societal benefit, and preventing discrimination, government rationing provides the best framework for navigating the difficult choices inherent in a world of limited healthcare resources.

Second, government rationing of healthcare inherently devalues human life by creating an arbitrary worth based on selected criteria. When healthcare is rationed based on government-defined factors, it inevitably assigns different values to individuals. This leads to ethical concerns and potential discrimination. According to *a 2020 article in PMC*, criteria such as "life-years saved" or "long-term predicted life expectancy," although not referencing age directly, will predictably disadvantage older people relative to younger people. This contradicts the fundamental principle that all human lives have intrinsic value, regardless of their potential or quality of life.

Third, government-imposed healthcare rationing can disproportionately harm vulnerable populations who lack the resources to navigate complex bureaucratic systems. Marginalized groups, such as the elderly, disabled, and low-income individuals, may face even greater barriers to accessing needed care under a rationing system. These populations often lack the resources and advocacy needed to navigate complex bureaucratic systems, making them particularly vulnerable to being denied essential care. These potential harms of rationing underscore the need for alternative solutions that improve healthcare access and efficiency without sacrificing fundamental ethical principles. Investing in preventative care and telemedicine, for instance, can alleviate resource constraints and improve access for vulnerable populations.

Finally, we want to address the framing of this debate. The opposition uses emotionally charged language, suggesting that without government intervention, healthcare will be unfairly distributed based solely on socioeconomic status. However, this presents a false choice. Instead of resorting to rationing, we should focus on improving healthcare efficiency and promoting preventative care to alleviate resource constraints. According to *research from PMC in 2020*, a transition from disease treatment to prevention can reduce the incidence of chronic disease and decrease the cost of healthcare.

For these reasons, we urge you to reject this dangerous proposal that devalues human life and opens the door to discrimination. Let's work towards a healthcare system that prioritizes efficiency, innovation, and the inherent dignity of every individual.
