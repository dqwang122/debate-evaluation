Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

In this debate, we shall argue that banning certain inappropriate books in schools is necessary to protect child welfare and promote a safe learning environment. We define "inappropriate books" as those containing explicit depictions or endorsements of sex, violence, or drug use that are deemed unsuitable for children and adolescents due to their potential to cause harm or negatively influence their development. "School" refers to educational institutions catering to children and adolescents, from elementary to high school levels. Our judging criteria is protecting child welfare. The side that best demonstrates how their position promotes the well-being and healthy development of children in schools should win the debate.

First, exposure to explicit sexual content can lead to negative attitudes and behaviors. Studies show a correlation between exposure to sexually explicit material and increased sexual aggression and risky sexual behavior in minors . Specifically, research indicates that early exposure to sexually graphic material is likely to have a negative influence and a potentially harmful impact on children . Jenni White, a former public school science teacher, stated that “Numerous studies on the use of graphic material by students indicate negative psychological effects,” including having “more casual sex partners and having sex at younger ages” . By banning books containing such content, we directly protect students from these potential harms, promoting their well-being and contributing to a healthier developmental trajectory. Therefore, limiting access to sexually explicit material is a proactive step toward safeguarding our children's future.

Second, exposure to violence in literature can contribute to desensitization and increased aggression in adolescents. Adolescents are exposed to about 30.23 acts of aggression for every hour of reading popular novels . Studies by the American Academy of Pediatrics have found that exposure to violence in media, including books, can impact kids by making them act aggressively and desensitizing them to violence . Further research indicates that adolescents with higher violence acceptance were more likely to be rejected by their classmates than adolescents with low violence acceptance . Banning books with graphic depictions of violence can help create a safer and more supportive learning environment, as violence desensitization poses a risk to children’s mental health. By reducing this exposure, we can foster a more positive and nurturing atmosphere, conducive to better learning outcomes and social interactions.

Third, parents have the right to guide their children's education and protect them from inappropriate content. While schools aim to provide diverse materials, parental rights are paramount in safeguarding their children's well-being. If books with inappropriate material are available in libraries, children or teens can be exposed to books their parents wouldn’t approve of before the parents even find out what their children are reading . In fact, on the question of book bans in schools and school libraries, 63% of parents agree that bans infringe on their rights as parents . Parental rights are already in place in most school districts: if parents do not want their child to read a text that the class has been assigned, they can ask for an alternative option, and if they do not want their child to check out specific books from the school library, they can ask for restrictions to be placed on their own children’s access . Banning inappropriate books empowers parents to ensure their children are not exposed to material that conflicts with their values or beliefs.

In this debate, we shall argue that banning certain books deemed "inappropriate" in schools is detrimental to students' intellectual freedom, critical thinking abilities, and overall educational development. We define "inappropriate books" as those materials considered unsuitable for students due to their content related to sex, violence, or drug use, recognizing that this definition is subjective and context-dependent, varying based on community standards and parental concerns.

Our judging criteria is promoting intellectual freedom and educational value. The side that best demonstrates how their approach fosters intellectual freedom, critical thinking skills, and comprehensive education for students should win the debate. This framework is appropriate because it directly addresses the core values at stake when considering book bans in schools. Banning books restricts intellectual freedom, while allowing access to a wide range of materials, even those considered controversial, promotes critical thinking and a more comprehensive understanding of the world.

First, banning books violates students' First Amendment rights and intellectual freedom. Students possess the right to access a wide range of information and ideas, and censorship directly limits their ability to form their own opinions and engage in critical thinking. The First Amendment guarantees the freedom of speech, which includes the right to receive information and ideas . According to a federal lawsuit filed in Escambia County, Florida, by PEN America, Penguin Random House, authors, parents, and students, removing books from schools violates their First Amendment right to free speech . This right is violated when decisions are based on ideological objections to the books' contents or disagreement with their messages or themes. Intellectual freedom, which includes the freedom to read, freedom of expression, and freedom to know, is a hallmark of the library and education professions . Thus, banning books directly infringes upon students' constitutionally protected rights and their intellectual development.

Second, banning books stifles critical thinking and limits exposure to diverse perspectives. By shielding students from challenging or controversial content, schools hinder their ability to develop essential critical thinking skills and understand different viewpoints. Opponents of book bans argue that restricting information and discouraging freedom of thought undermines one of the primary functions of education: teaching students how to think for themselves . This endangers tolerance, free expression, and democracy. Furthermore, challenges to books often target diverse voices and perspectives, limiting students' ability to engage with different cultures, ideas, and experiences . Banning books can negatively impact teenagers' education and critical thinking because it limits access to diverse ideas. This restriction can hinder their intellectual development and understanding of the world .

Third, banning books fails to address the root causes of issues like risky behavior or social problems. Instead of banning books, schools should focus on comprehensive sex education, anti-drug programs, and open discussions about difficult topics. Banning books is a superficial solution that fails to address the underlying issues. The top three reasons cited for requesting a book be removed are sexually explicit content, offensive language, and content "unsuited to any age group" . However, addressing these issues requires more than just removing books. Comprehensive approaches, such as those recommended, lead to more meaningful change .

In conclusion, banning books in schools undermines students' First Amendment rights, stifles critical thinking, limits exposure to diverse perspectives, and fails to address the root causes of important social issues.

This definition is not only vague but also deeply subjective. What one person deems harmful, another might recognize as a valuable opportunity for learning and personal growth.

Sheltering children from challenging content actually hinders their ability to develop crucial critical thinking skills. As highlighted in a *2024 study by American University*, controversial literature is vital in developing critical thinking skills, encouraging students to analyze complex themes, challenge societal norms, and engage in meaningful discussions. Banning books diminishes the quality of education students have access to and restricts their exposure to important perspectives. These perspectives include different cultures, lifestyles, and experiences, which are important for students to understand and respect. Exposure to diverse viewpoints, even those considered controversial, allows students to analyze, evaluate, and form their own opinions. Banning books stifles this intellectual growth, directly contradicting the goal of preparing students for informed participation in society. According to *research in 2023*, 78 percent of students are reading more when given the choice to read banned books, *showing that they are more engaged to read those types of content*.

Second, we firmly believe that teachers are trained professionals fully capable of selecting appropriate materials for their students. Trusting educators' expertise in curriculum development and age-appropriate content allows for flexible and responsive learning experiences tailored to specific student needs. The *National Coalition against Censorship* rightly points out that decisions about classroom materials should be based on professional judgment and standards, not individual preferences.

Third, banning books simply does not address the root causes of societal problems like violence and drug use. Focusing solely on censorship ignores the complex social, economic, and environmental factors that contribute to these issues, ultimately hindering the development of effective solutions. *Sonya Douglass, Professor of Education Leadership at Teachers College*, explains in *2023* that book bans diminish the quality of education students have access to and restrict their exposure to important perspectives. The opposition's strategy of banning books assumes that removing content eliminates the problem. However, censorship often backfires. Banned books often become more desirable and sought after. Removing books doesn't address the root causes of issues like violence or drug use.

In conclusion, banning books is not a solution; it's a disservice to our students. Instead, let's empower them with the tools they need to navigate the complexities of the world, fostering empathy, critical thinking, and a lifelong love of learning.
