Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote how persuasive each side is in each stage. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

Debate Structure

The full debate includes:

  • Opening: 4 min audio per side
  • Rebuttal: 4 min audio per side
  • Closing: 2 min audio per side

You'll evaluate a portion of this debate.

Your Evaluation Tasks

For each stage, you'll:

  • Rate the persuasiveness of each side's statements
  • Update your position after hearing each argument
  • Provide optional feedback
Final Comparison

In the final stage:

  • You'll see two versions of each side's closing statement
  • Rate each version independently
  • Select which version you found more persuasive
Important: Before beginning, you'll vote for the side you initially support. After each stage, you'll have the opportunity to reconsider and update your position based on the arguments presented.
Note: Throughout the evaluation, you'll encounter attention check questions to ensure data quality. Participants who demonstrate thoughtful engagement will receive compensation as agreed. If you're unable to commit to providing quality responses, you may exit the survey at any time without penalty.

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

In this debate, we shall argue that Congress should abolish the debt ceiling, a legislative limit on the amount of national debt the U.S. Treasury can incur. We define "abolish" as formally putting an end to the system, practice, or institution of the debt ceiling.

Our judging criteria is the promotion of economic stability and efficient governance. We contend that the debt ceiling, while intended to promote fiscal responsibility, has repeatedly created economic instability and political gridlock. Abolishing it will lead to a more stable economic environment where the government can reliably meet its obligations and focus on long-term economic growth.

First, the debt ceiling creates unnecessary economic instability and uncertainty. The debt ceiling has repeatedly led to political standoffs and threats of default, which negatively impact financial markets and the overall economy. For instance, the 2011 debt ceiling crisis led to a downgrade of the U.S. credit rating by Standard & Poor's, resulting in increased borrowing costs for the government and businesses . A report by the Government Accountability Office found that the 2011 debt ceiling impasse resulted in billions of dollars in increased borrowing costs . Eliminating the debt ceiling would remove this recurring source of instability and prevent future economic damage. According to a CNN article in June 2023, reforms that make future standoffs less risky would be a "positive" from a credit ratings perspective . By removing the debt ceiling, we are actively securing the U.S. credit rating and avoiding needless economic damage.

Second, the debt ceiling does not effectively control government spending. The debt ceiling is a poor tool for fiscal discipline, as it only addresses the *result* of spending decisions, not the decisions themselves. Congress still has the power to set the budget, and the debt ceiling simply creates a potential crisis when the government needs to pay its bills. The debt ceiling has not achieved its stated goal of imposing fiscal discipline on Congress. The statutory debt ceiling is a completely arbitrary value, there has never been any economic justification for any of its historical values . A New York Times article argues that the debt ceiling has not served a useful purpose in living memory . Congress can implement other fiscal restraints that provide more direct oversight and impact on fiscal policy.

Third, abolishing the debt ceiling would streamline government operations and reduce political gridlock. The debt ceiling process consumes significant time and resources, diverting attention from more pressing economic issues. It also provides opportunities for political posturing and gridlock, hindering the government's ability to address important challenges. Louise Sheiner, in her testimony to the House Budget Committee, argued that the debt ceiling distracts from real, tangible economic challenges . Freeing Congress from these manufactured crises would allow them to focus on actual economic policies that bolster the economy.

In conclusion, the debt ceiling causes unnecessary economic instability, fails to control government spending, and fosters political gridlock. Abolishing the debt ceiling will promote economic stability, improve government efficiency, and allow policymakers to focus on addressing real economic challenges. For these reasons, we strongly affirm the resolution: Congress should abolish the debt ceiling.

The Government Accountability Office recommends that Congress consider immediately replacing the current process with an approach that links debt decisions to spending and revenue decisions at the time they are made . The CRFB proposes other alternatives, such as granting the President authority to increase the debt limit if specific fiscal targets are met, or indexing the debt limit to GDP growth . These alternatives offer a more nuanced approach to fiscal management, preserving a degree of oversight and accountability without the drastic consequences of abolishment.

In conclusion, abolishing the debt ceiling poses significant risks to fiscal stability and economic well-being. It removes a necessary check on government spending and increases the likelihood of economic instability. More responsible and effective solutions exist to address the challenges of fiscal management. Therefore, we strongly advocate against abolishing the debt ceiling.
