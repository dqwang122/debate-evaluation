Debate Performance Evaluation

Evaluate Fine-grained Performance of the debater

Thanks for participating!

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are three stages (openining, rebuttal, and closing) in the debate process. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. Finally, you will rate the performance of each side in the entire debate process and provide some optional feedback.

  • 5: Excellent
  • 4: Good
  • 3: Neutral
  • 2: Bad
  • 1: Terrible

Honorable judges, esteemed colleagues, and fellow advocates for the well-being of our students, I stand before you today to argue in support of the motion: We should ban certain inappropriate books containing themes of sex, violence, and drug use in our schools.

The hallowed halls of our educational institutions are designed to be sanctuaries of learning, growth, and enlightenment. They are entrusted with the critical responsibility of shaping young minds, nurturing intellectual curiosity, and preparing students for the complexities of the world. It is precisely because of this sacred duty that we must exercise vigilance in guarding against materials that can pollute this haven of knowledge and potentially harm impressionable minds.

Our first claim rests on the principle of safeguarding the innocence and developmental well-being of our children. Exposure to graphic content depicting sex, violence, and drug use can be deeply disturbing and emotionally scarring for young people whose minds are still developing a nuanced understanding of the world. Just as we carefully regulate the types of images and videos accessible to minors in other spheres of society, we have a moral imperative to shield them from literary content that can distort their perceptions, normalize harmful behaviors, and potentially trigger traumatic responses.

Secondly, the educational environment demands a focus on age-appropriate and pedagogically sound materials. The inclusion of books containing explicit sexual content, gratuitous violence, and glorification of drug use can distract from the core curriculum and detract from the primary mission of imparting knowledge and critical thinking skills. These sensitive topics, when addressed, should be integrated into the curriculum thoughtfully and strategically, with the guidance of trained educators and in a manner sensitive to the developmental stages of the students. The presence of such materials on library shelves or assigned reading lists risks undermining the carefully curated educational experience we strive to provide.

Finally, we must acknowledge the vital role of parents and families in shaping the moral and ethical compass of their children. While we value intellectual freedom and access to a diverse range of perspectives, the ultimate responsibility for determining appropriate exposure to sensitive content lies with parents. Allowing unfettered access to books containing graphic depictions of sex, violence, and drug use within the school environment undermines parental authority and limits their ability to guide their children's moral and emotional development according to their own values and beliefs. Schools should be partners with parents in this endeavor, not facilitators of potentially harmful exposure.

Question 4: Rebuttal Stage - For

(Optional) Transcript
Honorable judges, esteemed colleagues, while our opponents raise points cloaked in the guise of diversity and inclusion, we must not be swayed by arguments that ultimately prioritize theoretical ideals over the tangible well-being of our students. The assertion that banning certain books disproportionately impacts marginalized communities crumbles under closer scrutiny. While it is true that some challenging books reflect the experiences of underrepresented groups, it is a fallacy to assume that these groups are monolithic entities with a singular perspective on sensitive issues like sex, violence, and drug use.

To suggest that restricting access to graphically violent or sexually explicit material somehow deprives these students of essential insights into their own identities is not only patronizing but also ignores the plethora of other literary works that explore these themes with nuance and sensitivity. Are we to believe that the entirety of human experience, the richness of diverse cultures and perspectives, can only be found within the pages of books saturated with potentially harmful content? To imply such is an insult to the countless authors who have tackled complex social issues in a manner that is both impactful and age-appropriate.

Furthermore, the opposition argues that exposure to mature themes is crucial for developing critical thinking and empathy. However, this argument hinges on the flawed assumption that these themes must be presented in their most graphic and explicit forms to be impactful. This is simply not true. Countless educational resources and literary works address complex social issues, promote empathy, and stimulate critical thinking without resorting to graphic depictions that may be harmful to developing minds.

The claim that banning certain books will stifle open communication between students and educators is equally tenuous. In reality, establishing clear boundaries and guidelines on sensitive content can foster a more trusting and respectful learning environment. When students understand that certain topics are best addressed within the context of structured guidance from educators and parents, it can actually encourage more open dialogue, not less.

Instead of throwing open the doors to every potentially harmful piece of literature under the banner of "diversity" or "critical thinking," we should strive to curate a learning environment that provides a balanced and age-appropriate approach to sensitive topics. The opponents' arguments are built on a foundation of misplaced priorities, prioritizing hypothetical benefits over the very real potential for harm. We must not sacrifice the innocence and well-being of our students on the altar of unrestricted access. Let us choose to protect our students, not expose them. Let us choose carefully curated knowledge, not unfettered access. Let us choose to be guardians of their well-being, not gatekeepers of potentially damaging influences.
