Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.
  • The process will take about 1 hour and a $30 Amazon gift card will be sent via email as a gift for your participation.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide:

Limited (1)

Basic claims are provided, but there is no evidence or reasoning to influence your view. Or, the evidence provided is off-topic or contradicts the claims.

Weak (2)

Some evidence or reasoning is present, but the arguments are not sufficient or poorly organized, limiting their persuasive impact.

Moderate (3)

On-topic evidence and logical reasoning are used, moderately swaying your opinion toward the given position.

Strong (4)

Strong evidence and clear logic substantially shift your perspective, though minor gaps may remain.

Compelling (5)

Compelling evidence, effective counterpoints, and powerful reasoning strongly convince you to support the given position.

Esteemed judges, respected colleagues, and fellow students, we stand before you today to advocate for a measure that lies at the very heart of our education system: the protection of young minds. The motion before us – to ban certain inappropriate books in schools – is not a question of censorship, but a question of responsibility, a responsibility we hold as educators, parents, and community members to provide a safe and nurturing environment for our children.

Let me be clear: we are not advocating for the suppression of ideas. Our libraries and classrooms should remain vibrant marketplaces of diverse thought. However, we must acknowledge that there exists a distinct line between freedom of expression and the exposure of children to materials that can be emotionally damaging, developmentally inappropriate, and potentially harmful to their well-being. This motion urges us to draw that line with wisdom and with care.

Our first point of contention lies in the undeniable fact that children are uniquely vulnerable to the influence of external stimuli. Their moral compasses are still under construction, their emotional intelligence still developing. To expose them to graphic violence, explicit sexual content, and detailed accounts of drug use before they possess the emotional maturity to process such experiences is not merely irresponsible, it is potentially detrimental to their development.

The American Academy of Pediatrics, a leading authority on child health and development, has issued clear warnings about the dangers of desensitization to violence through media exposure. This desensitization, they argue, can increase the risk of aggressive behavior, normalize violence as a solution to conflict, and diminish empathy for victims. Similarly, the Journal of Adolescent Health has published extensive research on the negative impact of early exposure to explicit sexual content, citing its potential to distort children's understanding of healthy relationships, consent, and their own developing sexuality.

Secondly, we must recognize that schools shoulder the weighty responsibility of providing a safe and age-appropriate learning environment for all students. Parents entrust their children to our care during crucial formative years, with the implicit expectation that we will safeguard their well-being, both physical and emotional. This principle is enshrined in the very fabric of our educational system, supported by organizations like the National PTA, which consistently emphasizes the importance of a secure and supportive school environment.

Furthermore, this responsibility is not merely a moral imperative; it is a legal one. The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act, better known as FERPA, clearly outlines the legal obligation of schools to protect students from harm, including exposure to harmful materials. Banning inappropriate books within the school environment falls squarely within this legal and ethical obligation.

Finally, we must address the undeniable fact that exposure to inappropriate content can be a deeply traumatic experience for some students, leading to significant emotional distress and hindering their ability to thrive academically. The National Child Traumatic Stress Network has documented the profound impact that exposure to certain themes – violence, abuse, addiction – can have on children, particularly those who have experienced trauma in their own lives.

For these students, encountering such material in the supposed safety of a classroom can trigger feelings of anxiety, fear, and even re-traumatization. The American Psychological Association has established a clear link between emotional distress and academic performance, demonstrating how trauma and anxiety can negatively impact concentration, memory, and overall academic success.

In conclusion, banning certain inappropriate books in schools is not about limiting access to knowledge, but about responsible stewardship of young minds. It is about ensuring that the information our children encounter is delivered at an appropriate stage in their development, within a safe and supportive environment. This motion is about fulfilling our moral and legal obligation to protect our children from harm, to nurture their emotional well-being, and to empower them to reach their full potential. We urge you to vote in favor of this motion, for the sake of our children and the future they are building.

Honorable judges, esteemed colleagues, and fellow members of the audience, today we grapple with a proposition that, while perhaps well-intentioned, ultimately threatens the very foundations of intellectual freedom and critical thinking that we hold dear: the proposition that we should allow the banning of certain books in schools. We stand firmly in opposition to this motion. The path to a more informed, empathetic, and critically engaged citizenry lies not in shielding our students from challenging topics, but in equipping them with the tools to navigate complex issues through exposure to diverse perspectives, even those deemed uncomfortable or controversial. To argue otherwise is to embrace censorship, a dangerous tool that stifles intellectual growth and undermines the very essence of a free and open society.

Firstly, the inherent subjectivity of what constitutes "inappropriate" renders any attempt at banning specific books a slippery slope with dangerous implications. What one individual or group deems harmful, another may consider essential to understanding history, societal dynamics, or the complexities of the human experience. Take, for example, the often-cited argument for banning books depicting drug use. While some may perceive these narratives as glorifying substance abuse, others, and indeed many experts in the field, argue that these very narratives offer invaluable insights into the realities of addiction, its social impact, and the struggles of those affected. By censoring these voices, we risk silencing crucial conversations and hindering our collective ability to address complex societal issues.

Furthermore, denying students access to literary works that explore challenging or uncomfortable themes robs them of the opportunity to develop crucial critical thinking skills. It is within the pages of books that we encounter different perspectives, grapple with ethical dilemmas, and confront the uncomfortable realities of the human condition. Shielding students from these experiences under the guise of protection ultimately leaves them ill-equipped to navigate the complexities of the world beyond the classroom. Would we shield students from historical accounts of war and violence, denying them the opportunity to grapple with the consequences of conflict and the importance of peacebuilding? Would we censor literary classics that expose social injustices, fearing that exposure to such realities might prove unsettling? To do so would be to fail in our duty to foster critical thinkers capable of engaging meaningfully with the world around them.

In closing, we urge you to consider the dangerous precedent set by endorsing censorship, even under the well-intentioned guise of protection. By embracing open access to diverse perspectives, even those deemed challenging, we empower our students to become informed, empathetic, and critical thinkers, capable of engaging responsibly and thoughtfully with the complexities of the world around them. To argue otherwise is not to protect but to infantilize; it is not to guide but to limit; it is not to empower but to stifle the very minds we strive to educate. Let us equip our students not with blinders but with lenses, diverse and multifaceted, through which they can critically examine the world and contribute meaningfully to its betterment.

My esteemed colleagues make passionate arguments for the freedom of ideas, and indeed, we share their commitment to intellectual growth and critical thinking. However, they stumble into a false dichotomy: a choice between unfettered access and responsible curation. Our position is not about eliminating challenging material; it's about ensuring that material is presented within an age-appropriate context where it can be processed effectively, not become a source of harm.

Let's address the slippery slope fallacy. Just because we advocate for thoughtful restrictions doesn't mean we're advocating for emptying libraries. Schools already curate their materials. We don't hand kindergartners physics textbooks. Why? Because developmental readiness matters. Exposure to excessively violent or sexually explicit content at a young age, when not contextualized within a guided learning environment, can be deeply disturbing and warp a child's understanding of healthy relationships and behaviors.

The American Academy of Pediatrics, in its guidelines on media violence, stresses that children lack the abstract reasoning skills of adults. They are more likely to imitate what they see and less likely to understand long-term consequences. This doesn't mean hiding reality, but it does mean presenting it in stages, allowing their critical thinking to develop alongside their emotional maturity.

The argument that banning books about drug use deprives students of valuable insights is similarly flawed. It presents a false choice: either allow unfiltered access to potentially triggering content or remain utterly ignorant. The reality is that age-appropriate discussions about addiction, its causes, and consequences can occur without resorting to graphic depictions that may be harmful or triggering, particularly for students with personal or family histories of substance abuse.

Moreover, the claim that exposure to challenging content is always beneficial for critical thinking ignores the reality of trauma. For a student who has experienced sexual assault, for example, reading a graphic depiction of rape in a novel, no matter how critically acclaimed, may not be a catalyst for intellectual growth. It could trigger severe distress, anxiety, and even PTSD symptoms, hindering their learning and overall well-being.

The National Child Traumatic Stress Network emphasizes that trauma-informed schools are not about avoiding difficult topics but about approaching them with sensitivity, providing appropriate support systems, and recognizing that not all students are equally equipped to process certain types of content at a given age. This is not censorship; it's responsible education.

The heart of the matter is that schools have a fundamental duty of care. Parents entrust us with their children's well-being during school hours, and this includes protecting them from foreseeable harm, whether physical or emotional. Just as we have a duty to prevent bullying in the playground, we have a duty to prevent psychological distress caused by developmentally inappropriate materials within the classroom.

Banning certain books isn't about stifling intellectual curiosity; it's about nurturing it responsibly. It's about recognizing that children are not miniature adults, and their exposure to potentially harmful content needs to be carefully considered within the context of their age, emotional maturity, and overall well-being. We can encourage critical thinking, empathy, and engagement with complex issues without resorting to materials that can traumatize, desensitize, or distort their understanding of the world.

To equate the two is not only intellectually dishonest but deeply disingenuous to the power of literature to foster critical engagement with complex issues.

Moreover, their argument assumes a dangerous level of uniformity among students, painting them as fragile vessels prone to immediate and irreversible damage upon encountering a challenging text. This is an insult to the intelligence and resilience of young minds. To suggest that a student who has experienced sexual assault, for example, would be automatically and irrevocably harmed by reading a fictional account of such an experience is to deny the power of literature to offer solace, understanding, and even empowerment. Yes, some students may require additional support, but to deny all students access to potentially challenging works based on the hypothetical trauma of a few is not responsible education; it is intellectual cowardice.

The opposition claims that schools have a duty of care to protect students from harm. We agree. But true protection lies in empowering students to navigate a complex world, not in shielding them from it. It lies in providing context, fostering critical thinking, and encouraging open dialogue, not in arbitrarily deeming certain topics off-limits.

The opposition's argument ultimately rests on fear: fear of discomfort, fear of controversy, fear of the unknown. But fear is a poor foundation upon which to build an education system. If we truly wish to cultivate informed, empathetic, and critically engaged citizens, we must trust in the power of knowledge, even when it is unsettling, even when it challenges our preconceived notions, and yes, even when it makes us uncomfortable.
