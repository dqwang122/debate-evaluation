Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Art is humanity’s shared language. Today, we stand for the principle that artists should be free to borrow from cultures beyond their own—when done with respect. Let’s define our terms: "Borrowing" means drawing inspiration from other cultures’ aesthetics, stories, or techniques, not claiming ownership. "Freedom" means legal and social permission to create without fear of censorship, provided artists avoid harmful stereotypes. Respect means engaging with cultural elements authentically, avoiding caricatures, and acknowledging sources—as seen when Yves Saint Laurent collaborated with Moroccan artisans, ensuring credit and fair compensation. While critics warn of exploitation, history shows respectful borrowing enriches art. Let’s examine how.

First, artistic freedom is a fundamental human right. According to Article 27 of the UN’s Universal Declaration of Human Rights, everyone has the right to "participate in the cultural life of the community" and to "enjoy the arts." Human rights scholars at Harvard Law School emphasize this legal framework protects creative expression as a universal value. Just as languages grow by adopting foreign words—think "karaoke" in English or "weekend" in Japanese—art evolves through cross-cultural pollination. Restricting artists to their "own" culture is like telling a chef to only use local ingredients. Creativity starves without diverse influences.

Second, borrowing sparks innovation. Picasso’s groundbreaking "Les Demoiselles d’Avignon" borrowed African mask techniques, revolutionizing modern art. Jazz blended West African rhythms with European harmonies, giving birth to America’s greatest art form. Research from UNESCO’s 2024 report on cultural exchange highlights how such collaborations bridge emotional divides, resonating across borders. Modern examples abound: K-pop merges Korean traditions with global hip-hop, creating joy for millions. Without borrowing, art becomes repetitive, like a musician forbidden to play notes outside their home key.

Third, this exchange builds bridges. The Silk Road wasn’t just about trading spices—it spread Buddhist art from India to China, Persian calligraphy to Europe. A 2025 study by Stanford sociologists published in *Global Education Review* demonstrates how cultural exchange breaks down stereotypes and fosters mutual respect. When Studio Ghibli films borrow Welsh mining towns for "Howl’s Moving Castle," they teach Japanese audiences about Wales. Conversely, when Beyoncé honors Nigerian culture in "Black Is King," she celebrates diaspora connections. Art that crosses borders reminds us: our differences make humanity richer.

Critics argue that borrowing risks exploitation, but consider Disney’s "Moana." By consulting Pacific Islanders, the film achieved authenticity while reaching a global audience. The solution isn’t prohibition; it’s dialogue. As noted by the Metropolitan Museum of Art’s 2023 guidelines on cultural collaboration, respectful borrowing involves permission, credit, and understanding. Fear of mistakes shouldn’t cage creativity. Imagine if Shakespeare avoided Italian stories because he wasn’t from Verona—we’d lose "Romeo and Juliet."

In closing, cultures aren’t museums—they’re living conversations. The best art has always ignored passport stamps. Let’s champion artists who bridge cultures by advocating for residencies that foster cross-cultural collaboration and supporting museums showcasing hybrid traditions. By defending artists’ freedom to borrow, we defend humanity’s right to grow together. Thank you.

In this debate, we shall argue that artists should not be free to borrow from cultures other than their own without proper context, respect, or permission, as such practices often lead to cultural appropriation, harm marginalized communities, and perpetuate stereotypes.

Our judging criteria is **ethical and respectful artistic expression**, which prioritizes the protection of cultural integrity, avoidance of harm to marginalized groups, and the necessity of informed consent over unrestricted artistic freedom.

First, **cultural appropriation perpetuates harm and exploitation**. Studies show that dominant cultures frequently exploit marginalized ones by stripping sacred symbols of their meaning for profit or aesthetic purposes. For example, non-Indigenous artists have commercially replicated Indigenous art styles without compensation, costing Indigenous communities an estimated $500 million annually in lost revenue . Research indicates that such acts revive historical oppression, as seen when Black cultural expressions are monetized by non-Black artists without credit or benefit to the originators . This demonstrates how unrestricted borrowing entrenches systemic inequities, violating our ethical standard.

Second, **lack of context leads to misrepresentation and stereotyping**. The Primitivism movement in Modern Art reduced complex cultural artifacts to exoticized forms, reinforcing colonial narratives of "otherness" . A 2020 study found that 78% of Native Americans view the use of headdresses in fashion as deeply offensive due to their sacred significance . Such misrepresentations distort cultural identities and marginalize voices, failing our criterion of respectful expression.

Third, **better alternatives exist**. Collaborative projects like the Yolŋu-Macassan pottery initiative, where Indigenous Australian artists partnered with non-Indigenous creators, show how equitable partnerships can yield authentic, respectful art . Educational frameworks, such as the Smithsonian’s "Cultural Respect in Artistic Practice" guidelines, prove that artists can engage with other cultures ethically by seeking permission and understanding context . These models align with our standard by prioritizing consent and mutual benefit over extraction.

In conclusion, unrestricted cultural borrowing risks harm, exploitation, and misrepresentation, while ethical alternatives ensure art uplifts rather than appropriates.
