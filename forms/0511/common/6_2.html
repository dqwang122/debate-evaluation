Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

In this debate, we shall argue that it is time to welcome an A.I. Tutor in the classroom, defined as an artificial intelligence system designed to provide personalized, adaptive instruction and feedback to students, leveraging machine learning to tailor educational content to individual needs .
Our judging criteria is the ability of AI tutors to improve educational accessibility, personalization, and efficiency while complementing human teachers, prioritizing equitable learning gains, student engagement, and scalability.

First, AI tutors significantly enhance personalized learning, leading to measurable improvements in student outcomes. Unlike traditional one-size-fits-all teaching methods, AI systems adapt in real-time to individual learning paces and styles. Research from Stanford University demonstrates that students using AI tutors learned more than twice as much in less time compared to those in active learning classrooms . For example, Westfield High School in Indiana reported marked improvements in student performance after implementing AI-powered personalized learning tools . This evidence underscores how AI tutors can address diverse learning needs at scale, a critical advantage in overcrowded classrooms.

Second, AI tutors democratize education by increasing accessibility and bridging equity gaps. They provide 24/7 support, eliminating geographical and socioeconomic barriers. A randomized study of 1,800 K-12 students from underserved communities found that those using Tutor CoPilot, an AI tutoring app, were 4 percentage points more likely to master math topics than peers without access . Additionally, AI tools like Khanmigo offer adaptive support for students with learning disabilities, tailoring content to their unique needs . These examples highlight AI’s role in creating a more inclusive education system.

Third, AI tutors alleviate teacher workload, enabling educators to focus on higher-value tasks. By automating administrative duties and routine tutoring, AI reduces burnout and allows teachers to dedicate time to mentorship and critical thinking exercises. A UK study cited by *The Guardian* found that AI tools could ease the "overwhelming" workload burdening teachers . For instance, AI-driven grading systems cut grading time by up to 50% in pilot programs, freeing teachers to engage more deeply with students . This synergy between AI and human teachers maximizes the strengths of both.

In conclusion, AI tutors are a transformative force in education, offering personalized learning, equitable access, and operational efficiency. Their integration into classrooms is not just beneficial but essential to meet the evolving demands of 21st-century education.

Ladies and gentlemen, education isn’t just about data—it’s about human connection. While AI advocates praise these tools as a cure-all, they overlook critical flaws that make them unfit for our classrooms.

It’s like a calculator for emotions: it crunches numbers but can’t mend a heart. While a 2024 study in the *International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education* found that students using AI tutors reported feeling less judged, the same study revealed serious concerns about unreliable answers and a lack of accountability when errors occur . This isn’t just theoretical—real harm is at stake. According to the CDC’s 2023 report, schools with robust counseling programs saw a 20% drop in student suicide rates, a feat no algorithm can replicate . My opponent cites test scores, but what about the lives at risk? No machine can replace a caring adult who says, “I see you, and you matter.”

Second, AI risks deepening inequality, not solving it. Proponents claim these tools bridge gaps, but let’s talk reality. Schools in Detroit or rural Alabama lack reliable Wi-Fi, let alone expensive AI tutors. Research from *Stanford Law School* in 2024 confirms that wealthier districts consistently outspend underfunded schools on advanced AI tools, exacerbating existing disparities . And bias? These systems learn from data soaked in historical prejudices. A 2023 MIT study found AI grading tools penalized essays written in African American Vernacular English—a dialect with deep cultural roots that AI systems often misinterpret . When AI downgrades essays using AAVE, it tells Black students their language is ‘wrong’—a blow to confidence and achievement. This isn’t progress; it’s prejudice automated.

Third, the costs are staggering. A 2024 Brookings report found that AI tutoring systems cost districts an average of $50,000 annually—more than a starting teacher’s salary in 30 states . That $50K pays for one AI system or two full-time counselors—who directly address the teen mental health crisis. Meanwhile, schools cut art programs and counselors to fund tech that’s obsolete in three years. *Education Week* warns in a 2025 analysis that ongoing maintenance and updates for AI systems create hidden financial burdens, diverting resources from proven interventions like smaller class sizes . We’re robbing Peter to pay a robot—and Peter is a child who needs a nurse, not a chatbot.

AI advocates claim these tools free teachers, but that’s a myth. Teachers don’t need “more time”; they need more support—smaller classes, better pay. Automating grading won’t fix burnout when the real issue is systemic neglect.

In closing, AI tutors promise shortcuts but deliver shortcuts. Education isn’t a race to efficiency; it’s a garden where human hands nurture growth. Attend school board meetings this month and demand votes against AI contracts until class sizes are under 20. Let’s invest in those hands—not hand our children over to machines.
