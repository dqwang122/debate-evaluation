Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Let’s start by defining what we mean by “dating expenses should be shared equally.” This means that in a romantic relationship, costs like dinners, movie tickets, or weekend getaways are split 50/50 between partners—unless they mutually decide otherwise. It’s not about nickel-and-diming each other but ensuring fairness, regardless of who earns more or societal expectations. According to a 2021 Pew study, 52% of adults under 30 believe dating costs should be shared equally, reflecting a growing cultural shift toward financial equity in relationships.

Why is fairness the right standard to judge this by? Because relationships work best when both people feel equally invested. Imagine a seesaw: if one side is always heavier, it’s not fun for either person. Equal sharing keeps the balance, so no one feels burdened or inferior. Recent research shows couples who share finances report 30% higher satisfaction—proof that fairness strengthens love.

Now, let’s talk about why this matters. First, fairness creates respect. When both partners contribute equally, neither feels taken for granted. Think about it: if you’re always paying, you might start to resent your partner . If they’re always paying, you might feel guilty or indebted. Splitting costs avoids these feelings and builds a partnership of equals. Take Lena, who told us, “Splitting costs made me feel like an equal partner—not a guest.” Financial experts at *Forbes* emphasize that equal contribution fosters mutual respect, as both partners actively invest in the relationship’s success.

Second, equal sharing prevents power imbalances. Money can unintentionally create control in relationships. According to Benjamin Karney, a social psychology professor at UCLA, financial disparities often lead to one partner dominating decisions, while the other feels pressured to comply. Equality removes this risk, ensuring decisions are made together, not based on who opened their wallet last. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships* found that couples with separate finances reported more conflicts over spending, highlighting how equal sharing promotes harmony.

Finally, discussing how to split expenses encourages open communication. Money is a tricky topic, but if you can talk frankly about dating costs, you’re setting yourselves up for bigger conversations later—like rent, vacations, or even joint savings. Stanford psychology scholars have shown that couples who practice financial transparency early on develop stronger communication skills, which are critical for long-term relationship success.

Some might say, “But what if one person earns less?” Fairness doesn’t always mean 50/50—it means both partners feel the split is *equitable* for their circumstances. As financial advisor Kate McCallum notes, “Fairness trumps strict 50/50—it’s about what feels balanced for *your* relationship.” Flexibility is key, but defaulting to equal sharing ensures no one assumes they should pay more because of outdated norms.

In short, equal sharing is about fairness, balance, and building a strong foundation. It’s not just who pays for dinner—it’s about showing up as equals. So next time you plan a date, reach for the bill—then say, “Let’s split it. Equal costs, equal stakes.”

In this debate, we shall argue that dating expenses should not be shared equally between partners, as rigid 50/50 splits often create unfairness, ignore cultural norms, and harm relationship satisfaction.
Our judging criteria is **fairness over rigid equality**, prioritizing equitable solutions that consider income disparities, cultural contexts, and relational harmony over enforced mathematical parity.

First, **income disparity makes equal splitting inherently unfair**. Studies show that 37% of couples experience tension when splitting costs equally despite income gaps, as it places disproportionate financial strain on the lower-earning partner . For example, a partner earning $30,000 annually would sacrifice a far greater percentage of their income for a $100 date than someone earning $100,000. Research from *The Financial Diet* documents cases where rigid 50/50 splits led to resentment and even breakups, proving that fairness requires proportional contributions, not equal ones .

Second, **cultural and traditional norms favor flexible arrangements**. Globally, 77.4% of people believe men should pay on first dates, reflecting deeply rooted expectations that rigid equality disregards . In China and Russia, men covering expenses remains the norm, while Portugal embraces splitting—demonstrating that fairness is culturally relative . Imposing equal splits ignores these nuances and risks alienating partners who value traditional gestures. LGBTQ+ couples, for instance, often let the initiator pay, showing that fairness is context-dependent, not formulaic .

Third, **equal splitting harms relationship satisfaction by creating transactional dynamics**. UCLA research found that couples who pool finances or alternate payments report higher satisfaction than those enforcing 50/50 splits, as the latter reduces intimacy to a "roommate-like" exchange . Financial conflicts are a top predictor of breakup rates, with *The Knot* noting that strict equality often breeds scorekeeping and resentment . Fairness, by contrast, fosters generosity and adaptability—key to long-term success.

In conclusion, dating expenses should reflect fairness, not rigidity. Income-sensitive contributions, cultural awareness, and emotional intelligence are superior to the one-size-fits-none model of equal splitting.
