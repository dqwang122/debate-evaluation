Welcome to the Debate Evaluation!

You'll be evaluating a 20-minute debate where two sides discuss a topic. Your opinion matters - you'll vote both before and after listening the debate. We will use your feedback to improve the debate quality.

What to Expect:

  • Listen to a debate divided into Opening (4 min per side), Rebuttal (4 min per side), and Closing stages (2 min per side).
  • Share your initial opinion before listening.
  • Evaluate how each side performs during the debate.
  • Cast your final vote after listening.

How to Vote:

  • Support "For" Side - If you agree with the motion
  • Support "Against" Side - If you disagree with the motion
  • Choose "Tie" - If you do not have a preference

Rating Guide for Persuasiveness:

1
Poor

Limited evidence with poor organization or fundamental logic flaws. Disengage with no audience awareness.

2
Weak

Reasonable statements with at least one noticeable weakness.

3
Moderate

Reasonable statements, which provide on-topic evidence with logical flow and balanced emotional tone showing basic audience awareness

4
Strong

Reasonable statements with at least one impressive shining points.

5
Compelling

Powerful evidence with effective counterpoints and create deep connection with audience.

Purpose of this Study

Competitive debate involves critical thinking, clear and persuasive expression, conflict resolving, and the organization of reliable evidence. The purpose of the study is to design an LLM-based agent for competitive debate with humans, which models the behaviors of the opponent and the audience for more persuasive and compelling statements.

Summary

You'll review a recorded 20-minute debate between two debaters (humans or AI debaters) and evaluate the performance of each side. You will be asked to complete a questionnaire, which includes your vote before and after the debate process on a predetermined topic. There are also several fine-grained aspects and you will need to rate the performance between 1 to 5 for each side. The evaluation stage will take around 5 minutes and the demographic questions at the end of the survey will take at most 5 minutes, and the total time is at most 30 minutes.

This human evaluation would provide valuable insights into the current state of AI debating capabilities, highlight areas for improvement, and help identify ways to enhance AI systems to more closely emulate human-level performance in complex argumentative tasks.

Let’s start by defining our terms clearly. "Inappropriate books" means those containing explicit depictions of sex, violence, or drug use—material that would earn an R-rating in films, like graphic descriptions of abuse in *The Bluest Eye*. This isn’t about banning ideas; it’s about protecting children’s developmental readiness, just as schools already do with internet filters under laws like CIPA.

Our judging standard is straightforward: Does banning these books shield children’s mental health, fulfill schools’ legal duty to protect students, and respect parents’ rights? The answer is a resounding yes on all counts.

First, **mental health**. Children’s brains are still developing, and exposing them to graphic content can cause lasting harm. Research from the *American Academy of Pediatrics* in 2022 demonstrates that exposure to violence in media, including books, increases aggressive behavior and desensitizes children to real-world violence. A 2022 study by the *American Psychological Association* found that 60% of middle-schoolers exposed to violent literature exhibited heightened aggression. But the stakes are even higher than behavioral changes. Consider the 2021 case in Ohio, where a 10-year-old mimicked self-harm after reading a book with graphic suicide scenes. This isn’t hypothetical—it’s a documented reality. Books like *The Bluest Eye*, which depict trauma without age-appropriate context, risk warping emotional and psychological growth. Schools shouldn’t gamble with students’ well-being by leaving such material on shelves.

Second, **legal responsibility**. Schools act *in loco parentis*—in place of parents—during the day. Just as they lock doors to keep out physical threats, they must filter out harmful mental ones. Courts consistently uphold schools’ authority to set content standards, from dress codes to curriculum. If a teacher can’t show an R-rated movie in class, why allow books with equally explicit material? In Texas in 2023, parents successfully petitioned to remove *Gender Queer* for its explicit imagery, proving that communities expect schools to uphold these standards. This isn’t about moral judgments; it’s about developmental appropriateness. We teach *Romeo and Juliet* in high school, not middle school, because of its mature themes. The same logic applies here.

Finally, **parental rights**. Parents, not schools, should decide when their children encounter mature topics. According to a 2023 study in *BMC Pediatrics*, teens with unrestricted access to mature-rated media were three times more likely to experiment with drugs or early sexual activity. Imagine a parent discovering their 12-year-old checked out a book with graphic sex scenes from the school library—without any prior notification. That’s not just inappropriate; it’s a betrayal of trust. Parents must demand that schools notify them *before* stocking mature content, just as they do for sex education curricula.

Some will cry “censorship!” But this isn’t about silencing voices. It’s about age-appropriate curation—like how we rate movies or video games. We don’t let children buy cigarettes; why give them access to books glorifying drug use? This isn’t banning *ideas*—Shakespeare’s violence stays, but with teacher guidance. We’re filtering *explicitness*.

In closing, banning inappropriate books isn’t about fear. It’s about responsibility: to children’s minds, to the law, and to parents. Let’s put safety first.

Our judging criteria is whether banning books in schools enhances or hinders students' intellectual growth, critical thinking, and exposure to diverse perspectives. The negative side contends that such bans limit educational opportunities and violate students' rights to access information.

This legal precedent underscores the importance of protecting students' access to diverse viewpoints. Intellectual freedom is foundational to education, as it prevents indoctrination and fosters independent thinking . By banning books, schools risk creating an environment where only "approved" ideas are tolerated, stifling academic discourse and critical inquiry.

Second, exposure to challenging themes fosters critical thinking and empathy. Books like *To Kill a Mockingbird* and *The Catcher in the Rye*—often targeted for bans—explore complex issues such as racism and adolescent struggles, providing invaluable lessons in empathy and social awareness . Research from Harvard University demonstrates that engaging with difficult topics in classrooms strengthens students' problem-solving skills and emotional intelligence . Banning these works deprives students of opportunities to grapple with real-world issues in a guided, educational setting.

Third, book bans disproportionately target marginalized voices and create a culture of censorship. For example, LGBTQ+ narratives like *All Boys Aren’t Blue* are frequently challenged, denying representation to students who may see themselves reflected in these stories . A 2023 report by the American Library Association revealed a 65% increase in censorship attempts, with a significant focus on books by authors of color and LGBTQ+ individuals . Such bans not only silence diverse perspectives but also send a harmful message that certain identities or experiences are "inappropriate" for discussion.

In conclusion, banning books in schools undermines intellectual freedom, hampers critical thinking, and marginalizes already vulnerable voices. Instead of censorship, schools should adopt alternatives like parental opt-out policies or structured discussions to address concerns while preserving educational integrity.

But unchecked access? That’s gambling with kids’ minds. According to *Stanford psychology scholars*, controlled studies show that structured exposure to challenging topics yields better outcomes than unfiltered access. This approach respects both parental rights and educational goals.

Banning inappropriate books isn’t censorship—it’s care. Let’s put safety first by using clear, developmentally appropriate standards. We can protect children while still fostering critical thinking and inclusivity. The stakes are too high to compromise.
